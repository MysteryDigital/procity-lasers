## 2. How laser cleaning works — the physics

### The core process ✅ Verified

Laser cleaning is a **photothermal and photomechanical process**. A pulsed laser beam is directed at a contaminated surface. The contaminant (paint, soot, grime, graffiti pigment) absorbs the laser energy and is ejected from the surface — evaporating or fragmenting away — while the underlying substrate absorbs far less energy and is left intact.

The key mechanism is **differential absorption**: the soiling layer absorbs the laser energy at a much higher rate than the substrate beneath. This selectivity is what allows laser cleaning to remove a graffiti tag from a sandstone wall without damaging the stone.

### The equipment ✅ Verified

Standard laser cleaning equipment uses **Q-switched Nd:YAG lasers** (neodymium-doped yttrium aluminium garnet) operating at:
- Wavelength: **1.064 µm** (near-infrared)
- Pulse duration: **5–10 nanoseconds**

Pulsed operation is preferred over continuous-wave (CW) lasers because short pulses produce a much lower heat load on the substrate — the energy is deposited so quickly that heat does not spread significantly into the material beneath. This is sometimes described (informally) as "cold ablation", though the process is not literally cold.

### The safety threshold ✅ Verified

Above approximately **1.5 J/cm² fluence** (energy density per unit area), plasma forms above the surface and shock waves develop. At this point the process becomes less selective and can damage the substrate. Skilled operators calibrate fluence carefully to stay below this threshold for the material being cleaned.

Source: [buildingconservation.com — Laser Cleaning](https://www.buildingconservation.com/articles/laser/laser.htm), [RP Photonics Encyclopedia — Laser Cleaning](https://www.rp-photonics.com/laser_cleaning.html)

### Femtosecond lasers (advanced) ✅ Verified

More advanced (and expensive) femtosecond laser systems exist. A 2023 peer-reviewed study in *Applied Physics A* (Springer) demonstrated complete removal of red, green, and blue spray-paint graffiti from granite at 1.0 J/cm² fluence over 10 scans. Post-cleaning Raman spectroscopy detected only the original stone minerals — zero residual pigment. Most UK contractors currently use nanosecond (Nd:YAG) systems rather than femtosecond systems, which are significantly more expensive.

Source: [Springer Applied Physics A, 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00339-023-06455-x)

## 3. Key benefits vs traditional methods

### No consumables ✅ Verified

Laser cleaning requires no sand, chemical solvents, brushes, or abrasive media. Once the equipment is purchased, there is no ongoing consumable cost per job.

Source: [RP Photonics Encyclopedia](https://www.rp-photonics.com/laser_cleaning.html)

### Substrate selectivity ✅ Verified

The selective ablation mechanism greatly reduces the risk of accidentally removing substrate material alongside the contaminant. This is a direct and significant advantage over abrasive methods, which are inherently erosive and offer no equivalent selectivity.

**Important hedge:** laser cleaning *reduces* substrate damage risk — it does not eliminate it. Under mis-set parameters (wrong fluence, wrong pulse settings), laser cleaning can still damage the substrate. Operator skill and equipment calibration matter.

Source: [RP Photonics Encyclopedia](https://www.rp-photonics.com/laser_cleaning.html)

### Abrasive blasting is damaging to masonry ✅ Verified (medium confidence)

Air-abrasive mechanical systems (sandblasting, grit blasting) cause surface damage to brick and stonework even when operated at low pressure. They are unlikely to be suitable as a standalone method for removing graffiti from masonry.

Source: [buildingconservation.com — Graffiti Removal](https://www.buildingconservation.com/articles/graffiti/graffiti.htm)

### Other commonly stated benefits ⚠️ Unverified

This covers less than half of London's boroughs and excludes Transport for London and other operators — meaning total London-wide spend is substantially higher. Source: [SW Londoner, December 2024](https://www.swlondoner.co.uk/news/03122024-cleaning-graffiti-costs-councils-tens-of-millions-as-reports-of-illegal-artwork-rise)

#### Why laser suits masonry graffiti

Spray-paint graffiti on brick, stone, and concrete is a core use case for laser cleaning. The substrate is porous and easily damaged by abrasives, making laser the preferred approach for heritage and conservation-grade surfaces.

### Heritage buildings and listed structures

Heritage conservation is a major application. The key pitch to heritage buyers is substrate preservation: listed stone, historic brick, carved details, and decorative stonework cannot be treated with abrasive methods without risk of irreversible damage.

## 6. Regulatory and accreditation context

🔲 **Research gap — this entire section needs primary research before writing.**

The following topics are relevant to Pro City's buyers but no verified claims were confirmed during research. Do not write about these without checking primary sources:

- **Listed Building Consent:** when laser cleaning of a listed building requires consent, and what Historic England's position is on laser methods specifically.
- **COSHH (Control of Substances Hazardous to Health):** laser ablation generates a fume/vapour stream (particulates, volatile organic compounds from paint binders, metal oxides from corrosion layers). This is a health and safety concern that requires a COSHH assessment. The HSE website is the authoritative source.
- **Laser safety legislation:** high-powered lasers are Class 4 devices. Operators must comply with BS EN 60825-1 (laser safety standard) and may require a Laser Safety Officer. Check [HSE laser guidance](https://www.hse.gov.uk/radiation/nonionising/lasers.htm).
- **Accreditation schemes:** no specific laser cleaning accreditation body was identified. Contractors may hold CSCS cards, SSSTS/SMSTS, or be members of the Federation of Master Builders — but no laser-specific credential was confirmed.

## 8. Writing guidance for this audience

**Who reads these articles:** procurement officers, estates managers, conservation officers, loss adjusters, and site managers. They are not interested in how cool lasers are — they want to know: *will this solve my problem, does it comply with our obligations, and can I trust this company?*

**What works:**

- **Real project evidence** — before/after photography is the site's primary asset. Reference it in articles. "We cleaned [type of surface] at [type of location]" is more persuasive than any claim about laser physics.
- **Regulatory reassurance** — heritage and council buyers are risk-averse. Articles that help them understand their obligations (Listed Building Consent, COSHH, contractor insurance) position Pro City as knowledgeable and trustworthy.
- **Comparisons** — "why not just sandblast it?" is a question every buyer asks. Articles that explain the limits of abrasive methods (substrate damage, particularly on heritage masonry) are directly useful.
- **Cost framing** — buyers think in whole-life cost. An article that explains how laser cleaning reduces repeat treatments, avoids substrate repair costs, and eliminates waste disposal can shift the conversation away from day-rate comparisons.

**What to avoid:**

- Unsourced superlatives ("the safest", "the most effective", "zero environmental impact") — these were mostly refuted in research and read as marketing copy rather than authoritative content.
- Global market statistics from paywalled research reports — the numbers did not survive verification and look out of place in credible B2B content.
- Claims about Listed Building Consent or COSHH compliance without checking primary HSE/Historic England sources first.
