Hot-dip galvanising is one of the most durable corrosion protection systems available for structural steel. It forms a metallurgical bond between zinc and steel that survives decades of outdoor exposure. But that bond only forms on a chemically clean, oxide-free surface. If steel arrives at the galvanising bath carrying rust, mill scale, paint, oil, or organic residue, the zinc will not wet the surface properly, leaving bare patches that corrode at the rate of unprotected steel.

Surface preparation is therefore not an optional quality step — it is the foundation of a compliant, long-lasting coating.

## What does the galvanising process require of the steel surface?

The standard galvanising sequence is: degrease → pickle → flux → dip → cool. Each stage depends on the one before it.

Molten zinc at around 450°C forms a metallurgical bond with steel only when the iron surface is completely free of oxides and contaminants. A thin film of oil prevents the flux from activating the surface. Residual mill scale — the hard iron oxide layer produced by hot rolling — blocks zinc adhesion in the same way. Paint and organic coatings burn off in the zinc bath, but their residues contaminate the melt and leave bare areas, known as skips, in the finished coating.

Skips are not a cosmetic problem. In service, those areas corrode without any of the sacrificial protection that makes galvanising cost-effective. A coating failure traced back to inadequate preparation is a liability issue, not a maintenance one.

## How is steel conventionally prepared for galvanising?

### Degreasing

Oils, greases, and cutting fluids are removed first, either in a hot caustic solution or with solvent-based products. This stage must be completed before pickling because acid will not penetrate an oily surface — any contamination missed here carries through to the finished coating.

### Acid pickling

Pickling removes rust and mill scale. The most common process in the UK uses hydrochloric acid at around 10–15% concentration, which dissolves iron oxides without attacking the base metal at normal temperatures. Sulphuric acid is an alternative used at elevated temperatures.

Pickling works, but it generates waste classified as hazardous: spent acid, iron chloride sludge, and rinse water contaminated with zinc and acid. Disposal is regulated and carries a direct cost. Under COSHH regulations, operators must control exposure to acid mist and carry out a written risk assessment covering the fume generated during the process.

High-strength steels — generally those with a tensile strength above 1,000 MPa — carry a further risk. Atomic hydrogen produced by the acid reaction can diffuse into the steel lattice, causing delayed fracture under sustained load. This is hydrogen embrittlement. Standards BS EN ISO 9587 and BS EN ISO 9588 set out pre-treatment and baking-out requirements, but the risk cannot be eliminated entirely by post-pickling relief treatment. For critical components in high-strength grades, acid pickling may be unacceptable as a preparation method regardless of the quality of subsequent treatment.

### Fluxing

After rinsing and drying, fabrications pass through a flux bath containing zinc ammonium chloride solution. Flux removes any residual surface oxide that has reformed since pickling and activates the steel so the molten zinc can wet it uniformly. Flux is the final preparation step before the galvanising dip. Its effectiveness depends on the steel arriving clean and dry.

## What cleanliness standards apply?

For mechanically prepared steel — blast cleaned rather than acid pickled — ISO 8501-1 provides the reference grades. The standard cleanliness target for hot-dip galvanising is equivalent to Sa 2½, meaning near-white metal with at least 95% of the surface free from all visible residue. Surface profile also matters: a measured anchor pattern improves zinc adhesion on blast-cleaned steel.

Acid-pickled steel is assessed differently, but the underlying requirement is the same: no oxides, no contamination, uniform reactivity across the whole surface. The Galvanizers Association of UK publishes practical guidance on what fabricators and galvanisers should expect from incoming material.

## Where does laser cleaning offer an alternative?

Laser cleaning removes mill scale, rust, paint, and organic coatings without acid, without abrasive media, and without the associated waste streams. The process uses pulsed laser energy — typically from a Q-switched Nd:YAG source at 1.064 µm — that is absorbed selectively by the contaminant layer. Because the substrate beneath has different absorption characteristics, it is largely unaffected when the operator sets fluence correctly.

For galvanising preparation, laser cleaning is relevant in a number of specific situations.

**High-strength steels.** Where acid pickling presents an embrittlement risk, laser cleaning removes mill scale and surface oxides without introducing hydrogen into the steel lattice. The cleaned surface remains suitable for fluxing and galvanising in the conventional way.

**Large or complex fabrications.** Acid pickling requires full immersion in a tank of appropriate dimensions. Where a fabrication exceeds available tank capacity, or where geometry creates pockets that retain acid and resist rinsing, laser cleaning offers a dry, localised alternative that can be applied selectively.

**Refurbishment of coated steelwork.** Structural steelwork being regalvanised may carry existing paint systems, failed galvanising, or both. Laser cleaning removes these coatings without the mechanical damage caused by aggressive blasting, and without the chemical waste of caustic stripping.

**Workshop and site constraints.** Acid pickling facilities are fixed installations. Laser cleaning equipment is portable, allowing surface preparation to be completed on site or in workshops that lack acid handling infrastructure.

### What governs whether laser-cleaned steel is acceptable to a galvaniser?

Laser-cleaned steel still passes through the flux bath before the galvanising dip. Flux will remove light surface oxides that have reformed between cleaning and dipping, as it does with acid-pickled steel. The critical requirement is that the steel arrives free of organic contamination and without a heavy oxide layer that the flux cannot penetrate.

Operators should confirm acceptability with the galvaniser before committing to a laser-cleaning specification. Some galvanisers carry out incoming inspection and may request test dips or documented trials. Process parameters — fluence settings, scan speed, and pass count — should be recorded and repeatable.

Fume extraction is a COSHH obligation during laser ablation, regardless of substrate. Vaporised contaminants and metal oxide particulates are generated in the ablation plume and must be extracted and filtered. The fume composition will vary depending on what coatings are being removed, and this should be reflected in the site-specific COSHH assessment.

## Does laser cleaning address the hydrogen embrittlement risk?

The risk of hydrogen embrittlement from acid pickling is proportional to steel strength and acid contact time. For steels with tensile strengths above 1,000 MPa — including many high-tensile fasteners, structural cables, and pre-stressed components — it is a documented failure mode with its own body of standards and incident investigation literature.

Laser cleaning removes the hydrogen source from the process entirely. There is no acid, no electrochemical reaction at the steel surface, and no atomic hydrogen available to diffuse into the lattice. For fabricators and specifiers working with high-strength grades that require galvanised corrosion protection, this is a practical reason to evaluate laser-based preparation alongside the conventional pickling route — not as a novelty, but as a technical response to a recognised risk that pickling cannot fully eliminate.

The same logic applies to any component where post-pickle baking is impractical due to geometry, component size, or the tight tolerances that elevated temperature relief treatment can affect.
