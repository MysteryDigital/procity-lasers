Schools sit in an uncomfortable position when it comes to graffiti. Incidents arrive unpredictably, surfaces are varied, budgets are limited, and the reputational stakes are higher than at most commercial sites. A tagged boundary wall or defaced reception entrance sends a signal to parents, prospective pupils, and inspectors — whether intended or not. For facilities managers, the challenge is not simply removing graffiti when it appears, but building a repeatable process that keeps the estate presentable, protects surfaces from cumulative damage, and produces the documentation a governing body or Ofsted inspection might need.

## Why are schools particularly vulnerable to graffiti?

Several factors combine. School premises are open to the public during the day, surrounded by footpaths, and frequently targeted at weekends or during holiday periods when natural surveillance drops. A large secondary school may have several hundred metres of boundary fencing, multiple outbuildings, and sports facilities — all potential surfaces. Unlike a retail unit where a single manager oversees a defined exterior, a school estate can span dozens of distinct zones. Coordinating a response across all of them requires a system, not just a reactive call to a contractor.

Frequent incidents also carry a cumulative cost. Each removal job, if handled without a framework, tends to be more expensive per incident than a managed programme. Surface damage from repeated or inappropriate treatment compounds this over time.

## How should facilities teams monitor the estate?

Effective monitoring starts with a surface inventory. Map every external surface and record its material: engineering brick, sand-faced render, UPVC cladding, painted blockwork, powder-coated steel, or timber fencing. The material determines which removal method is appropriate, and knowing this in advance removes delay when an incident occurs.

Pair the inventory with a weekly walkround rota — ideally on Monday mornings, after the weekend when incidents are most likely. Assign responsibility clearly: split zones between site team members on larger estates. A digital log with timestamped photographs taken on a smartphone is adequate; the priority is consistency, not complexity. The log matters because it records when each incident was discovered and how quickly it was addressed — information that becomes important if a pattern of targeting emerges, or if the matter is referred to the police.

Some schools also fit CCTV to boundary walls and outbuildings. This can deter repeat offenders and occasionally provides material for a police referral, though the evidential threshold for enforcement action is high.

## How quickly should graffiti be removed?

Speed is consistently cited in local authority guidance as the primary deterrent against repeat marking. Environmental cues matter: graffiti left in place signals that a site is not actively managed, which tends to invite further incidents at the same location. A 24 to 48 hour response target is achievable on a managed estate and substantially reduces the likelihood of a tagged surface becoming a repeat target.

This has budget implications. Some facilities teams negotiate a standing contract with a specialist that gives priority response times and a predictable annual cost. For schools within a multi-academy trust, pooling a removal contract across several sites can reduce the per-site spend. A pre-agreed schedule of rates and response times is also easier to present to a finance committee than a series of ad hoc invoices.

## Which removal method suits which surface?

### Brick and render

Traditional painted masonry presents specific problems. Chemical solvents can drive pigment deeper into porous surfaces, making subsequent attempts more difficult. Abrasive blasting — even at relatively low pressure — causes surface erosion, and on sand-faced render can strip the texture layer entirely. Laser removal works by differential absorption: the contaminant layer absorbs laser energy more readily than the substrate beneath, so the paint is ablated without mechanically abrading the surface. On softer or porous brick, this is a meaningful material advantage. Operator calibration matters considerably — settings appropriate for hard engineering brick will differ from those needed for softer lime-mortared Victorian brickwork — so confirm that any contractor can demonstrate experience with your specific substrate type.

### UPVC windows, doors, and cladding

UPVC is chemically sensitive. Many solvent-based removers attack the surface, leaving a dull or hazed finish that may be as visible as the original mark. Laser removal has been used on UPVC and powder-coated steel, but parameters must be set conservatively and tested on an inconspicuous area before treating any prominent surface. For aerosol marks caught early on UPVC, mechanical wiping with a specialist product formulated for plastics is often effective; for older or heavier applications, a specialist assessment before committing to a method is worth the time.

### Playground equipment and metal surfaces

Powder-coated and galvanised steel surfaces — goal posts, climbing frames, cycle shelters — are common targets on school grounds. Anti-graffiti protective coatings applied at installation make future removal significantly easier: the mark sits on the sacrificial coating rather than bonding directly to the substrate. Where equipment was not originally protected this way, applying a sacrificial coating after each removal is a cost-effective long-term measure that reduces both labour time and the risk of surface damage on subsequent incidents.

## What documentation do governors and inspectors need?

Ofsted does not inspect graffiti management as a standalone category, but premises condition feeds into judgements on leadership and management. A school that can demonstrate a written policy, a maintained incident log, and a consistent rapid-response approach is better positioned than one that handles incidents informally.

A straightforward documentation pack should include:

- A surface inventory with material notes and any known sensitivities
- A written response procedure specifying target response times and method selection criteria
- A dated photograph log for each incident, covering both the defaced state and the outcome after removal
- Copies of contractor agreements or individual callout records
- A record of any incidents reported to the police and the reference numbers assigned

Governor reporting rarely requires photographic detail. A summary count of incidents per term and the average response time is usually sufficient for a safeguarding or premises committee agenda item.

## What reduces the frequency of incidents?

Physical measures help. Anti-graffiti protective coatings on high-risk surfaces reduce the time and cost of each removal. Improved lighting around boundary walls and outbuildings removes cover. Thorough, rapid removal within 24 to 48 hours of discovery remains the single most consistent deterrent in practice.

Engagement with the local authority's community safety or environmental enforcement team is worth pursuing where incidents cluster at a specific location or time of year. Schools are sometimes eligible for local authority rapid-response arrangements that can supplement a private contractor, depending on what the local offer covers.

A graffiti management programme does not need to be elaborate to be effective. A surface inventory, a weekly inspection rota, pre-agreed contractor terms, and a simple photograph log cover the core requirements and give a facilities manager something credible to report upward.
