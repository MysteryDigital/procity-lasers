Concrete is often treated as though it were a single, uniform material. In practice it spans a wide range of densities, textures, and surface profiles — from dense engineering-grade concrete to the highly porous open-textured blocks used widely in commercial and municipal construction. The same aerosol paint that sits close to the surface on dense concrete will have penetrated several millimetres into an open-textured block within hours of application. The removal method that clears one cleanly may leave ghost marks, surface damage, or residual pigment on the other.

This article is for estates managers, procurement officers, and site managers specifying graffiti removal contracts. It explains why a blanket specification fails to account for the variable behaviour of concrete substrates, and why that oversight leads to repeat treatments, surface erosion, and whole-life costs that a better initial specification would have avoided.

## What Range of Concrete Surfaces Are We Dealing With?

### Dense engineering concrete

Dense in-situ concrete, formed at low water-cement ratios and used in structural applications, highways infrastructure, and basement construction, has a relatively closed surface. Capillary porosity is low. Paint applied to a dense concrete face will not penetrate significantly unless the surface has carbonated, weathered, or been subjected to freeze-thaw damage that has opened surface voids. The coating tends to remain as a film on the face.

### Architectural and fairfaced concrete

Architectural concrete — exposed aggregate finishes, board-marked panels, fairfaced blockwork — presents a more varied picture. Surface texture is often deliberately roughened, creating recesses that resist both chemical and mechanical removal because physical access for removal media is limited.

### Porous concrete blockwork

Concrete masonry units vary significantly in porosity depending on aggregate type and mix design. Lightweight aggregate blocks can have surface absorption values closer to soft sandstone than to dense concrete. Paint applied to these surfaces migrates rapidly into the pore structure, and by the time removal is scheduled — often several weeks after the incident — the paint binder may have polymerised within the pore and cannot be reached by surface-applied solvents without saturating the block.

## How Does Porosity Affect Where the Paint Goes?

The key variable is not the paint type alone — it is the interaction between the paint vehicle (the liquid carrier that evaporates or polymerises as the coating cures) and the capillary structure of the substrate.

On a dense concrete surface, most of the vehicle evaporates at or very near the face, leaving pigment and binder largely as a surface film. Chemical dwell time is short because the active agent can reach the coating directly.

On a porous block, the vehicle carries pigment and binder into the capillary system. As it evaporates, the solid components are left distributed through the surface layer — sometimes to a depth of three to five millimetres or more, depending on the paint formulation and the absorption coefficient of the block. A chemical applied to the surface cannot reach pigment distributed deeper without saturating the block to that depth. That saturation can cause efflorescence as salts migrate with the drying front, and it introduces moisture that frost cycles may convert to face spalling.

## Why Does Pressure Washing Fail Differently on Each Type?

For dense concrete with fresh graffiti, high-pressure water combined with a surface-active agent can achieve reasonable results: the paint is still largely at the surface and the substrate is not highly absorbent.

On porous concrete block, pressure washing creates a different set of problems. Water penetrates the surface readily, and jet pressure set high enough to dislodge embedded pigment also erodes the surface paste of the block — the fine mortar matrix that surrounds the aggregate particles. Over repeated treatments this erosion exposes the aggregate, roughens the surface profile, and increases surface absorption: each treatment makes the substrate more susceptible to rapid paint penetration in future incidents. Reactive programmes that rely on pressure washing can reach a point where each successive treatment makes the next incident harder to clear and the surface progressively more difficult to maintain.

## What Happens When Abrasive Methods Are Used on Concrete?

Abrasive blasting is more commonly specified on concrete than on brick or stone, on the reasonable basis that dense concrete is more mechanically robust. For dense in-situ or precast concrete with adequate cover depth, it may be acceptable — though it should still be assessed against any carbonation layer and structural cover requirements.

On lightweight or porous concrete block, the force required to dislodge embedded pigment exceeds the mechanical strength of the surface paste. Surface erosion results, and future contamination penetrates further. For architectural finishes — board-marked concrete, exposed aggregate, polished surfaces — abrasive cleaning can permanently destroy the designed texture. The cost of cosmetic reinstatement, where achievable, typically exceeds the cost of a laser cleaning contract by a substantial margin.

## Does Laser Cleaning Offer a Different Approach on Porous Substrates?

The difficulty with porous concrete is not that the contaminant cannot be targeted — it is that conventional removal methods cannot apply sufficient energy or chemistry to the contaminant without also applying it to the substrate. Laser cleaning operates through a different mechanism.

A pulsed laser delivers energy absorbed selectively by the target material. The pigment and binder in graffiti paint absorb the laser energy and ablate — converting rapidly from solid to vapour or fine particulate. The energy transfer is fast enough that the surrounding substrate does not reach temperatures that cause thermal damage, provided the operator is working within calibrated fluence limits. The process is dry: no water enters the pore structure, so there is no risk of efflorescence or frost damage from saturation.

On porous concrete where paint has migrated into the surface layer, laser cleaning can work through the depth of contamination progressively, treating successive passes until the substrate colour returns. The depth of treatment is controlled by operator technique rather than fixed by the chemistry of a dwell-phase product. Fluence calibration is critical — incorrect settings carry their own risks, and operator competence matters as much as the technology. A surface that is not progressively eroded by treatment does not become progressively more difficult to treat.

## Specifying Correctly for the Concrete You Have

The most common error in graffiti removal specifications for concrete is specifying method purely on cost per square metre without accounting for substrate type. A price comparison that does not account for long-term surface condition and the number of repeat treatments a porous substrate will require is a day-rate comparison, not a whole-life cost comparison. On porous concrete, it frequently leads to escalating maintenance spend.

A site assessment that identifies the concrete type, surface absorption characteristics, and depth of paint penetration takes little time and significantly improves the reliability of the specification that follows. For any asset subject to repeat incidents, that assessment is well worth commissioning before the first treatment contract is placed.
