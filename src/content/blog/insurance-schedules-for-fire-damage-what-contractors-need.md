Fire damage claims are rarely straightforward. When a building suffers smoke, soot, or heat damage, the question of what can be cleaned versus what must be replaced sits at the centre of almost every insurer-funded remediation project. For contractors, getting this right — and documenting it correctly — determines both the scope of work approved and, ultimately, whether payment is made without dispute.

This guide sets out what the schedule of loss requires from a contractor, how reinstatement decisions are evidenced, and why the sequence of documentation matters as much as the quality of the work itself.

## What Does a Schedule of Loss Cover?

A schedule of loss is the insurer's structured record of the damage and the proposed cost of reinstatement or replacement. It is compiled by, or in consultation with, a loss adjuster, and it directly governs the scope and value of the claim.

For surface contamination — soot deposits, smoke staining, fire-suppression residues — the schedule distinguishes between two outcomes:

- **Reinstatement**: the element is cleaned, treated, and returned to pre-loss condition.
- **Replacement**: the element is damaged beyond economic or practical reinstatement and must be replaced.

The contractor's role is to provide the technical basis for which outcome applies to each affected element. A schedule that lists "clean stonework" without a supporting method statement and condition survey is an open invitation for a dispute at the valuation stage.

## How Are Cleaning and Replacement Decisions Documented?

The loss adjuster makes the formal decision, but they base it on the technical evidence provided by the contractor and, in complex cases, an independent building surveyor.

For each element, the contractor should provide:

- **A written condition assessment** — noting the substrate type, depth and type of contamination, and any pre-existing damage that predates the fire.
- **A technical rationale** — why the chosen method is appropriate for that substrate, and what outcome is achievable.
- **An honest statement of limits** — where full reinstatement is not possible, or where there is a residual risk of substrate damage, this should be stated in writing rather than discovered during the work or at the point of completion.

Where laser cleaning is the proposed method, the rationale should explain the differential absorption principle: the contaminant absorbs laser energy at a much higher rate than the substrate beneath, allowing controlled removal without abrasive contact. On porous stone, this is particularly relevant — unlike wet or abrasive methods, laser ablation does not drive residues deeper into the substrate. That is a technically supportable claim and worth making explicitly in any schedule submission.

Where replacement is the more cost-effective option, say so. Loss adjusters respect contractors who distinguish clearly between what can be cleaned and what cannot. It builds credibility for the entire schedule.

## What Method Statements Must Contractors Provide?

Every fire damage remediation project involving laser cleaning should be accompanied by a full RAMS — Risk Assessment and Method Statement — specific to the site, the substrate, and the contamination type.

For fire damage work, a method statement should address:

### Scope and sequence

State which elements are being treated, in what order, and why. If phasing is required — for example, to allow interim inspections or access adjustments — set this out explicitly.

### Laser parameters

Document the equipment model, wavelength, fluence settings, and pulse duration. This is not merely a technical formality. If a dispute arises about whether a substrate was damaged during treatment, a written record of the settings used is essential. Fluence calibration is operator-dependent and should be confirmed on a test area before full treatment begins.

### Fume and residue management

Laser ablation of fire-damaged surfaces generates a fume stream that may contain combustion residues, bound particulates, and potentially hazardous compounds depending on what burned. COSHH obligations apply. The method statement should address extraction arrangements, PPE requirements, and disposal of captured material in accordance with applicable waste regulations.

### Laser safety

Nd:YAG lasers operating at 1,064 nm are Class 4 devices. Operators must comply with BS EN 60825-1. The method statement should identify the laser safety officer, define the controlled zone, and note any interlock or exclusion arrangements.

### Access and containment

Fire-damaged structures may have compromised load-bearing or structural elements. The method statement should set out how access equipment is selected and erected, and how the work area is contained to prevent secondary contamination of areas already cleared.

For listed buildings or structures in conservation areas, confirm whether Listed Building Consent is required for the remediation work itself before commencing. In some cases the insurer or loss adjuster will have already addressed this; in others it falls to the contractor to raise it at the outset.

## Why Does Before/After Photographic Evidence Matter to Insurers?

Photographic evidence is not a courtesy — it is the primary record against which the claimed scope is verified at settlement. The absence of it gives grounds to challenge payment.

What is expected goes well beyond a single photograph at the start and one at the end. The standard typically required is:

- **Pre-treatment photographs** of each element to be treated, taken before any work commences, at sufficient resolution to show the nature and extent of contamination.
- **In-progress photographs** where treatment is staged, or where access conditions change during the project.
- **Post-treatment photographs** of each element, taken from the same position and distance as the pre-treatment images where possible, to allow direct comparison.
- **Test area records** showing the calibration process carried out before full treatment, particularly where the substrate is sensitive or the pre-loss condition was already imperfect.

Photographs should be date-stamped and, where possible, geotagged. If the site has multiple areas of similar appearance — for example, repeated bays in a terrace — label each element clearly so there is no ambiguity about which image corresponds to which location in the schedule.

## Does the Documentation Sequence Affect Payment?

Yes — and this is where many otherwise competent contractors run into difficulty.

Insurers expect documentation to precede the work it describes. A method statement submitted after the job is complete, or before/after photographs assembled retrospectively, will face scrutiny and may result in delayed or reduced settlement.

The correct sequence is:

1. Survey and condition assessment submitted to the loss adjuster.
2. Schedule of proposed works agreed, including cleaning versus replacement decisions.
3. Method statement approved before work commences.
4. Pre-treatment photographs taken and logged against the schedule.
5. Work carried out in accordance with the approved method statement.
6. Progress and completion photographs taken at each stage.
7. Completion record and invoice submitted with photographic evidence attached.

Deviating from this sequence — even where the outcome is demonstrably good — can delay settlement or reduce the approved value of the claim. Treating the paperwork with the same rigour as the technical work is not bureaucracy: it is how the claim gets paid in full.
