Council graffiti removal contracts tend to fail at the specification stage, not during delivery. A contractor who removes graffiti by pressure washing a limestone façade has technically fulfilled an under-specified brief, even if they have damaged the substrate in the process. A contract that omits response time categories leaves both parties exposed when offensive content on a school wall sits untreated for a fortnight. Getting the specification right protects the council, sets clear expectations for bidders, and produces better outcomes on the ground.

This guide covers the practical elements of a robust graffiti removal specification: scope definition, surface and substrate categorisation, service level tiers, method requirements, health and safety obligations, and the evidence framework that lets you manage the contract after award.

## Define the Scope Before Specifying Anything Else

### Surface Types and Substrate Categories

The single most consequential decision in a graffiti removal specification is how you categorise surfaces. Graffiti removal is not a single technical operation. Removing spray paint from smooth-rendered concrete is straightforward. Removing it from soft sandstone, brick with lime mortar pointing, or a painted heritage structure requires a different method, a different skill level, and in some cases different regulatory approvals.

Your specification should group surfaces into categories and state which removal methods are approved for each. A workable minimum grouping covers smooth non-porous surfaces, hard porous masonry, soft or friable masonry, painted or coated surfaces, and mixed substrates such as timber and metal. For each category, the spec should state whether the contractor must carry out a test patch before commencing work on any new substrate type encountered. On sensitive substrates, this requirement is not optional.

### Ownership and Geographic Boundaries

Define clearly which assets are in scope. Council-owned structures, highway furniture, and public realm paving are typically straightforward. Complications arise with bridge structures, canal-side walls, railway infrastructure, and subways shared with transport operators, where ownership may rest with a separate statutory body.

Clarify at the outset whether the contractor is expected to manage access permissions with third-party asset owners, or whether the council will obtain access agreements separately. Leaving this ambiguous leads to delays and retrospective cost disputes.

## Response Time Tiers and Service Level Definitions

Most graffiti removal contracts use a tiered response model based on the nature and visibility of the graffiti. The tier structure varies between councils, but a common approach distinguishes between offensive or discriminatory content, high-visibility public locations such as town centre walls or transport hubs, and lower-visibility residential or peripheral locations.

Whatever tiers you choose, the specification must define what triggers each tier, when the response time clock starts (report receipt, contractor acknowledgement, or site attendance), and whether the clock stops on attendance or on verified completion. Response time failures are one of the most common causes of performance disputes in this type of contract. Vague definitions allow contractors to interpret the requirement in whichever direction suits them at the point of dispute.

The escalation route for missed service levels should also be set out in the specification rather than left to the general contract conditions.

## Specifying the Removal Method

### Solvent and Chemical Methods

Chemical solvents remain the most widely used method for routine graffiti removal. If you permit chemical methods, the specification should require the contractor to demonstrate that their chosen products are compatible with the substrate. Certain solvents can strip applied finishes, bleach natural stone, or introduce chloride contamination into masonry. Requiring product data sheets and substrate compatibility evidence before work commences is a reasonable and defensible condition.

### Pressure Washing

Pressure washing is appropriate for some surfaces and destructive on others. The specification should set maximum working pressures for different substrate categories, or require that pressure settings be agreed with a supervising officer before each site. Unconstrained high-pressure water on historic masonry is a common source of substrate damage that then generates disagreement about who bears the cost of remediation.

### Laser Removal

Laser cleaning is increasingly specified for listed structures and conservation areas, where chemical or mechanical methods risk surface damage or are incompatible with planning conditions. Works to listed structures fall under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990, and a conservation officer will typically constrain the methods available. Laser removal produces no chemical waste stream and involves no mechanical abrasion, which makes it a practical option where substrate sensitivity is the overriding constraint.

### When to Specify Method-Neutral

For most routine public realm graffiti, specifying method-neutral and requiring the contractor to demonstrate substrate suitability is the appropriate approach. It widens the bidder pool and avoids tying the contract to a single technique that may not suit every situation encountered. Reserve method-specific requirements for contracts that include sensitive, listed, or high-value assets where the risk of method-related damage justifies the narrower specification.

## Health, Safety, and Environmental Obligations

Any specification that permits chemical removal methods should require the contractor to provide COSHH assessments under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 for all materials used on site. Waste materials must be disposed of in compliance with the Environmental Protection Act 1990, and for work near surface water drainage, runoff risk should be assessed and controlled.

Site safety requirements should address working at height, traffic management where work occurs on or adjacent to the highway, and public exclusion zones where chemical sprays or high-pressure water are in use. These provisions should reference the contractor's own safe system of work rather than simply reproducing generic Health and Safety at Work etc Act 1974 obligations.

## Performance Evidence and Reporting

A graffiti removal contract without a structured reporting requirement is difficult to manage and even harder to audit. Specify that the contractor must provide photographic evidence for each removal: a georeferenced before photograph and a completion photograph, both with timestamps. This creates an audit trail that supports payment verification and enables performance monitoring.

A monthly or quarterly management report covering job volumes by tier, response time compliance rates, and recurring hotspot locations allows the council to manage the contract proactively and provides input for decisions about anti-graffiti coating programmes or environmental design interventions.

## Common Causes of Disputes in Under-Specified Contracts

The most frequent sources of dispute in graffiti removal contracts are substrate damage from an unsuitable removal method, missed response times where the trigger or clock mechanism was ambiguous, and scope creep where the contractor claims additional cost for assets not clearly defined in the brief. A robust specification addresses all three by categorising surfaces explicitly, defining the response time mechanism without ambiguity, and mapping asset ownership boundaries before the contract is tendered.

When a contract is awarded on an under-specified brief, specification risk transfers to the contractor, who will price it conservatively or dispute it retrospectively when problems arise.
