## The Prevalence of Lead Paint in Older Buildings

Most buildings constructed before the late 1960s contain lead-based paint somewhere in their fabric. Lead was added to paint as a pigment, drying agent, and stabiliser; its use was standard across residential, commercial, and public sector stock until progressive restrictions culminated in a prohibition on its use in decorative paints sold in the UK. Properties built between the early twentieth century and the 1970s are therefore treated as presumed to contain lead paint unless survey evidence says otherwise.

The hazard arises not from intact paint on a sound substrate, but from disturbance. Sanding, scraping, torching, or mechanical abrasion during refurbishment or maintenance generates dust and fume that carry the risk of lead exposure. At that point the activity becomes subject to specific legal obligations, and the choice of removal method has a direct bearing on the scale of the controls required.

## What the Law Requires Before Work Begins

### Control of Lead at Work Regulations 2002

The primary regulatory instrument is the Control of Lead at Work Regulations 2002. These regulations require employers to assess exposure risk before work commences and to prevent or adequately control that exposure where it is likely to be significant. The assessment must be specific to the site, the substrate, and the proposed removal method. Generic assessments submitted without modification for each project do not satisfy the requirement.

Where the assessment identifies significant risk, the controls required may include enclosed working areas, negative-pressure ventilation, air monitoring during the work, health surveillance for regularly exposed workers, and dedicated hygiene facilities. The assessment must be produced by a competent person, and clients commissioning the work are entitled to request sight of it before the contractor starts.

### COSHH 2002 and the Control Hierarchy

The Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH) apply alongside the lead-specific regulations. COSHH establishes the hierarchy of controls: eliminate the substance if possible, substitute a less hazardous alternative, and control exposure at source before relying on personal protective equipment. In practice the two frameworks are addressed together in a single integrated assessment. The hierarchy is directly relevant to method selection: a removal technique that generates less airborne particulate represents a higher-order control than one that relies primarily on respiratory protective equipment to manage exposure after the fact.

### CDM 2015 and Pre-Construction Information

For projects within the scope of the Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015, including most refurbishments of commercial or public buildings, the Principal Designer carries a duty to identify foreseeable risks and ensure they are captured in pre-construction health and safety information. Lead paint identified in surveys must be communicated before contractor appointment. For councils, housing associations, and heritage bodies commissioning work on older stock, this means the obligation to surface the lead paint risk sits within the project structure and should not be left to a contractor to discover on site.

## Health Risks During Removal

Lead is a cumulative toxin. It accumulates in the body over time and affects the nervous system, kidneys, and blood-forming processes; the effects at elevated exposure levels are serious and are not reversible. Workers carrying out lead paint removal face risk from inhaling dust and fume, and from ingesting particles via hand-to-mouth contact where hygiene controls are inadequate.

The risk is not limited to direct workers. Uncontrolled lead dust from exterior removal operations can migrate to adjacent areas and, on outdoor sites, can settle on surrounding land. On urban sites near schools, parks, or public footways this is a material consideration. Public sector clients and principal contractors working in occupied or publicly accessible environments should treat airborne lead management as a specification requirement rather than a site-level discretion.

## Removal Methods and Their Compliance Profiles

The choice of removal method is not only a matter of programme or cost. It determines the volume of lead dust generated, the scale of the controls required, and the risk to the substrate.

### Mechanical Abrasion

Grit blasting, needle gunning, disc grinding, and hand scraping are effective at removing paint from large surface areas. All generate significant quantities of fine dust and, on lead-painted surfaces, that dust is contaminated. Containing and capturing it requires careful enclosure with negative-pressure extraction, and air monitoring during work is generally necessary. On sensitive or detailed substrates, mechanical methods also carry a risk of surface damage, which on historic fabric is irreversible.

### Chemical Stripping

Chemical strippers dissolve the paint binder, reducing mechanical dust generation. The trade-off is that the resulting waste is a lead-contaminated liquid or gel requiring classification and disposal as hazardous waste. The chemical agents themselves carry COSHH obligations, and on historic or porous substrates, product selection must be matched carefully to the surface material: the wrong chemistry can stain stonework, react with lime mortars, or drive dissolved pigments deeper into the substrate.

### Laser Ablation

Laser cleaning delivers controlled pulses of laser energy to the paint surface. The energy ablates the coating without mechanical contact and without chemical agents. Dust and fume are produced at the point of ablation, so local extraction remains necessary, but the volume of airborne particulate generated is substantially lower than with mechanical abrasion. The process is also highly controllable: energy levels, pulse duration, and spot size can all be adjusted to target paint layers without disturbing the substrate beneath. This is particularly relevant on carved stonework, ornate metalwork, and historic surfaces where both compliance and substrate preservation matter.

For lead paint removal, the lower particulate output of laser ablation can reduce the scope of environmental containment required and simplify the COSHH assessment. It does not remove the need for assessment and extraction, but it can be a significant factor in method selection on constrained urban sites or on heritage structures where conventional methods would require more extensive enclosure.

## Waste Classification and Disposal

Lead paint waste, regardless of the removal method, is classified as hazardous waste under the Hazardous Waste (England and Wales) Regulations 2005. Collection, storage, transport, and disposal must all be handled by appropriately licensed contractors, with consignment notes maintained throughout. The duty of care rests with the site owner and principal contractor from generation to final disposal.

Waste volumes from mechanical removal on a large building can be substantial. Hazardous waste disposal costs and logistics should be built into the project specification and pricing at the outset, not treated as a variable to be resolved once work is under way.

## Procuring the Work: What a Method Statement Should Cover

A sound method statement for lead paint removal should address the substrate condition, the removal technique, the containment and extraction approach, the respiratory protective equipment specification, the air monitoring protocol, and the waste disposal route. On listed buildings or structures within conservation areas, the method statement may also need to address requirements under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990 where the removal affects original fabric or decorative features.

Specifiers and contract managers reviewing these documents should look for evidence of a site-specific risk assessment rather than a template submitted without modification. A contractor with genuine experience in lead paint removal on occupied or sensitive sites should be able to demonstrate that the assessment addresses the specific conditions of the project.
