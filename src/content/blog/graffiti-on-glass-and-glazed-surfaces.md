Glass is a common target for graffiti, and the automatic response from many contractors and glaziers is to quote for replacement. That quote is sometimes the right answer — but not always, and in some contexts, replacement is not legally permissible at all.

Understanding what type of graffiti has been applied to glass is the first step. The three categories — surface contamination, acid etching, and mechanical scratching — have very different prognoses, and the treatment options differ accordingly.

## What type of graffiti is on your glass?

Glass graffiti is not a single problem. Three distinct types call for different responses, and misidentifying the type can lead to either unnecessary replacement costs or failed cleaning attempts.

### Surface contamination: spray paint, markers, and stickers

Spray paint applied to glass sits on the surface. The paint film bonds to the glass, but it does not alter the glass matrix beneath. Permanent markers, paint pens, and adhesive stickers fall into the same category: they are surface contaminants rather than structural damage.

Surface contamination on glass is generally cleanable. Chemical solvents will remove most spray paints from glass, though aggressive solvents can affect surrounding frames, seals, and adjacent materials. Mechanical methods such as scrapers work for some paint types but risk introducing scratches. Laser cleaning offers a third route, particularly where solvent use is constrained or where the surrounding frame materials are sensitive.

### Acid etching

Acid etching — typically applied using hydrofluoric acid-based etching fluid or cream, which is widely available in craft and art supply contexts — chemically alters the surface of the glass. The acid attacks the silica matrix and creates a permanently frosted or opaque area. Unlike spray paint, the damage is not on the glass: it is in the glass.

Very light acid etching, where penetration is shallow, can sometimes be improved by surface polishing. In practice, however, most acid etching visible to the naked eye has penetrated too deeply for polishing to restore acceptable optical clarity. Replacement is usually required.

### Mechanical scratching

Diamond-tipped scribes, coins, keys, and other abrasive objects are used to scratch or score glass. The damage is physical removal of material from the glass surface. As with acid etching, the damage cannot be undone by cleaning.

Shallow surface scratches can often be reduced or eliminated by professional polishing, which removes a thin layer of surrounding glass to bring the surface back to a level plane. The practical limit is determined by the depth of the scratch and the acceptable distortion introduced by the polishing process. Deep scratches, or scratches on thin glass panels, generally require replacement.

## Does acid etching always mean replacement?

Not always, but it usually does. The key variable is the depth of attack. A brief contact with etching cream — interrupted before full effect — may leave very shallow surface hazing that polishing can improve. A fully applied deliberate etch, left for the intended contact time, will typically have penetrated beyond what polishing can remedy without introducing unacceptable optical distortion.

Where partial improvement is acceptable — for example, reducing the visual prominence of etching on a utility window — polishing may be worth attempting before committing to replacement. On display glazing, shopfronts, or historic glass, the optical standard is usually higher and replacement more likely to be necessary.

## When is laser cleaning relevant for glass?

Laser cleaning is well suited to surface contamination on glass. At the wavelength used by Q-switched Nd:YAG systems (1.064µm), glass is largely transparent to the laser energy. Paint and other surface coatings absorb the energy and ablate cleanly from the surface. This differential absorption — the contaminant absorbs far more energy than the substrate — is what makes the process effective and controlled.

For spray paint, markers, and similar surface contamination, laser cleaning removes the coating without the need for chemical solvents, abrasive contact, or water. This makes it particularly relevant where the surrounding frame is delicate or where chemical use is constrained: inside occupied buildings, in areas with drainage restrictions, or on historic frames where solvent contact may damage original finishes.

Laser cannot reverse acid etching or mechanical scratching. The process removes surface material; it cannot restore material that has been chemically altered or physically removed.

## What about historic glass in listed buildings?

This is where the treatment decision becomes significantly more constrained, and where the distinction between cleanable and non-cleanable graffiti becomes critical.

Historic glazing — crown glass, cylinder glass, early plate glass, and hand-painted decorative glazing produced before the widespread adoption of float glass in the twentieth century — has characteristics that modern glass does not replicate. Slight variations in thickness, surface texture, and optical quality are inherent to the manufacturing process of the period. They are also, in heritage terms, irreplaceable.

In listed buildings, replacing historic glazing typically requires Listed Building Consent. Historic England guidance makes clear that original fabric should be retained wherever possible, and that like-for-like replacement of historic glass is rarely achievable with modern products. A glazier's quote for replacement, however technically straightforward it may appear, does not resolve the consent question.

Where the graffiti on historic glass is surface contamination — spray paint applied by vandals, for instance — cleaning is both the practical and the heritage-appropriate response. Laser cleaning is a particularly careful method in this context: no abrasive contact, no chemical ingress into surrounding lime putty or historic lead, and no risk of the solvent migration that wet chemical methods introduce.

Where acid etching or deep scratching has damaged historic glass, the situation is more difficult. Replacement may not be approved, and polishing may not restore acceptable clarity. In these cases, conservation officers and Historic England specialist staff should be consulted early. Do not assume that because replacement is technically straightforward, it is therefore the permitted route.

## Getting the initial assessment right

The first requirement in any glass graffiti case is accurate identification of the damage type. A visual inspection under appropriate lighting will usually distinguish surface contamination from acid etching or scratching. Touching the surface confirms whether the glass matrix has been physically altered.

If you are uncertain, or if the glazing has heritage significance, commissioning a specialist assessment before instructing works is prudent. Acting on an incorrect assumption — cleaning what requires replacement, or replacing what could be cleaned — creates additional costs and, in a listed building, potential regulatory exposure.
