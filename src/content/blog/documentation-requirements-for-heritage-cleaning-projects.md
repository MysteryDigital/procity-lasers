## Why Is Documentation Not Optional on Heritage Sites?

When a cleaning project involves a listed building or scheduled monument, the regulatory context changes significantly. This is not a matter of good housekeeping — it is a legal and professional obligation enforced through the planning consent regime.

Historic England's guidance and the requirements of local planning authorities (LPAs) establish a clear expectation: any intervention on a protected structure should be recorded before work begins, documented during treatment, and formally reported afterwards. The level of detail required is higher than most contractors encounter on standard commercial work, and underestimating it carries real risk — to the building and to the contractor's professional standing.

The four core documents are the condition survey, the method statement, the photographic record, and the post-treatment report. Each serves a distinct purpose, and responsibility for producing them does not always fall to the same party.

## What Is a Condition Survey and Who Produces It?

A condition survey establishes the baseline state of the structure before any intervention. For heritage cleaning work, this means recording the nature and extent of soiling, biological growth, graffiti, or other surface deposits; the condition of the substrate beneath; and any pre-existing defects, cracks, or areas of friability that might be affected by the cleaning process.

The survey is typically commissioned by the client or their appointed heritage consultant, not the cleaning contractor. However, the contractor should review it before mobilising, because it informs the method statement and provides a reference point if any dispute arises about pre-existing damage.

For scheduled monuments and Grade I or Grade II* listed buildings, the survey may need to be carried out by or reviewed by an accredited conservator. LPAs will sometimes specify this in Listed Building Consent conditions. Where consent has not yet been obtained, a condition survey is usually required as part of the application.

## What Does a Method Statement for Heritage Work Need to Cover?

A method statement for heritage cleaning is more detailed than a standard COSHH or site safety document. It must explain the proposed cleaning method, the rationale for selecting it over alternatives, the proposed test area protocol, the equipment and settings to be used, and the procedures for monitoring substrate response during treatment.

For laser cleaning specifically, this means stating the laser type, wavelength, pulse duration, and the fluence range to be applied. It must also address fume extraction — the ablation process releases a particulate fume stream, and managing this is a genuine COSHH obligation. Operators working with Class 4 laser equipment must comply with BS EN 60825-1, and the method statement should confirm that appropriate laser safety measures are in place.

Historic England's guidance indicates that method statements for work on protected structures should be conservative in scope — specifying minimum effective intervention rather than the most aggressive approach available. The method statement is often reviewed by the LPA or a specialist conservation adviser before consent is granted.

### Who Approves the Method Statement?

In practice, approval sits with the client's heritage consultant, the LPA conservation officer, and in some cases Historic England directly for the most significant designations. The cleaning contractor prepares the document; obtaining approval is the client's responsibility. Contractors should confirm in writing that an approved method statement is in place before work begins — this is a protective measure, not an administrative obstruction.

## Photographic Records: Before, During, and After

Photographic documentation is not simply a commercial asset for marketing. On heritage sites, it functions as an evidential record that may be referenced in any future investigation — a conservation review, a planning inquiry, or a dispute about the scope or effect of the works carried out.

The photographic record should cover four stages:

- **Pre-treatment**: overall views establishing the site context, close-ups of the areas to be treated, and detail shots of any pre-existing defects noted in the condition survey.
- **Test area**: before-and-after photographs of the trial area used to calibrate the cleaning method, taken at the same resolution and under comparable lighting conditions.
- **In progress**: periodic photographs showing the progression of work, particularly at any location where the substrate's response raised a question during treatment.
- **Post-treatment**: coverage matching the pre-treatment images, taken once surfaces have dried and settled — not immediately after cleaning, when moisture can alter the apparent colour of stone.

Where possible, photographs should include calibrated colour references and scale bars. For significant designations, LPAs may specify that photographic records follow Historic England's guidance on the measured survey and recording of historic buildings.

## What Should a Post-Treatment Report Contain?

The post-treatment report closes the loop on the documentation package. It records what was actually done — the areas treated, the equipment settings used, any deviations from the method statement and the reasons for them, and the observed outcome.

It should also provide a comparison of the post-treatment condition against the baseline established in the condition survey, noting any changes other than the intended cleaning effect. If any micro-cracking, surfacing, or colour variation is observed, this must be documented and brought to the client's attention promptly, not omitted from the record.

The report is typically prepared by the contractor and reviewed by the client's heritage consultant. It forms part of the project file that the client may be required to retain under the terms of the planning consent, and in some cases to submit formally to the LPA on completion of works.

## How Does Documentation Protect the Contractor?

Beyond regulatory compliance, thorough documentation is one of the most practical forms of professional protection available to a cleaning contractor working on protected structures.

A condition survey reviewed and signed off before work begins means that any pre-existing defect cannot reasonably be attributed to the cleaning operation. An approved method statement means the contractor worked within a defined scope that the relevant authorities had reviewed. A complete photographic record and post-treatment report mean that the outcome of the work is a matter of documented fact, not disputed recollection.

Heritage buildings attract long-term scrutiny. Conservation officers, heritage bodies, and insurers may review the works months or years after completion. Contractors who cannot produce contemporaneous documentation carry a professional exposure that those with complete project files do not.

## Starting a Heritage Cleaning Project the Right Way

The documentation package should be agreed before any pricing or programme is confirmed. Trying to retrofit records after work has begun is both practically difficult and professionally unsatisfactory. Where Listed Building Consent is required, the method statement and supporting documentation are part of the consent application, not an afterthought.
