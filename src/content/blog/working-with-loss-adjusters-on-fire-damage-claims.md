Fire damage remediation claims involve multiple parties with different priorities: the policyholder wants the building restored, the insurer wants liability defined, and the loss adjuster must satisfy both while managing indemnity exposure. For the remediation contractor, the challenge is producing a workstream that meets the adjuster's evidential requirements from day one, rather than retrofitting documentation to a claim that has already progressed.

This guide sets out the practical steps contractors should take when working alongside loss adjusters on commercial and institutional fire damage claims.

## Understanding the Loss Adjuster's Role

A loss adjuster acts on behalf of the insurer to investigate, quantify, and recommend settlement of a claim. They are not simply a gatekeeper for payment. Their function includes verifying that the scope of damage is consistent with the reported cause, that proposed remediation costs are reasonable, and that the works will restore the insured to their pre-loss position.

Loss adjusters operating on commercial claims are typically members of the Chartered Institute of Loss Adjusters and are bound by professional obligations that require their reports to withstand audit scrutiny. Every element of the contractor's submission becomes part of the adjuster's evidential chain. Understanding this allows contractors to pitch their documentation at the right level. The adjuster is not reading for marketing purposes; they are reading to build a defensible settlement file.

## What Documentation Loss Adjusters Require

### Initial Condition and Scope Report

The first substantive document a contractor should produce is a detailed condition report covering the extent of fire, smoke, and heat damage. This is distinct from a quotation. A scope report describes what is present, not what the contractor proposes to charge.

The report should identify the affected substrates (masonry, structural steel, timber, composite, or mixed), the nature of the contamination (soot, char, water damage from suppression activity), and any structural concerns that require specialist assessment before remediation can safely begin. It should also record grid references or zone numbers that will be used consistently throughout the claim file.

Photographs should be embedded within the report rather than supplied as a separate image dump. Adjusters reviewing claims across multiple loss events do not have time to correlate numbered images with a written scope that lives in a separate file. Inline images with captions and zone references make review faster and sign-off more defensible.

### Photographic Evidence Standards

Photography is the primary evidentiary medium in a fire damage claim. Courts, insurers, and adjusters all treat photographic records as the baseline against which final works are compared.

Each photograph should include a scale reference where relevant, a visible date stamp, and a consistent spatial anchor such as a fixed building feature or a gridded floor plan so that before and after comparisons are unambiguous. For larger or more complex losses, a photographic schedule cross-referenced to the scope report prevents later disputes about whether a specific area was treated or whether additional damage predated the remediation.

Thermal imaging data, where used, should be retained and supplied alongside conventional photography. It provides an independent validation of the scope and can be particularly useful where the depth of heat penetration into masonry or insulated panels was not apparent on visual inspection alone.

### Method Statement and Risk Assessment

Most insurers will not authorise commencement of specialist remediation without an approved method statement. For laser cleaning and residue removal, this document needs to address each of the following without vague or generic language:

- The equipment proposed, including operating wavelength and output parameters as relevant to the substrate
- The substrate types being treated and the basis on which those parameters were selected
- Containment arrangements for dislodged particulates, including compliance with the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 where soot, asbestos-containing materials, or other hazardous residues may be present
- Waste classification and the intended disposal route

A method statement that references the correct regulations by name signals professionalism and reduces the likelihood of queries. Where a specialist subcontractor is being engaged for a particular element of the works, their method statement should be appended to the principal contractor's submission rather than paraphrased, so the adjuster can review it in full.

## Structuring a Claim-Ready Workstream

### Pre-Start Sign-Off

Do not commence specialist remediation works without written authorisation from the loss adjuster or the insurer's appointed representative. Oral confirmations and email responses from the policyholder alone are not sufficient where the insurer is meeting the cost.

The practical sequence at pre-start stage is as follows. Submit the condition report and method statement together, not in separate stages. Obtain written instructions to proceed and retain them on file. Confirm the agreed scope in a brief instruction acknowledgement referencing the report version and date. Agree a reporting interval for stage photographs before works begin.

Any scope items that are uncertain at initial inspection should be flagged as provisional and priced separately. Absorbing uncertain scope into a lump sum figure creates variation disputes at final account, which adjusters will query methodically.

### Stage Reporting During Works

Adjusters managing commercial losses are handling multiple claims simultaneously. Contractors who supply unsolicited progress reports are providing a practical service, not creating paperwork. A brief weekly or per-stage report covering work completed, percentage of agreed scope achieved, and any emerging scope items keeps the adjuster informed without requiring a site visit at every phase.

Where additional damage is uncovered during works, a supplementary scope note should be raised promptly rather than carried through to the final account. This is common in fire damage remediation: heat penetration into masonry cavities, concealed structural steel corrosion from suppression water, and sub-surface smoke migration are all regularly identified only once surface layers are removed.

## Managing Supplementary Claims

Additional scope identified during works is a routine feature of fire damage remediation. Insurers will generally accept supplementary items where they arise from the original cause of loss, but the evidential standard is the same as for the primary claim.

Photograph newly identified damage before any further remediation is carried out. Submit a supplementary scope note cross-referenced to the original report and to the specific zone or grid reference. Obtain written sign-off before proceeding with the additional work.

The common failure at this stage is treating supplementary items as part of the main works and raising them on the final account. Adjusters will query every unapproved line item, which delays settlement and introduces an adversarial element to a relationship that works better when it is collaborative.

## Heritage and Listed Structure Considerations

Where a fire damage claim involves a listed building or a structure in a conservation area, the evidential and procedural requirements increase materially. The Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990 restricts certain categories of repair and alteration, and the local planning authority's conservation officer will typically need to be consulted before remediation methods are finalised.

For laser cleaning on heritage fabric specifically, the adjuster will want confirmation that the proposed method has been assessed against the relevant substrate type and does not conflict with any listed building consent conditions. A method statement alone is insufficient in this context. A brief technical rationale for the choice of method over alternatives such as chemical cleaning, poulticing, or controlled abrasion strengthens the submission and gives the adjuster the material they need to support approval.

## Working With Specialist Subcontractors

Where a principal contractor is appointing a specialist for residue removal or surface decontamination, the loss adjuster is entitled to review the subcontractor's credentials as part of the overall claim file. This is not an unusual request. It reflects the adjuster's duty to confirm that works will be carried out to a standard consistent with restoring the insured asset.

Contractors should obtain their subcontractor's current public liability insurance certificate, relevant accreditations, and method statement before submitting to the adjuster. Waiting until the adjuster requests these documents adds avoidable delays at the approval stage, and in a live loss situation those delays have a direct cost to the policyholder.
