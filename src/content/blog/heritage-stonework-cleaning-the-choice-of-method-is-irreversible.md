Heritage stonework is not like commercial property maintenance. When a cleaning method is applied to a Victorian sandstone facade or a medieval limestone wall, there is no second chance. If the method is wrong, the damage is structural, the historic finish is altered, or the patina is gone. None of these outcomes can be reversed.

For specifiers, procurement officers, and conservation professionals, this irreversibility is the central fact around which every other decision must be organised.

## Why Heritage Stone Responds Differently

Heritage buildings present a more complex cleaning problem than modern construction for several reasons that are not always apparent before work begins.

### Substrate variation within a single structure

Stone is not uniform. Sandstone laid in 1880 may differ considerably in porosity, bedding plane, and residual moisture from sandstone quarried in the same period from a different source. Limestone varies in density and crystalline structure. Even within a single elevation, the original builders may have mixed stone from different quarries when supplies ran short, introduced later repairs in different materials, or patched weathered sections with lime mortar of varying composition. A cleaning method that is appropriate for one zone may be too aggressive for another only a few metres away.

### The role of patina and soiling layers

On an unprotected heritage surface, biological growth, atmospheric soiling, and applied coatings do not sit cleanly on top of the stone. They penetrate, react with, and in some cases stabilise the surface they colonise. Algae and lichen hold moisture against the stone but can also, over decades, provide a degree of protection from mechanical weathering. Some soiling layers carry sulfate salts migrated from within the stone itself. Removing the layer without understanding what lies beneath can expose a substrate that was in equilibrium with its covering and is now newly vulnerable.

This is not an argument against cleaning. It is an argument for understanding what you are cleaning before choosing how.

## What Cleaning Methods Are Available?

There is no universal method for heritage stonework, and a reputable contractor will tell you this before any work is proposed.

### Water-based methods

Water washing, either as a controlled mist or a low-pressure rinse, is gentle and avoids chemical introduction. It is effective for surface dust and loosely adhering atmospheric soiling. Its limitations in heritage contexts are significant: it can mobilise soluble salts within porous stone, it may cause staining through iron oxidation where ferrous inclusions are present, and it introduces moisture that must be managed carefully in structures where freeze-thaw action is a risk. Poultice systems, which use an absorbent material to draw soluble salts out of the stone, are a related technique used in controlled conservation work but require specialist application and monitoring.

### Chemical cleaning

Proprietary cleaning systems, typically acid or alkali formulations depending on the substrate and soiling type, can be effective on certain stonework. They are also the method most frequently associated with irreversible damage in heritage contexts. Acid-based products can dissolve calcium carbonate, the binding mineral in many limestones and in the lime mortars that surround them. Inappropriate alkali treatments can cause silica gel formation in sandstone, permanently altering the surface texture. Residual chemistry left in the stone after neutralisation and rinsing can continue reacting for months. Where chemical cleaning is specified, the contractor should hold assessments under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 and should be able to demonstrate the product has been trialled on a matching substrate under controlled conditions.

### Abrasive methods

Grit blasting, bristle tools, and fine abrasive media can remove persistent soiling but are destructive by nature. The surface of a stone is its most durable layer, compacted and mineralised by prolonged exposure. Once abraded, the fresh material beneath is softer and more porous. For heritage masonry, abrasive methods are generally considered inappropriate except in tightly defined circumstances, such as the removal of a previous treatment that was itself inappropriate. They are rarely endorsed in Historic England guidance for primary cleaning operations.

### Laser ablation

Laser cleaning works by delivering short, controlled pulses of energy to the surface. The soiling layer absorbs the energy and is removed, while the underlying stone, which reflects the wavelength differently, is largely unaffected. The method is substrate-selective and can be calibrated in real time by an operator monitoring the surface response directly. It produces no aqueous runoff, introduces no chemistry into the stone, and leaves no abrasive residue. The precision it offers makes it well-suited to listed structures, decorative stonework, and surfaces where adjoining materials must not be disturbed. It is not the right tool for every job, and it is not the least expensive option available, but in contexts where the margin for error is narrow, the degree of control it provides is a meaningful advantage.

## The Planning and Regulatory Framework

Work to listed buildings in England and Wales requires listed building consent under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990. This applies not only to structural alterations but to any work that would affect the character of a listed structure, and cleaning falls within that scope. Local planning authorities and their conservation officers interpret this broadly. In practice, a proposed cleaning method may need to be submitted for approval before any work begins, and in some cases the authority will require a trial panel to be assessed before consent is granted for the full scope.

Historic England publishes technical guidance on the care and repair of historic structures. These documents do not mandate specific methods, but they establish principles that planning authorities apply when evaluating applications. Method statements that conflict with those principles are likely to be refused or required to be substantially revised before consent is issued.

## How to Specify the Right Method

Specification without site-specific evidence is a significant liability in heritage cleaning projects.

### Substrate assessment

Before any cleaning method is specified, a suitably qualified conservator or specialist contractor should carry out a condition survey of the surface. This establishes the stone type and its current condition, identifies previous treatments or repairs, documents active defects such as spalling or open joints, and maps areas of differential soiling. The survey findings should drive the method statement, not confirm a method that was already chosen on other grounds.

### Trial panels

Even a correctly chosen method should be trialled on a discrete, representative area before being applied across the structure. The trial panel should be documented photographically and monitored over a period sufficient to observe any short-term surface response, typically a minimum of several weeks depending on conditions. The results, including any adverse effects observed, should be recorded in full and made available to the client and to the planning authority where required.

### What a method statement should contain

A method statement for heritage stone cleaning should address the cleaning method and any products or equipment to be used, the substrate assessment findings, the results of any trial panels undertaken, the COSHH assessment where chemicals or respirable dusts are involved, and details of operative training and relevant qualifications. Clients requesting method statements from contractors should expect all of these elements to be present and should treat the absence of any as a reason to ask questions before committing.
