The first 72 hours after a fire are disproportionately consequential. Decisions made — or deferred — in this window directly affect remediation scope, contractor liability, and what insurers will and will not cover. For estates managers, loss adjusters, and site managers, understanding the correct sequence matters as much as the remediation work itself.

## What Happens to a Building in the First 72 Hours?

Secondary damage begins before the smoke clears. Soot and combustion residues are acidic; on metal surfaces, corrosion starts within hours of exposure to humid air. Water used in firefighting saturates structural materials, encourages mould growth, and — if unextracted — leads to timber decay and persistent damp that is expensive to resolve months later.

This is not an argument for rushing. It is an argument for sequencing correctly, with the right professionals on site at the right time.

## Step One: Why Structural Sign-Off Cannot Be Skipped

No remediation contractor should enter a fire-damaged building until a structural engineer has assessed and confirmed safe access. This is not a formality — it is a legal baseline under the CDM Regulations 2015. The responsible person (typically the building owner or principal contractor) must ensure a safe place of work before any other trades are introduced.

The structural engineer's assessment will:

- Identify compromised load-bearing elements (steel members distorted by heat, concrete with spalling or delamination, roof structures at risk of collapse)
- Define safe working zones and exclusion areas
- Specify temporary propping required before other works proceed
- Confirm whether the building can be re-entered at all, or whether demolition is the appropriate route

Until this sign-off is in place, the remediation contractor's role is limited to external documentation: photographic evidence, scope assessment from safe vantage points, and preliminary planning. Any contractor who enters before structural clearance creates a safety risk and a liability exposure that may complicate the insurance claim.

## Step Two: Securing the Site

Once structural clearance is confirmed for safe zones, securing the building is the immediate priority. This means:

- Boarding or hoarding openings to prevent weather ingress and unauthorised access
- Temporary roofing where the structure permits and where the structural engineer confirms it is safe to install
- Fencing the perimeter to satisfy Health and Safety obligations and to protect an active evidence site for the insurer

The insurer's loss adjuster will need to inspect the building in its post-fire condition. Do not begin cleaning or demolishing internal surfaces before the adjuster has visited and agreed scope. Premature removal of damaged material can be interpreted as altering the evidence base, which creates complications in the claims process.

Co-ordinate with the loss adjuster before any significant intervention — standard procedure, but one that contractors under pressure to move quickly sometimes overlook.

## What Can Contractors Do Before the Adjuster Visits?

Stabilise, do not remediate. Actions generally acceptable in the interim include:

- Water extraction and structural drying, which is time-critical and loss adjusters typically expect to be underway promptly
- Installing dehumidification equipment
- Securing the building fabric against further weather damage

Actions that should wait for adjuster approval:

- Removing or demolishing any fire-damaged structural or decorative elements
- Beginning surface cleaning of soot, smoke residue, or heat-staining
- Disposing of any materials on site

This distinction matters. Water extraction reduces the overall claim value by preventing secondary damage — adjusters generally support this. Cleaning surfaces before photographic and scope evidence is captured can create disputes about what was actually damaged.

## Step Three: Water Extraction and Structural Drying

Firefighting water is often the single largest source of ongoing damage in industrial buildings. Thousands of litres can saturate floor slabs, insulation, cladding systems, and retained contents.

Structural drying is a specialist operation. Industrial dehumidifiers, air movers, and desiccant drying systems are deployed systematically, with moisture readings taken at regular intervals. The drying log becomes part of the claims documentation, demonstrating that secondary damage has been mitigated.

For industrial buildings with insulated metal panel (IMP) cladding, water ingress behind panels is a particular concern: it is not visible on the surface, it creates conditions for corrosion of the steel substructure, and it is easily missed if drying is handled superficially.

## Step Four: When Can Surface Treatment Begin?

Once the adjuster has visited, structural drying is underway, and scope has been agreed, initial surface treatment can begin. The objective at this stage is to halt further deterioration, not to return surfaces to their pre-loss condition.

### Soot and Smoke Residue

Soot is chemically aggressive. On steel, porous masonry, and concrete, it accelerates corrosion and staining if left in place. Priority surfaces for early treatment include:

- Exposed structural steelwork
- Masonry in contact with steel fixings or embedments
- Any surface that will be enclosed before final remediation — for example, roof steels being covered by replacement cladding

### Where Laser Cleaning Has a Role

For steel structures, heritage masonry, or architectural features where conventional wet chemical or abrasive methods would risk substrate damage or generate significant waste volumes, laser cleaning offers a targeted alternative. The dry process — no water, no solvents, no abrasive media — is relevant where waste disposal logistics are constrained, or where the substrate must be retained and precision matters.

Laser cleaning does not replace bulk demolition or heavy-duty extraction; it is most effective as a finishing and preservation tool where the substrate itself must not be disturbed. It is also relevant where COSHH obligations apply to abrasive or chemical methods and where minimising the waste stream is a project requirement.

### Asbestos and Multi-Trade Co-ordination

Where the building was constructed before 2000, an asbestos survey must be commissioned before remediation works proceed in any area where ACMs (asbestos-containing materials) may be present. This is a legal requirement under the Control of Asbestos Regulations 2012, not an optional precaution. Remediation cannot proceed in unsurveyed areas.

Where multiple trades are on site simultaneously, a clear programme and single point of co-ordination prevent duplication and protect the insurance evidence trail.

## How Do Early Decisions Affect the Insurance Schedule?

Insurers and loss adjusters operate on the principle of minimising the ultimate loss. They will generally support expenditure on drying and stabilisation that demonstrably reduces the final reinstatement cost. What creates friction is:

- Works that begin without adjuster approval, altering the scope baseline
- Poor documentation — no moisture logs, no photographic record of pre-cleaning condition
- Disputed causation, where it is unclear whether damage is fire-related or the result of delayed intervention

A well-managed first 72 hours produces a documented evidence trail that accelerates claims settlement. A poorly managed first 72 hours produces disputes, delays, and sometimes contested liability between the building owner, the remediation contractor, and the insurer.

The sequence requires discipline: structural assessment first, then secure the site, then extract water, then agree scope with the adjuster, then treat surfaces. The insurance schedule reflects how well that order was followed.
