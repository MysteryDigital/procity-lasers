Removing corrosion, mill scale, or failed coatings from structural steelwork in a workshop is a controlled exercise. Blast it, inspect it, coat it. In-situ work is different. The steel is integral to a structure that may be occupied, load-bearing, or inaccessible from below. The cleaning method has to fit around those realities, not the other way around.

This article covers the practical constraints that govern in-situ steel cleaning, how they affect the most common surface preparation methods, and where laser cleaning provides a credible alternative.

## What changes when steelwork cannot come off site?

Workshop abrasive blasting happens inside a contained booth. Grit, blast debris, and extracted fume are managed within the facility. The operative has floor-level access to every face of the component.

In-situ work removes all of those defaults. The steel is fixed in position—often overhead in a roof structure, at height on a bridge or gantry, or within a building that remains occupied. The operative must come to the steel, bring access equipment, create containment around the work area, and manage all waste without affecting the surrounding structure or the public beneath. That changes the cost, the programme, and the choice of method.

## What access do in-situ steel cleaning projects require?

The access requirement depends almost entirely on geometry and height. Common options include:

**Scaffolding** gives a stable working platform and can be adapted to form part of a containment structure, but erection and strike costs are significant on short-duration jobs.

**Mobile Elevated Work Platforms (MEWPs)** are faster to deploy but restrict repositioning and are unsuitable where ground conditions, overhead clearance, or building structure prevent outrigger use.

**Rope access** (IRATA-trained operatives) suits large open elevations—bridge steelwork, external columns—but limits the weight of equipment the operative can carry.

Working at Height Regulations 2005 require a documented hierarchy of controls, with collective protection preferred over personal fall protection. Any method statement for in-situ steel cleaning must address this; access is a principal scope item, not an afterthought.

## How does abrasive blasting perform in confined or occupied spaces?

Open-nozzle dry abrasive blasting is the standard method for achieving the high surface preparation grades—typically Sa 2.5 (near-white metal) to Sa 3 (white metal) under ISO 8501-1—required by corrosion protection systems specified in BS EN ISO 12944. In a workshop, achieving Sa 2.5 is routine. On site, three factors complicate it.

**Containment.** Blast media, rust particles, and dislodged coating fragments must be captured. Lead-based paints and industrial coatings may present a COSHH obligation that does not disappear because the work is happening in the open air. Full encapsulation—sheeted scaffolding with filtered extract and negative pressure—is the standard response, but it adds cost and programme time, and requires ongoing inspection for integrity.

**Occupancy.** Noise levels from conventional blasting equipment (typically 105–115 dB at source) are impractical in occupied buildings or near the public without hour restrictions, physical barriers, and acoustic monitoring. Many contracts impose work windows that compress the productive day.

**Waste disposal.** Spent grit and coating debris collected in-situ must be classified, containerised, and disposed of in accordance with waste regulations. Where hazardous coatings are present, disposal costs escalate substantially.

### Vacuum blasting and UHP water jetting

Vacuum blasting (abrasive blasting with an integrated recovery head) mitigates several of these issues. The blast head is pressed against the steel surface and spent media is recovered at source, significantly reducing airborne dust and the encapsulation requirement. However, vacuum blasting is slower than open-nozzle blasting, and complex sections—joints, web stiffeners, re-entrant angles—resist the flat-plate geometry the recovery head requires.

Ultra-high-pressure water jetting (UHP) avoids abrasive media entirely but introduces moisture into the surface, requiring careful management of flash-rusting before coatings are applied. The surface profile produced by UHP differs from abrasive blast and may not meet some coating manufacturers' adhesion specifications.

Hand and power tool cleaning (St 2, St 3 under ISO 8501-1) is an option for light preparation but does not achieve the surface profile or cleanliness required for high-build industrial coatings on heavily corroded sections.

## What surface preparation standards apply to structural steelwork?

The applicable surface preparation grade is almost always specified by the coating manufacturer and referenced in the corrosion protection system, typically drawn from BS EN ISO 12944-4. The most common specifications on structural steelwork are:

- **Sa 2.5 (near-white blast cleaning)** — required for high-performance coatings in industrial environments (corrosivity categories C3–C5 under ISO 12944).
- **Sa 2 (thorough blast cleaning)** — acceptable for moderate environments with standard paint systems.

The operative and the supervising engineer must inspect and agree the prepared surface against ISO 8501-1 visual reference photographs before coating begins. Any substitution of method—for example, UHP water jetting in place of abrasive blasting—should be confirmed with the coating supplier in advance, as it may affect warranty and performance claims.

## Where does laser cleaning change the in-situ picture?

Laser cleaning removes rust, mill scale, and degraded coatings through a photothermal and photomechanical process. A pulsed Nd:YAG laser (typically 1.064 µm wavelength) delivers energy preferentially absorbed by the contaminant rather than the steel beneath, ablating the corrosion layer without removing significant base metal. Several characteristics of the process are directly relevant in-situ.

**No blast media.** There is no abrasive to contain, collect, and dispose of. The principal waste stream is ablated particulate, managed by local extraction and high-efficiency filtration at the handpiece. COSHH obligations remain—the fume stream must be assessed and controlled, particularly where lead-containing coatings are present—but the containment requirement is proportionally smaller than for open-nozzle or vacuum blasting.

**Low noise.** Laser cleaning equipment operates at a fraction of the acoustic output of abrasive blast equipment. This makes it viable in occupied buildings and within restricted working-hour windows where blasting would not be permitted.

**Access to complex geometry.** The laser handpiece is compact and handheld. Joints, web stiffeners, connection plates, and re-entrant angles that resist the geometry of a vacuum blast head can be addressed without adapting technique or losing cleaning quality.

**Operator safety.** Laser cleaning devices are Class 4 under BS EN 60825-1. Operatives must be trained, appropriate barriers in position, and wavelength-specific eye protection worn. These are managed obligations that must appear in the method statement.

**Throughput.** Laser cleaning is more time-intensive than open-nozzle blasting across large areas. For in-situ maintenance—where the practical comparison is with vacuum blasting or UHP rather than open-nozzle blast—the gap in output rate narrows considerably.

Achieving Sa 2.5 equivalence by laser is possible with correct calibration of fluence and dwell time. Coating specifications should be confirmed with the manufacturer before substituting laser for abrasive blast, as the resulting surface profile differs.

## Is laser cleaning suitable for your in-situ steel project?

Laser cleaning is not the right answer for every in-situ steel project. On large, accessible elevations where encapsulation is cost-effective and blasting is permitted, conventional methods remain efficient. Where access is restricted, occupancy prevents blasting, sections are geometrically complex, or hazardous coating waste makes conventional preparation costly, laser cleaning is worth evaluating as part of method selection.
