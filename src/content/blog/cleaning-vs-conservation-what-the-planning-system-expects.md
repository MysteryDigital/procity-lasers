## Why the Planning System Draws a Line Between Cleaning and Conservation

The National Planning Policy Framework (NPPF) requires local planning authorities to protect designated heritage assets from harm. For most procurement officers or site managers, “harm” conjures images of demolition or unsympathetic extensions. Surface cleaning barely registers as a concern.

That assumption is where problems begin.

Historic England’s guidance documents — in particular *Conservation Principles, Policies and Guidance* — treat the fabric of a building as a primary source of historical evidence. Every layer of paint, weathering crust, or biological growth is, from a conservation standpoint, part of the asset’s history. Removal is not automatically acceptable simply because the material removed is a contaminant.

This does not mean cleaning is prohibited. It means the planning system expects cleaning decisions to be made deliberately, on the basis of evidence, and with outcomes proportionate to the significance of what is being treated.

## What Does “Minimum Intervention” Mean in Practice?

Minimum intervention is not a specific statutory term, but it is embedded throughout Historic England’s guidance as a governing principle. In practice it means:

- Do the least that is necessary to achieve the conservation aim.
- Prefer reversible or retreatable methods over irreversible ones.
- Retain as much original fabric as possible, including patina where it forms part of the asset’s character.

For surface cleaning, this principle has direct consequences. The aim of cleaning should be to remove a specific, defined contaminant — graffiti, biological growth, fire soot, industrial soiling — rather than to restore an assumed original appearance. Cleaning to an imagined “clean” baseline is not a conservation objective and is unlikely to satisfy a conservation officer.

The practical test: can you define precisely what you are removing, why it is harmful, and what you will leave behind? If the answer is yes, and your method can achieve that outcome with minimal risk to substrate integrity, you are working within the spirit of minimum intervention.

## When Does Cleaning Require Listed Building Consent?

This is the question that catches contractors and building owners off guard.

Listed Building Consent is required for any works that would affect the character of a listed building as a building of special architectural or historic interest. The threshold is not restricted to structural alterations. Under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990, “alteration” is interpreted broadly by local planning authorities and the courts.

A cleaning method that causes physical change to the substrate — removing protective patina, opening the surface structure of stonework, bleaching or etching decorative elements — may constitute an alteration requiring consent, even if the intention was simply to clean.

The determining factors tend to include:

- **Significance of the surface**: A Victorian encaustic tile floor carries more significance than a modern render patch. The importance of the element within the wider asset matters.
- **Reversibility**: Can the change to substrate condition be undone if it proves problematic? Irreversible surface alteration is treated more seriously than retreatable intervention.
- **Visibility**: Changes to principal elevations are scrutinised more closely than secondary or service areas.
- **Method aggressiveness**: Research documented by buildingconservation.com confirms that abrasive blasting causes substrate erosion even at low pressure. That erosion may constitute an “alteration” in the eyes of a conservation officer.

If there is any doubt, the correct course is to contact the local authority’s conservation officer before work begins and seek pre-application advice. Proceeding without that conversation — and without consent where one is required — creates enforcement risk that no insurance policy or project programme can easily absorb.

## What Does a Compliant Method Statement Need to Cover?

A method statement is the primary document by which a contractor demonstrates that their proposed works comply with heritage obligations. Conservation officers, local planning authorities, and heritage bodies use method statements to assess whether to grant consent or discharge planning conditions.

A method statement that simply states “laser cleaning, no damage to substrate” is not adequate. A compliant document should address:

### Significance assessment

A brief summary of the heritage significance of the surface to be treated, drawing on the listed building description, any existing conservation management plans, and a visual inspection report. This demonstrates that the contractor understands what they are protecting, not just what they are removing.

### Defined scope

Precisely what contaminant is being removed, from which surfaces, and what the physical limits of the works are. This prevents scope creep during works and provides a clear benchmark for sign-off.

### Method rationale

Why this cleaning method was selected, with reference to the alternatives considered and why they were rejected. For laser cleaning, this should explain the principle of selective absorption — that laser energy is absorbed preferentially by the contaminant layer rather than the substrate beneath — and the parameters to be used, including fluence settings and pulse duration.

### Pre-treatment trials

A description of the test patch programme to be carried out before full works commence. Test patches on an inconspicuous area of the same substrate establish calibration settings and provide visual evidence of outcomes. They are standard practice on any heritage cleaning project and are expected by conservation officers as evidence of due diligence.

### Monitoring and contingency

How substrate condition will be monitored during works, what triggers a pause in operations, and what the contingency plan is if an unexpected response occurs. This demonstrates that the contractor is treating the work as a managed process rather than a production exercise.

### Health, safety and environmental controls

Laser ablation of contaminated surfaces produces a particulate fume stream. Under COSHH, that fume stream must be assessed and appropriate controls put in place. The method statement should confirm that a COSHH assessment exists for the specific contaminant profile and that fume extraction or appropriate PPE is in place. Laser equipment operating at the powers required for building cleaning is Class 4; compliance with BS EN 60825-1 and a documented laser safety protocol are both required.

## Does the Cleaning Method Matter, or Just the Outcome?

Both. Historic England’s guidance consistently favours approaches that can be adjusted, repeated, or refined in response to substrate behaviour. Irreversible damage caused in the first pass cannot be recovered. Methods that allow the operator to modulate energy delivery and halt work without cumulative substrate harm are better aligned with minimum intervention principles than single-pass abrasive or chemical approaches.

That said, method alone does not guarantee compliance. An operator who has not calibrated settings correctly for the specific substrate, or who has not carried out pre-treatment trials, can still cause harm with any cleaning technique. The planning system is concerned with outcomes. A method statement that demonstrates competent process, tested parameters, and documented monitoring is the evidence base that shows proposed works will achieve acceptable results.

Cleaning on designated heritage assets is not simply a maintenance decision. It is a conservation decision, with the consent implications that follow from that.
