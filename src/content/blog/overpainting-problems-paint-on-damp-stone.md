Paint removal is rarely just about removing paint. A significant proportion of remediation jobs arise not from deliberate graffiti or accumulated grime, but from paint that was applied to masonry in the wrong conditions. When moisture is trapped beneath a coating, or when soluble salts are present in the substrate, failure is not a matter of if — it is a matter of when. Understanding the mechanisms behind that failure is essential for anyone specifying removal works on stone or brick buildings.

## Why Does Moisture Beneath a Coating Cause Failure?

Stone is porous. Moisture moves through masonry continuously, driven by rain, rising damp, condensation, and seasonal thermal cycling. When a coating is applied before the substrate has dried sufficiently, that moisture has nowhere to go.

As trapped water vapour migrates outward — following vapour pressure gradients — it accumulates at the interface between stone and paint film. In impermeable coatings (conventional alkyd or acrylic masonry paints, for instance), the film cannot breathe. Pressure builds and adhesion fails. The result is blistering, peeling, and flaking that often emerges within the first winter following application.

This failure mode — sometimes called vapour-pressure delamination — is particularly common on:

- External walls repainted too soon after prolonged rain or following damp remediation
- North-facing elevations that dry slowly between wet spells
- Substrates with persistent rising damp that was not resolved before painting
- Buildings where a new impermeable coating was applied over an older, already-failing one

The surface deterioration looks like straightforward paint failure. The root cause is a moisture management problem. Repainting without resolving the underlying damp will produce the same outcome within a few years.

## What Is Cryptoflorescence and Why Is It Damaging?

Salts present within masonry introduce a related but distinct failure mechanism. Stone and brick absorb soluble salts from multiple sources: groundwater, de-icing salts, historic lime mortars, atmospheric pollution, and previous remediation products. When moisture moves through contaminated stone and then evaporates, it deposits those salts.

If evaporation occurs at the masonry face, the result is visible efflorescence — white crystalline deposits on the surface. Efflorescence is unsightly but relatively benign; the salts are accessible and can be brushed away.

Cryptoflorescence is the more destructive variant. Where an impermeable coating is present, evaporation cannot occur at the masonry surface. Moisture carrying dissolved salts travels to just beneath the coating, where it evaporates. The salts crystallise in the narrow zone between stone and paint film, or within the outermost pores of the stone itself. Crystal growth generates considerable mechanical pressure — sufficient to fracture stone faces and spall aggregate from brick.

The visible symptom is a paint film that appears to be forced off the wall rather than simply peeling. Blistering tends to be hard-edged and irregular. On sandstone and softer limestone, removal of the coating can reveal stone face loss and a friable, powdering surface beneath.

## How Do You Identify These Failure Patterns on an Existing Building?

Correct diagnosis before specifying removal is not optional — the condition of the substrate beneath the coating determines which method is appropriate and whether repair work is also required.

### Signs of vapour-pressure delamination

- Blistering concentrated in areas that correspond to known damp sources: at ground level, below window sills, at parapet junctions
- Peeling that initiates at joints, edges, and laps where moisture ingress is highest
- Intact paint in sheltered or south-facing areas combined with pronounced failure on exposed elevations

### Signs of cryptoflorescence

- Hard blisters with a gritty or powdery texture when pressed
- White or rust-coloured staining visible at blister margins, where salts have leached to the edge of the coating
- When blisters are opened, the stone face may be absent or powdering, with the coating carrying fragments of substrate rather than releasing cleanly
- Irregular map-cracking of the paint film where salt crystal expansion has stressed the coating from below

A moisture reading taken through intact paint using a carbide hygrometer on exposed sections of stone will help establish whether active damp is present. Readings above approximately 5–6% moisture content by weight in the substrate indicate conditions that would not have been suitable for painting.

Salt content can be assessed using a conductivity meter or by submitting stone samples for laboratory testing. Confirming the presence and concentration of soluble salts directly affects removal planning, particularly where specialist conservation or heritage buildings are involved.

## What Does Removal Require When the Substrate Is Already Compromised?

When a coating has been applied to damp or salt-contaminated stone, removal is more involved than standard paint stripping. The substrate may be weakened, and the wrong method will accelerate damage rather than remedy it.

**Abrasive methods** — pressure washing, grit blasting, and rotary tools — carry significant risk where the stone face is already friable or salt-damaged. Abrasive blasting has been documented as damaging masonry even at low pressures. On porous or degraded stone, the risk is substantially higher. These methods cannot discriminate between coating and substrate at the point of contact, which means material loss is probable wherever the stone surface is compromised.

**Chemical stripping** introduces additional liquid into a substrate that may already have a moisture problem. Poultice-based systems can be used carefully, but they require thorough neutralisation and rinsing. On salt-contaminated substrates, some stripping agents can mobilise additional salts from within the stone if dwell times are not controlled.

**Laser removal** operates differently where substrate condition is a concern. The photothermal process removes the coating by selective light absorption — the paint absorbs the laser energy and ablates, while the stone beneath reflects or dissipates that energy. There is no mechanical abrasion and no additional liquid is introduced into the substrate. This makes the method particularly relevant where the stone face is already weakened and cannot tolerate physical or hydraulic stress.

Calibration remains essential. Fluence settings must be appropriate for the specific substrate: excessive energy on weakened stone can cause surface damage. A competent operator will assess substrate condition, run calibration passes at conservative settings, and adjust before committing to full coverage.

Where the stone face has already been lost to cryptoflorescence damage, coating removal reveals a repair requirement rather than a clean surface. Lime-based consolidants, micro-repair mortars, or specialist surface treatments may be needed before any new protective coating is considered — and only after the moisture and salt conditions have been resolved at source. Applying a new coating to an unresolved substrate simply restarts the same failure cycle.

## Is Laser Removal Appropriate for Every Case?

Laser removal is well-suited to compromised substrates, but it is not the only consideration. Site access, coating thickness, the extent of substrate damage, and any Listed Building or conservation area obligations all affect the specification. On scheduled or listed structures, a conservation officer should be involved in agreeing the method and test area approach before works begin.
