## Understanding Fire Damage to Masonry

Fire leaves a layered deposit on masonry surfaces. The visible discolouration is typically a combination of carbon particulates, vaporised polymers from burned materials such as furniture, fittings, and cladding, and residual combustion by-products absorbed into the substrate. On porous stone, brick, or concrete, these residues can penetrate several millimetres below the surface, making simple surface washing ineffective.

The severity and character of the deposit depends on the type of fire, the materials involved, and the masonry substrate. A structure fire involving plastics and synthetic materials produces a particularly tenacious, adhesive soot profile compared with one involving predominantly organic material. Flame-impinged zones close to the seat of the fire will also show thermal alteration of the substrate: spalling, micro-fracturing, colour change, and, in limestone or marble, partial calcination.

Loss adjusters and specifying contractors need to distinguish between superficial surface soiling and deeper substrate compromise. Laser cleaning addresses the former with precision; the latter requires structural assessment before any cleaning scope is written.

## Why Conventional Methods Create Secondary Losses

The standard remediation toolkit for fire-damaged masonry, including pressure washing, abrasive blasting, and chemical treatment, carries risks that can compound the original loss.

Pressure washing drives water-borne residues deeper into porous stone or brick. On buildings with compromised pointing or existing moisture ingress, this creates conditions for freeze-thaw damage and subsequent mortar deterioration. On historic or decoratively carved masonry, the water pressure alone can cause surface erosion that is unrecoverable.

Abrasive blasting removes material indiscriminately. It is effective at eliminating surface deposits but takes substrate material with it. On softer sandstones, limestones, or render, this means measurable surface loss. On listed or heritage structures, abrasive methods are generally incompatible with conservation requirements under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990 and the associated Historic England guidance on cleaning stonework.

Chemical cleaning introduces solvent or alkaline residues into the masonry. Incomplete neutralisation or rinsing can lead to salt migration and efflorescence over subsequent months, presenting as a new defect on the insurer's asset and creating potential for reinstatement dispute.

Each of these failure modes has the potential to generate a secondary claim. Specifying a cleaning method that avoids them is a practical procurement decision, not an academic one.

## How Laser Cleaning Works

Laser cleaning uses short pulses of concentrated light energy to ablate surface contaminants without contacting the substrate directly. The principle relies on a differential in light absorption: carbon-based fire residues absorb laser energy at considerably higher rates than clean stone, brick, or concrete. At calibrated power levels, the residue is vaporised and ejected from the surface while the underlying substrate absorbs insufficient energy to cause alteration.

The operator adjusts pulse duration, energy density, and spot diameter to suit the substrate and the deposit profile. This calibration is established through test patches at the start of each project, and the test patch records form part of the method statement documentation.

### Substrates Suitable for Laser Cleaning After Fire Damage

Laser cleaning is applicable across a wide range of masonry types: sandstone, limestone, granite, brick, terracotta, concrete, and render. Performance varies by substrate porosity, surface condition, and deposit thickness. Natural stone with existing micro-fractures from thermal shock may require lower energy settings to avoid propagating those fractures during ablation; a competent operator will identify this during test patching.

Heavily painted or coated surfaces affected by fire introduce additional complexity. The paint layer may itself be a hazardous material, as lead-based paint was common in UK construction until the 1960s. Its removal requires a risk assessment under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 before any cleaning work commences.

### What Laser Cleaning Cannot Do

Laser cleaning removes surface and near-surface deposits. It does not reverse thermal alteration of the substrate itself. Spalled or calcined stone remains structurally compromised after cleaning; the process reveals that condition by removing the overlying soot. This distinction is important for scope-writing: a cleaned surface may still require consolidation treatment, repair, or unit replacement, and these are separate scopes that should not be conflated in a single line item.

## Documentation and the Insurance Context

For insurers and loss adjusters, the practical value of laser cleaning extends beyond the cleaning outcome. The method generates a defensible audit trail that supports reinstatement valuation and reduces the likelihood of scope disputes.

Before work begins, the contractor should produce a method statement covering laser parameters, a risk assessment including fume extraction provisions (which are mandatory during ablation of fire residues under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002), and a photographic record of the pre-cleaning condition. Fume extraction is not optional: fire residues contain combustion by-products that become airborne during ablation, and site welfare provisions must reflect this.

This documentation also supports the specifying surveyor in demonstrating that the chosen method was appropriate to the substrate. On listed buildings or structures within conservation areas, the local planning authority or Historic England may require this evidence as a condition of any consent obtained for the cleaning work.

### Scope Writing Guidance for Appointed Contractors

When writing a scope of works that includes laser cleaning of fire-damaged masonry, the following items should be addressed explicitly:

1. Substrate type and pre-fire condition, including any known vulnerabilities or previous repairs.
2. Nature and depth of the fire deposit, and whether painted or coated surfaces are involved.
3. Hazardous materials assessment, particularly for lead paint or asbestos-containing materials in buildings of appropriate age.
4. Access method and how it sequences with the cleaning programme.
5. Fume extraction requirements, site welfare provisions, and waste disposal arrangements for ablated material.

Addressing these points in advance reduces the likelihood of variation orders and delays once work is on site. A contractor who cannot respond to each of them in their tender return is worth querying before appointment.
