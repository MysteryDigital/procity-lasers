Most coating failures are not coating failures. They are substrate preparation failures. The coating performs as specified — but the surface it was applied to was not clean enough, not profiled correctly, or harboured contamination that the preparation process failed to address.

Three contamination types account for the majority of adhesion failures on structural and industrial steelwork: soluble salts, oil and grease, and mill scale or surface oxides. Each operates by a different mechanism. Each requires a different preparation response. Missing any one of them produces a distinct failure pattern, but the outcome is the same — premature coating breakdown, accelerating corrosion, and a remediation bill that dwarfs the cost of getting preparation right in the first place.

## Why Does Surface Contamination Prevent Adhesion?

Adhesion depends on intimate contact between a coating film and a clean, profiled substrate. Contamination between the two creates either a physical barrier, a chemical disruption, or a subsequent reaction that lifts the coating from below.

Specifying a higher-performance coating does not compensate for inadequate preparation. It increases material cost without resolving the underlying problem.

## What Are the Three Main Contamination Types?

### Soluble Salts

Soluble salts — principally chlorides, sulphates, and nitrates — are invisible to the naked eye and will not be removed by abrasive blasting. They originate from marine environments, road de-icing products, industrial atmospheric fallout, and from corrosion pits in previously rusted steel.

The failure mechanism is osmotic blistering. All coating films are semi-permeable to water vapour. Where salts are present at the coating/substrate interface, they draw moisture through the film by osmosis, building localised pressure until the film blisters and ruptures. Once the film is broken, moisture and oxygen access the steel directly and corrosion accelerates beneath the surrounding coating.

Assessment is carried out using ISO 8502-6 (the Bresle patch extraction method) or ISO 8502-9 (field conductivity measurement). For most structural steelwork in moderate environments, the accepted threshold is 20 mg/m² equivalent NaCl. For aggressive environments or immersion service, lower limits apply. The coating manufacturer's specification will state the required limit for the system in question.

Where salt contamination is present, the surface must be washed with clean water prior to or following blast cleaning, then retested to confirm levels are within the acceptable range. Abrasive blasting alone is insufficient — it can drive salts deeper into the anchor profile rather than removing them.

### Oil and Grease

Organic contamination — cutting oil, hydraulic fluid, forming lubricant, or general handling grease — acts as a physical release agent. A coating cannot wet a surface contaminated with oil and cannot form a mechanical or chemical bond with the substrate. Adhesion failure caused by oil contamination is typically visible early in service: the coating peels cleanly from the surface in sheets, leaving little or no coating residue on the steel.

The critical sequence rule is degrease before blasting, not after. Blasting an oily surface spreads the contamination and embeds it in the anchor profile. Subsequent solvent cleaning cannot fully reverse this.

SSPC-SP 1 (Solvent Cleaning) is the standard covering removal of oil and grease contamination before surface preparation proceeds. Assessment involves visual inspection for sheen and, where required, surface wipe testing. This step is frequently omitted or abbreviated on site under scheduling pressure, which is why organic residue contamination is among the more common causes of early coating failure on industrial structures.

### Mill Scale and Surface Oxides

Mill scale is the blue-grey iron oxide layer that forms on the surface of hot-rolled steel during manufacture. It appears uniform and tightly bonded when the steel is new, but it is cathodic relative to the underlying base metal. Where mill scale breaks down, the exposed steel becomes the anode in a galvanic cell and corrodes preferentially.

When a coating is applied over intact mill scale, it bonds to the scale rather than to the steel. Mill scale breaks down over time as a result of differential thermal expansion, mechanical stress, and moisture ingress at surface discontinuities. As it does, corrosion develops at the scale/steel interface, the scale undercuts, and the coating lifts with it across a progressively wider area.

The preparation requirement is unambiguous: mill scale must be removed entirely before coating. ISO 8501-1 defines the visual cleanliness grades achieved by blast cleaning. Sa 2.5 (near-white blast cleaning) is the minimum requirement for most protective coating systems. Sa 3 (white metal) is specified for aggressive service conditions and immersion environments. Both grades are assessed visually using reference comparator panels, and inspection records should be documented and retained.

Surface oxides from previous corrosion — rust and iron hydroxide deposits — present the same problem. They are weakly bonded, have low cohesive strength, and must be removed regardless of how stable they appear. Applying a coating over rust, however thin, is not a preparation strategy.

## What Cleanliness Grade and Surface Profile Are Required?

The required cleanliness grade is defined by the coating specification, not by what is achievable on a given day. Applying a coating system to a lower preparation grade than the one the manufacturer specifies voids any performance guarantee and, in most contracts, transfers liability to the applicator. Recoating contracts routinely specify ISO 8501-1 grades and require documentary inspection records as a condition of sign-off.

Surface profile — the anchor pattern produced by blast cleaning — is a parallel requirement that is too often treated as secondary. ISO 8503 profile measurement, using a comparator or contact profilometer, should accompany every cleanliness inspection. Most two-coat epoxy systems require 40–75 microns Rz; zinc-rich primers typically call for the upper end of that range. A clean surface with insufficient profile provides inadequate mechanical key. A well-profiled surface that is contaminated allows the profile to trap rather than remove the contaminant, compounding the problem rather than resolving it.

Both requirements must be met simultaneously. Meeting one without the other is not an acceptable result.

## How Does Laser Cleaning Address These Failure Modes?

Laser surface preparation is used in industrial recoating projects where conventional methods present practical constraints — restricted access, confined spaces, prohibition on solvent use, or the need to minimise substrate damage.

For salt contamination on corroded steel, laser ablation removes corrosion products without driving salts deeper into the surface profile, which matters where repeated blast-and-test cycles would otherwise be required. For organic contamination, the laser vaporises the material rather than redistributing it across the substrate, eliminating the spread risk associated with blasting over oil. For mill scale and surface oxides, appropriate laser parameters achieve Sa 2 to Sa 2.5 cleanliness on steel, with the surface profile produced depending on the specific material condition and settings used.

Throughput is lower than abrasive blasting on large open surfaces. Laser preparation is best suited to precision work, areas where abrasive containment is not feasible, and substrates where controlled material removal is a priority.
