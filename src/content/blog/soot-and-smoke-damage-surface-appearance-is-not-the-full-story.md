When a loss adjuster, building surveyor, or contracts manager walks into a fire-damaged structure, the extent of the problem seems self-evident: blackened surfaces, heavy residue deposits, the acrid smell of combustion. Visual assessment appears to give a clear picture of scope. The difficulty is that what can be seen represents only a fraction of what is present.

Smoke is not a surface phenomenon. It is a penetrating aerosol, and the materials most commonly found in the structures you are managing, both old and new, are porous enough to absorb it at depth. Specifying remediation based on visible staining alone means specifying against a partial picture.

## What Smoke Actually Is

Before addressing remediation, it helps to be precise about the material being removed. Smoke is not simply black carbon. It is a complex aerosol mixture containing particulate carbon, condensed organic compounds, acids, oils, water vapour, and trace heavy metals depending on what burned. In fires involving modern building contents, this mixture includes residues from synthetic furnishings, cabling, plastics, and treated timbers, none of which burn cleanly.

The particulate fraction of smoke is extremely fine. Particles range from sub-micron scale up to coarser visible deposits, and the finest components travel with air pressure differentials through openings invisible to the naked eye: mortar joints, masonry pores, hairline cracks in render, the grain structure of timber, and the micro-channels within natural stone.

The result is a layered contamination profile. There is a visible surface deposit, typically greasy and acidic, that conventional cleaning methods address. Below it, there is a zone of penetrated contamination where particles and organic residues have been drawn into the substrate itself.

## Why Surface Appearance Is Misleading

### The Depth Problem

The depth to which smoke penetrates depends on several variables: the intensity and duration of the fire, the proximity of the material to the source, the porosity and density of the substrate, and whether the building was under positive or negative air pressure relative to adjoining spaces during the fire.

A wall that appears only lightly stained on the surface may have drawn smoke deeply into its substrate over the course of a slow, smouldering fire. A surface with heavy visible deposit from a fast, hot fire may conversely have a relatively shallow penetration profile, because the thermal updraft kept smoke moving rather than allowing it to settle and permeate.

This distinction matters at the specification stage. Treating a shallow-penetration, heavy-deposit problem is a different scope of works from treating a deep-penetration, lighter-surface problem, and the methods that address one may not resolve the other.

### Odour Persistence as a Diagnostic Indicator

One of the most reliable early indicators of sub-surface contamination is odour persistence. If a space continues to smell of smoke following surface cleaning, the residue causing that odour is not at the surface. Heat accelerates outgassing of sub-surface contaminants, which is why buildings that appear satisfactory in cooler months often present renewed odour complaints in warm weather.

Project managers and loss adjusters specifying reinstatement works should treat persistent odour as a direct indicator that surface treatment has not resolved the full depth of contamination.

## Substrates and Their Behaviour

### Masonry and Natural Stone

Brick, sandstone, limestone, and concrete all absorb smoke contamination, but their behaviour differs. Sandstone and limestone are comparatively soft and porous, particularly the older, hand-cut stone found in pre-1900 buildings. Smoke penetration in these materials can extend several millimetres into the face, and the acidic components of smoke residue will continue to react with calcium carbonate in the stone over time if left in situ.

Dense engineering brick presents a different profile: lower individual absorption, but smoke residue that has penetrated mortar joints may be distributed across a wide area even where individual brick faces appear relatively unaffected.

### Plaster and Render

Traditional lime plaster is highly porous and will absorb smoke throughout its thickness. Modern gypsum plaster is less porous but remains susceptible to staining and contamination, particularly where the fire produced significant moisture as a combustion byproduct. Where plaster is historically significant or forms part of a listed interior, replacement is not always an appropriate option, and treatment requires careful method selection.

### Structural Timber

Timber presents a particular challenge because smoke penetrates along the grain, sometimes considerably, and the organic residues left in wood interact with moisture over time, contributing to discolouration, persistent odour, and in some cases conditions that favour fungal growth. Charred timber that is structurally sound may still carry deep contamination that is not apparent from surface inspection, and this is frequently underestimated in early scope assessments.

## What a Pre-Works Assessment Should Establish

### Substrate Mapping and Contamination Depth

Before any remediation method is specified, the substrate types present should be established, including their approximate age, condition, and conservation status. A proper assessment should then attempt to characterise both the depth of penetration in representative areas and the distribution of contamination, including spaces outside the obvious fire zone that may have received smoke via ductwork, roof voids, or pressure differentials.

A blanket method applied uniformly across a mixed-substrate area is unlikely to be optimal for any of them.

### Conservation and Listed Building Obligations

Where the affected building is listed or situated within a conservation area, works affecting the fabric require appropriate consent under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990. This includes cleaning and remediation, not only structural intervention. The method specified must be demonstrably appropriate to the significance of the material and, where reversibility is relevant, this should be documented in the method statement submitted to the conservation officer.

Soot and acidic smoke residues are themselves damaging to historic fabric, so inaction is not a neutral position. The case for intervention is generally strong, but the method must be justified and evidenced.

### Health and Safety Obligations

Soot from fires involving synthetic materials can contain carcinogenic compounds. The Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 require that before remediation begins, the substances present are properly assessed and adequate controls are in place, including appropriate respiratory protection for operatives and, depending on the contamination profile, environmental controls to prevent secondary spread.

## Matching Method to What Is Actually Present

The range of available remediation methods includes pressure washing, abrasive cleaning, chemical treatment, and dry-ice or laser-based systems. No single method is appropriate for all substrates and all contamination profiles.

Laser cleaning offers precise control over energy delivery and has an established track record on heritage stone and sensitive masonry. Because it ablates contamination at or near the surface without introducing moisture or abrasive media, it is well suited to situations where substrate preservation is a priority, or where conventional methods have not fully resolved the problem after an initial attempt.

The key principle is that method selection should follow assessment, not precede it. Specifying a cleaning approach before the depth of contamination and the substrate profile are understood risks either under-treating the problem or applying a method that causes unnecessary damage to materials deserving a more considered approach.
