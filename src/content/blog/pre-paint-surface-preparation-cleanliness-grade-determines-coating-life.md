## The Cleanliness Grade Is a Formal Specification

For most coating systems applied in industrial, structural, or protective contexts, the quality of adhesion is determined before a single coat of paint touches the substrate. The cleanliness grade defines how completely a surface must be freed from contamination, rust, mill scale, and prior coating residues before any coating system is applied. It is not an aspiration: it is the baseline condition on which coating system warranties, engineering calculations, and corrosivity category specifications all depend.

The principal reference standard for visual assessment of steel surface cleanliness is ISO 8501-1. For abrasive blast cleaning, it defines a progression of grades: Sa 1 covers light blast cleaning that removes loosely adhering material; Sa 2 requires thorough removal of most rust and scale; Sa 2.5 specifies very thorough removal, permitting only slight discolouration on the prepared surface; and Sa 3 requires blast cleaning to visually clean steel with a uniform metallic colour. For hand-tool and power-tool preparation, the equivalent St grades apply, though these achieve a fundamentally lower level of cleanliness and are unsuitable for aggressive environments or most modern high-performance coating systems.

Specifiers working to BS EN ISO 12944, which covers corrosion protection of steel structures by protective paint systems, will be familiar with the way corrosivity categories map to required preparation grades and minimum coating thicknesses. The link is direct: the specifying engineer selects a target service life and a corrosivity category, and the standard then constrains both the coating system and the minimum acceptable surface preparation grade. Relaxing the preparation grade without also selecting a lower-performance coating system is not a legitimate substitution.

## Why Does Residual Contamination Cause Coating Failure?

### Adhesion and the role of the interface

Protective coatings rely on mechanical and chemical adhesion to the substrate. Both mechanisms are compromised by contamination at the interface. Oil and grease prevent the coating from achieving intimate contact with the steel surface, leaving unbonded areas that allow moisture ingress from the margins inward. Residual rust and mill scale present a more subtle problem: the coating may bond adequately to the contamination layer while that layer is itself only weakly attached to the parent metal. When the contamination layer fails, the coating lifts with it.

Mill scale, which forms on the surface of structural steel during hot rolling, appears initially to offer a stable substrate. However, it is cathodic relative to the steel beneath it. Once moisture reaches the interface through any defect in the coating, the electrochemical potential difference between mill scale and substrate drives corrosion beneath the coating film. The corrosion proceeds from the interface, often invisibly, until the coating delaminates across a wide area.

### Soluble salts and osmotic blistering

Chloride contamination is one of the most frequently underestimated risks in surface preparation, particularly on structures that have been exposed to marine environments, coastal weather, or de-icing salts. The problem is that chloride ions are not reliably removed by abrasive blasting if they have penetrated into the steel surface profile or into pitting from prior corrosion. Applying a coating over a chloride-contaminated surface creates conditions for osmotic blistering: the salt beneath the film draws moisture through the coating by osmotic pressure, and that pressure is sufficient to disbond the coating at the interface, producing blisters that originate from below rather than from any visible surface defect.

A surface can achieve the visual grade Sa 2.5 while still carrying a chloride burden that will cause premature failure. ISO 8502 provides methods for testing soluble salt levels on prepared surfaces, and where contamination risk is elevated, quantitative testing before coating application is the appropriate response. Visual cleanliness assessment alone is insufficient verification in these circumstances.

## What Is the Cost of Getting the Preparation Grade Wrong?

The relationship between preparation grade and coating life is not linear, but the variation is substantial enough to make under-preparation a significant financial risk rather than a minor technical shortcut.

Coating manufacturers issue performance data and warranties against specific preparation grades. A system specified and warranted at Sa 2.5 will typically not carry the same warranty, or any warranty, if applied to an Sa 2 surface. The coating material cost does not change with the preparation grade. The service life does.

When a coating fails prematurely due to inadequate surface preparation, the remediation cost is not equivalent to the original coating cost. In occupied or operational environments, access alone can cost considerably more than the surface preparation and coating work combined. If the failure has allowed corrosion to progress to the structural element, the cost escalates further. The false economy in cutting surface preparation standards is well understood in corrosion engineering but is still routinely reproduced in projects where preparation is managed as a cost-control variable rather than a quality input.

### Where specification creep tends to occur

Under-preparation is rarely the result of a single explicit decision. More commonly it is the product of accumulated compromises: programme pressure, restricted access preventing complete blast coverage, wet weather creating a gap between preparation and first coat application, or abrasive media used beyond its effective working life. Each may appear manageable in isolation; combined, they can reduce the achieved preparation grade significantly below the specified grade without any single decision being formally identified as non-compliant.

Verification is therefore as important as specification. Visual assessment to ISO 8501-1 is subjective, and in practice it should be supported by surface profile measurement to ISO 8503 where the coating system requires a specific anchor profile, and by soluble salt quantification where the contamination history warrants it. The achieved condition should be documented before coating commences, not assumed.

## Laser Cleaning in Surface Preparation Contracts

Laser cleaning removes surface contamination by ablating the target layer with concentrated optical energy, with parameters adjusted for the substrate type and contaminant. This allows controlled removal of rust, mill scale, previous coatings, and soluble salt-bearing corrosion products without imposing the mechanical impact load of blasting on the underlying substrate.

Because the process generates no secondary abrasive waste stream, it is applicable in situations where conventional blasting is impractical: enclosed or partially enclosed environments, substrates where dimensional accuracy must be maintained, and structures where abrasive contamination of adjacent plant or equipment is not acceptable. The process is also geometrically selective, making it suitable for complex profiles or detailed ironwork where consistent blast access cannot be achieved.

The cleanliness grades achievable by laser preparation are comparable to those achievable by abrasive blasting, and the outcomes are assessed and documented using the same ISO 8501-1 framework, so there is no ambiguity when the prepared surface is presented for inspection or handed over to the coating contractor.

## Starting from the Right Standard

Surface preparation cost is typically a small proportion of the total cost of a coating system over its full service life. The disproportionate influence that preparation quality has on coating longevity makes it one of the highest-leverage decisions in any painting or protective coating contract. Specifying the correct grade, verifying it before coating, and documenting the outcome protects all parties in the supply chain.
