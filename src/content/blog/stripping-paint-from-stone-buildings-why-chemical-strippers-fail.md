## Why Chemical Stripping Is the Default — and Why It Fails

When a contractor or estates manager faces painted stone — a listed façade coated during a past refurbishment, render on a sandstone church wall, or paint applied over Portland stone — the first instinct is often to reach for a proprietary chemical stripper. They are available off the shelf, relatively familiar, and appear cheaper than specialist alternatives when compared on a day-rate basis.

The problem is that porous natural stone behaves differently from timber, steel, or dense masonry. On those substrates, chemical stripping usually performs as intended. On open-textured stone, the same chemistry can cause damage that is expensive — and sometimes impossible — to reverse.

## How Do Chemical Strippers Work?

Chemical paint strippers rely on one of two primary mechanisms. Solvent-based products — including contemporary formulations based on NMP (N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone) or benzyl alcohol — swell and soften the paint film by penetrating the polymer matrix. Alkaline strippers, typically based on sodium or potassium hydroxide, break down the ester linkages in oil-based and alkyd paints through saponification, converting the binder to a soap-like material that lifts from the surface.

Both approaches work well on non-porous substrates where the chemical action stays at the interface between paint and substrate. The complication with porous stone is that this interface is not a clean line.

## Why Does Porous Stone Make Chemical Stripping Dangerous?

Natural stone — sandstone, limestone, granite, and most historic ashlar — is shot through with micro-pores and capillary channels. The porosity of UK sandstones can reach 20–30% by volume. Limestone values of 10–20% are common in English building stones, and Portland stone has been measured at around 15–18%.

When a liquid chemical stripper is applied and left to dwell, capillary action draws it inward along those channels. The stripper does not remain at the paint–stone boundary. It penetrates.

### Solvent Migration and Pigment Embedding

With solvent-based formulations, this migration carries dissolved paint pigments, binders, and plasticisers into the stone. As the solvent evaporates — from the surface first, then from progressively deeper layers — it draws material outward, but not always cleanly. Pigments, particularly iron oxide reds, yellows, and carbon blacks, tend to bind to the mineral matrix of the stone. Once embedded at depth, they are effectively part of the substrate and no subsequent chemical treatment will extract them without introducing further risk.

The result is a substrate that appears clean on the surface but carries a subsurface shadow that becomes more visible as the stone weathers and the lighter surface layer erodes with time.

### Alkaline Penetration and Salt Formation

Alkaline strippers carry an additional risk specific to calcareous stones. Sodium or potassium hydroxide driven into limestone or calcareous sandstone reacts with calcium carbonate in the stone to form calcium hydroxide. As this migrates to the surface and reacts with atmospheric carbon dioxide, it leaves white efflorescence. More damagingly, soluble sulphate salts already present in the stone — common in urban environments due to atmospheric deposition — can react with the alkali to form expansive compounds. The result is spalling and surface delamination that may not appear for weeks or months after treatment, making the link to the cleaning intervention easy to miss and difficult to attribute.

On iron-cemented sandstones, alkaline penetration can attack the cements that bind mineral grains together, causing progressive friability of the face.

## What About Poultice Methods?

Poultice application — packing an absorbent carrier such as kaolin or attapulgite, saturated with stripper, against the surface and allowing it to dry and draw material back out — is a recognised refinement used in conservation practice. It moderates the rate of liquid application, reducing deep penetration risk compared with direct brush or spray.

Poulticing reduces risk; it does not eliminate it. On very porous stone, or where multiple paint layers are present, repeated applications are common. Dwell times of 12–48 hours per cycle and three or more cycles on thickly painted surfaces make the method slow. On occupied buildings or live heritage sites with access constraints, this creates programme difficulties as well as technical ones.

## When Is Mechanical Removal the Better Option?

Where the paint is thick, well-adhered, and the substrate is robust — dense engineering brick, concrete, or hard granite — mechanical methods including disc grinding or controlled abrasive blasting may be appropriate. Abrasive methods are faster than chemical alternatives and do not introduce reactive liquids into the substrate.

However, on historic or soft stone, abrasive blasting causes measurable surface erosion even at pressures considered low by the operative applying them. Research endorsed by conservation bodies including the Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings confirms that wet abrasive blasting can remove several millimetres from softer limestones in a single pass — permanently removing the original dressed or tooled finish.

Mechanical methods are therefore better suited to robust industrial substrates than to listed buildings or heritage masonry.

## When Does Laser Removal Make Sense?

Laser paint removal works on different principles from both chemical and mechanical approaches. A pulsed laser — typically a Q-switched Nd:YAG operating at 1,064 nm — delivers energy in nanosecond bursts. The paint absorbs this energy preferentially because its optical properties differ from the stone beneath. The contaminant layer heats rapidly, ablates, and is removed from the surface. The stone absorbs far less energy at this wavelength and remains largely undisturbed.

Critically, no liquid is introduced into the substrate. There is no solvent migration, no chemical reaction within the pore network, and no wet cycle to carry dissolved material deeper into the stone. The process is dry.

This makes laser removal well suited to:

- Listed buildings and scheduled monuments where reversibility and substrate protection are regulatory requirements
- Porous limestones and sandstones where the risk of chemical penetration is elevated
- Situations where a previous chemical treatment has already caused staining, and further chemical exposure would compound the damage
- Sites where COSHH obligations make solvent or alkaline chemical handling difficult to manage safely

Operator calibration matters. Fluence — energy per unit area — must be set correctly for each paint and substrate combination: too high risks surface scorching; too low leaves removal incomplete. A professional contractor will test on a concealed area before committing to the primary face. Class 4 laser equipment requires operators to hold current laser safety training and comply with BS EN 60825-1, and the ablated fume stream requires appropriate extraction and PPE.

The method selection decision for painted stone is consequential. A wrong choice made early can leave permanent staining, surface damage, or compliance problems that cost substantially more to address than the original cleaning contract.
