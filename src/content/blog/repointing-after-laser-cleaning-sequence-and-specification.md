When a conservation project involves both surface cleaning and repointing, the order of operations is not simply a matter of programme convenience. Getting it wrong has measurable consequences for the quality of both the mortar joints and the cleaned substrate.

## Why Does the Sequence Matter?

The most common mistake is to repoint first and clean afterwards. This seems logical on the face of it — stabilise the structure, then address the surface. But in practice, cleaning a façade after repointing introduces significant risk.

Abrasive methods are obvious offenders: even low-pressure blasting can dislodge freshly laid mortar or drive contaminants into an uncured joint. Less obviously, laser cleaning also presents problems when applied to a recently repointed elevation.

Laser ablation generates a plume of vaporised contaminant material. On open masonry, this is extracted by local exhaust ventilation or disperses. But on a freshly pointed surface, fine ablation residues — carbon particulates, organic binders, metallic compounds from paints or coatings — can settle into the surface texture of uncured lime mortar before it has developed the carbonation skin that resists staining. The result is contamination of the very joint you have just installed.

Lime mortar is also porous and reactive in its early curing phase. Pre-wash chemistry sometimes used before laser treatment — biocides, poultices, pH-adjusting solutions — can migrate laterally into adjacent soft mortar, altering its chemistry or surface colour.

For all these reasons, the established conservation sequence is: clean first, repoint second.

## Are There Exceptions?

There are circumstances where selective repointing before cleaning is warranted. If joints are severely open and there is a risk of water ingress during the cleaning programme — particularly on exposed elevations in autumn or winter — pointing a proportion of the worst voids first is defensible. These should be treated as holding repairs, not finished pointing.

Equally, if a scaffold will not return to a section of the building once it is cleaned, a pragmatic judgement may be needed. In these cases, select a compatible hydraulic lime mix that will tolerate light surface cleaning after curing, and allow the full 28-day minimum curing period before any cleaning operation is brought near it.

Document any departure from the clean-then-repoint sequence and agree it in writing with the conservation officer or client's representative. Where the building is listed, the approach should be consistent with the method statement submitted with the Listed Building Consent application.

## What Should the Cleaned Surface Look Like Before Repointing Begins?

Before any new mortar is introduced, the cleaning contractor and the pointing contractor need to agree on the condition of the substrate — ideally with a formal inspection and photographic record.

The cleaned surface should be free of:

- **Active biological growth.** If laser cleaning has removed the visible algae, lichen, or moss, any remaining rhizinae embedded in the stone should have been addressed by a pre-treatment biocide programme before cleaning commenced.
- **Loose or friable stone at the arris of joints.** Laser cleaning will not dislodge competent substrate, but it will reveal weakness that was previously concealed by paint or soiling. Flag these areas before pointing proceeds.
- **Residual contaminant in the joint void.** Laser cleaning can be directed into joint voids to remove old painted-over mortar staining, but the operator must confirm this work is complete. Repointing over a contaminated void is a common cause of staining bleed in the first winter after completion.
- **Surface salts or efflorescence.** Laser cleaning removes surface contaminants and organic soiling, but it does not remove soluble salts. If the masonry has a history of salt crystallisation, a poultice treatment or a waiting period for natural leaching is needed before pointing. Sealing freshly pointed joints over a salt-active substrate traps crystallisation pressure behind the mortar face.

Once these conditions are confirmed, the substrate is ready for mortar application.

## How Should You Specify the Mortar for Cleaned Historic Masonry?

Mortar specification for historic masonry is a subject in its own right, and this article is not a substitute for laboratory analysis or a conservation specialist's input. That said, several principles specific to the cleaned-masonry context are worth stating clearly.

### Match Colour to the Clean Stone, Not the Soiled One

Laser cleaning returns the masonry surface closer to its original colour, which is directly useful for mortar matching. Sample new pointing mortar against the cleaned face, not against an adjacent un-cleaned section or an interior reference sample. Mortar colour will also shift as it cures, so assess against a cured sample panel on site — not a freshly mixed one.

### Lime Specification

For most listed and pre-twentieth-century masonry in the UK, the appropriate binder is natural hydraulic lime (NHL). NHL is graded by hydraulic strength: NHL 2, NHL 3.5, and NHL 5. Softer, more permeable stones — sandstone, soft limestone, handmade brick — generally require NHL 2 or a feebly hydraulic lime to avoid exceeding the compressive strength of the host material. Dense, competent stone can tolerate NHL 3.5. Ordinary Portland Cement has no place in historic pointing unless specified by a structural engineer for a specific consolidation purpose.

### Aggregate Grading and Source

The aggregate dominates the visual character of the mortar. Broadly match the particle size distribution and colour of the original mortar by commissioning a mortar analysis from a specialist laboratory. A sample can be taken at the same time as the laser cleaning programme is mobilised, before any disturbance of the existing pointing.

Pit sands and local river sands vary significantly in colour across the UK. A mortar that reads correctly in the laboratory can look wrong on the face of the building if the aggregate source is mismatched. Expect to trial two or three aggregate sources on a sample panel, viewed at different times of day, before committing to a mix.

### Joint Profile and Tooling

The joint profile should replicate the historic original where evidence survives. Laser cleaning frequently reveals surviving profile in sheltered areas — under cills, in set-backs, or where previous coatings have been thicker. Avoid standardising on a flush, rubbed-back joint across an elevation that shows evidence of a proud or struck finish. Standardising on the wrong profile is a common and difficult-to-reverse error.

## How Do You Coordinate the Two Programmes on Site?

Laser cleaning and lime repointing are both slow, weather-sensitive trades. On a complex heritage elevation, it is not unusual for cleaning to run at two to three square metres per hour, and pointing at a similar rate. Neither should proceed below 5°C, and lime mortar should not be allowed to dry too rapidly in hot sun or wind.

Build a joint programme at the start of the project. Agree handover zones between the cleaning contractor and the pointing contractor, and confirm that scaffolding arrangements suit both trades. Cleaning residues at the base of scaffolding lifts should be vacuumed before pointing begins — ablation dust must not be incorporated into mixing water or aggregate.

Fume management is a shared obligation. Laser ablation from lead-based paints or chemically treated timbers generates a hazardous fume stream governed by COSHH. Ensure the method statement addresses fume extraction and that pointing operatives working nearby are within the agreed exclusion zone when the laser is active.
