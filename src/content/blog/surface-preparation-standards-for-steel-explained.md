## Why Surface Preparation Grade Matters

When a protective coating fails prematurely on structural steel, bridge ironwork, or industrial plant, the cause is more often inadequate surface preparation than a problem with the coating specification itself. The condition of the substrate at the point of application governs adhesion, and adhesion governs the service life of any protective system.

The SA grades, formalised in ISO 8501-1 and derived from the original Swedish Standard SIS 055900, provide a common language for specifying and inspecting substrate condition. Understanding what each grade demands, and what cleaning method can realistically achieve it, is fundamental to writing a surface treatment programme that will perform as intended.

## The ISO 8501-1 Framework

ISO 8501-1 defines four blast-cleaning grades for steel, referred to as SA 1, SA 2, SA 2.5, and SA 3. Each is assessed visually against photographic reference comparators included in the standard. Before any grade is evaluated, the standard requires the surface to be cleared of visible oil, grease, and accumulated deposits using appropriate preliminary methods.

The grades describe the quantity of mill scale, rust, coating residues, and other contamination remaining on the surface after cleaning. They do not describe the cleaning method used to reach that condition.

### SA 1: Light Blast Cleaning

SA 1 is the lowest grade in the series. Loose mill scale, rust, and loose coatings must be removed, but tightly adherent mill scale, rust, and paint may remain. The surface should be free of visible oil, grease, and dirt.

In practice, SA 1 is rarely an acceptable endpoint for coating work. It may be appropriate for non-structural elements that will receive a highly tolerant primer, or as an interim pass in a multi-stage cleaning process. Specifying SA 1 as a final condition for structural steel that will carry a performance coating is generally considered inadequate, and most coating manufacturers will not warrant their systems applied over an SA 1 substrate.

### SA 2: Thorough Blast Cleaning

At SA 2, nearly all mill scale, rust, and coatings must be removed. Residues that remain are limited to light staining, which may appear as shadows, streaks, or slight discolouration. The anchor profile created by abrasive impact must be visible across the full surface area.

SA 2 is specified for steelwork in less aggressive service environments, or where the coating system itself provides substantial barrier protection. It is a useful grade for maintenance repainting on non-critical structural members and for plant operating in dry, interior conditions.

### SA 2.5: Very Thorough Blast Cleaning

SA 2.5 is the grade most commonly encountered in structural and industrial specifications. At SA 2.5, mill scale, rust, and coatings must be removed to the extent that any remaining traces appear only as slight stains in the form of spots or stripes, covering no more than a small proportion of each unit area. The anchor profile must be well-defined and consistent.

Most major coating manufacturers specify SA 2.5 as the minimum acceptable condition for high-performance epoxy, polyurethane, and zinc-rich primer systems. The grade is typically required by specifiers working to BS EN ISO 12944, the standard for corrosion protection of steel structures by protective paint systems, which links coating durability categories to preparation grade requirements.

For heritage ironwork, infrastructure assets, or steelwork subject to insurance-backed performance guarantees, SA 2.5 is often the baseline specified by loss adjusters and their technical assessors.

### SA 3: Blast Cleaning to Visually Clean Steel

SA 3 represents the highest achievable blast-cleaning grade. The entire surface must be free of all mill scale, rust, coatings, and other contamination. The steel should appear uniformly grey, with a well-defined and consistent anchor profile visible across its full extent.

SA 3 is specified for demanding applications: immersion zones such as submerged pipework and tank linings, thermal spray coatings, and high-build systems where surface discontinuities can propagate failure. The cost of achieving and maintaining SA 3 is considerably higher than SA 2.5, and the interval between blast cleaning and coating application must be tightly controlled to prevent flash rusting before the primer can be applied.

## How Cleaning Method Affects What Is Achievable

Not every cleaning method can reach every SA grade, and the choice of method carries implications beyond cleanliness grade alone.

Abrasive grit blasting remains the most widely used route to SA 2.5 and SA 3 on open sites and in blast chambers. It is effective and well-understood, but it generates significant waste streams, requires containment on sensitive sites, and produces respirable dust that must be managed under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002. On heritage assets or structures adjacent to sensitive environments, the containment requirement and waste management burden can become substantial.

Wet abrasive blasting reduces airborne dust but introduces moisture management requirements and can compress flash rust timescales. Ultra-high-pressure water jetting, assessed under the WJ grades of ISO 8501-4 rather than the SA series, can produce very clean substrates but does not create a mechanical anchor profile in the same manner as abrasive blasting, which affects primer selection.

Laser cleaning operates on a fundamentally different principle. Pulsed laser energy ablates contaminants, mill scale, and rust layers without abrasive media contact, producing no secondary waste stream of spent abrasive and requiring less containment infrastructure. Preparation grades equivalent to SA 2.5 are typically achievable on corroded structural steel, with SA 3 attainable on lighter contamination or with additional passes. For listed structures, conservation areas, or assets where minimal substrate removal is a planning or client requirement, laser cleaning offers a preparation route that abrasive methods cannot provide.

## Specifying Preparation Grade in Practice

When writing or reviewing a surface treatment specification, the preparation grade should be stated alongside the initial condition of the steel (using the rust grades A, B, C, or D from ISO 8501-1), the coating system and its own substrate requirements, the acceptable interval between preparation and coating application, and the inspection method and reference comparators to be used.

Leaving any of these undefined creates ambiguity that tends to be resolved in favour of the contractor rather than the client. A specification that states "abrasive blast" without a grade is a description of a process, not a surface preparation standard.

Where coating failures or disputes arise on previously treated steelwork, preparation grade records, inspection reports, and photographic evidence from the original treatment programme are the primary reference documents. Insurers and loss adjusters reviewing a claim will look for these records as a matter of course, and their absence materially weakens any warranty or performance claim.
