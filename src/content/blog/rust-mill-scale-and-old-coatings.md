## Diagnosing the Surface Before Specifying the Method

Steel in service rarely presents as a single, uniform problem. On any given contract, a structural steelwork package, a heritage ironwork gate, or an industrial plant component might combine active rust, residual mill scale, and previous paint systems applied over decades. Each of these conditions has a different chemistry, a different adhesion profile, and a different implication for the preparation method you specify. Treating them as interchangeable is the most common reason preparation work fails to achieve a durable substrate.

This article covers the three most frequently encountered steel surface conditions, what distinguishes them from each other, and why each demands a considered preparation strategy rather than a default specification.

---

## Rust: What Type, and How Deep?

The word "rust" covers a wide spectrum of conditions. Flash rust is the light surface oxidation that forms within hours of steel being exposed to moisture, particularly after power washing or abrasive blasting. It is superficial and can be removed with relatively light intervention. Pitting rust is different: it has penetrated the steel surface to varying depths and left a roughened profile that, even after the iron oxide is removed, presents as a series of microscopic craters that affect coating build and adhesion.

The critical distinction for specification purposes is whether the oxidation is confined to the surface layer or has begun compromising the structural section. Surface rust can often be addressed through controlled preparation to a defined cleanliness grade. Deep pitting changes the geometry of the substrate and may affect coating build requirements, weld integrity, or the structural cross-section itself.

### Preparation Grades and Why They Matter

ISO 8501-1 defines visual cleanliness grades for steel surfaces prior to coating. The most commonly specified grades in industrial and infrastructure work are Sa 2½ (very thorough blast cleaning, with no visible oil, grease, dirt, mill scale, rust, or coatings remaining other than slight staining in pits) and Sa 3 (blast cleaned to visually clean steel). The grade you specify is not a preference: it is a functional requirement. Most protective coating systems carry manufacturer instructions that set a minimum surface preparation grade. Applying a coating over a substrate that has not reached that grade compromises adhesion and shortens service life.

For lightly rusted steel where blast cleaning is not appropriate, such as confined spaces, proximity to sensitive adjacent surfaces, or heritage contexts where abrasive impact is excluded, power tool cleaning to St 2 or St 3 grades may be the only viable route. Understanding the ceiling imposed by the chosen method before committing to it is essential at the specification stage.

## Mill Scale: The Contaminator That Looks Like the Base Metal

Mill scale is the dense layer of iron oxides formed on the surface of hot-rolled steel during the rolling process. Visually, it presents as a tight, dark grey to blue-black layer that is often mistaken for the base metal itself. It is considerably harder than the steel beneath it and, when newly formed, is well bonded to the surface.

The problem for coating specification is electrochemical rather than mechanical. When moisture reaches the interface between mill scale and steel, galvanic action accelerates localised corrosion beneath the coating, causing delamination from below. This is why most industrial coating specifications require mill scale to be fully removed before application, regardless of how sound the surface appears.

### Detecting Mill Scale in the Field

Mill scale does not always announce itself. On fabricated steelwork that has been in service for some time, it may have partially weathered or been partly removed by previous maintenance activity. A practical field check is to apply a copper sulphate solution to the surface: a copper deposit forming on contact indicates exposed steel, while areas where no deposit forms indicate residual mill scale.

The key point for procurement: if the steelwork history is unknown and original surface preparation records are unavailable, it is safer to assume mill scale may be present and to specify preparation accordingly. The cost of re-preparation after coating failure substantially exceeds the cost of thorough preparation at the outset.

## Old Coatings: Layers, Chemistry, and Regulatory Obligations

Coatings applied over years or decades create a layered substrate whose full composition is often unknown. Each layer may have a different binder chemistry, film thickness, and state of adhesion. Some layers may be structurally intact; others may be failing internally even where the outer surface appears sound. Specifying a new coating system over existing paint without characterising what is beneath it carries significant risk.

### Adhesion Testing Before Any Specification Is Written

The standard approach before specifying work on a structure with an existing paint system is adhesion testing, typically a pull-off test to ISO 4624, to determine whether the existing coating is a viable base or requires removal. Where adhesion is adequate and the existing system is chemically compatible with the planned overcoat, some maintenance painting specifications allow for retention of the existing coating with spot treatment of failures and feathering of edges. Where adhesion is compromised or the system is of unknown composition, full removal is generally the more defensible specification.

### Lead Paint: A Compliance Obligation, Not a Preference

Steel structures of any age, particularly those built or last coated before the late 1980s, may contain lead-based paint. This is not a minor consideration. The Control of Lead at Work Regulations 2002 impose specific requirements on employers and contractors engaged in work that disturbs lead-containing surfaces, covering risk assessment, exposure monitoring, and appropriate controls. The Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 apply to the dust and fumes generated during removal operations.

For those writing specifications or procuring contractors, the obligation to identify the presence of lead paint, commission a risk assessment, and ensure the appointed contractor has appropriate controls in place is shared between the client, specifier, and contractor. An XRF (X-ray fluorescence) survey prior to specification is the standard method for confirming lead content without disturbing the surface.

Waste classification and disposal are also affected. Lead paint waste is subject to UK hazardous waste regulations, and disposal must be documented through appropriate licensed routes.

## Why the Surface Condition Should Drive the Method, Not the Reverse

Each of the three conditions above responds differently to the principal cleaning methods available.

Abrasive blasting achieves high cleanliness grades and creates a defined surface profile for coating adhesion, but it generates significant dust and debris, requires containment, and is unsuitable where the substrate or surrounding environment cannot tolerate abrasive impact or particulate contamination. Chemical stripping is appropriate for some paint removal applications but introduces its own waste stream and may not achieve the preparation grade that a subsequent coating system requires. Laser cleaning removes surface contamination by absorbing the laser energy in the contaminant layer rather than through mechanical impact, making it appropriate in contexts where substrate integrity, containment constraints, or environmental sensitivity rule out conventional methods. No single method is universally correct: the right choice is the one that matches the surface condition, achieves the required preparation grade, and is compatible with the constraints of the working environment.

Specifiers who identify the surface conditions first and work backwards to the method are significantly less likely to encounter preparation failures, coating disputes, or contractor variation claims.
