A graffiti hotspot is defined not by a single incident but by recurrence. If the same wall, underpass, or substation panel has been cleaned three or more times in twelve months, routine reactive response is no longer fit for purpose. Cleaning without strategy simply resets the clock — the same access routes, sightlines, and social conditions that produced the original tagging will produce more.

The shift from reactive to systematic management requires three things: good data, honest causal analysis, and method selection that accounts for the persistence of the problem. This article sets out how estates teams can approach each stage.

## Why Does a Graffiti Hotspot Stay a Hotspot?

Recurrence is rarely random. A location becomes persistent because structural conditions favour it: low natural surveillance, easy access and egress, adjacency to known congregation points, or a surface that holds marks visibly even after cleaning due to ghost imaging from incomplete removal.

The first question to ask of any persistent site is not "how do we clean this?" but "why does this keep happening here?" Without that answer, the cleaning budget is spent on a symptom rather than a cause.

Estates teams that have reduced hotspot recurrence consistently point to the same finding: the maintenance log alone tells you almost nothing useful about causation. Incident date and paint type are recorded; time of day, tagging typology, and whether conditions changed after nearby development or business closures rarely are. That wider context is where the answer usually sits.

## What Does Mapping Actually Look Like?

Mapping is not simply placing incidents on a plan. Useful mapping captures:

- **Location of each incident** at sufficient resolution to identify which surface, which elevation, and which section is targeted
- **Date and approximate time of discovery** — discovery date, not cleaning date
- **Tagging typology**: throw-ups, tags, pieces, or marker and sticker vandalism are produced by different actors with different risk tolerances and time available on site
- **Cumulative incident count per surface section**, not aggregated by location alone

Once four to six incidents have been mapped, spatial patterns become visible. A surface consistently targeted on its blind side, or one that accumulates marks within a few metres of a particular access point, is communicating something about its vulnerability geometry.

### Frequency and Pattern Analysis

Frequency data over a rolling twelve-month period lets you determine whether a hotspot is seasonal, escalating, or stable. Seasonal peaks — common in late spring and summer when evenings are lighter — are distinct from sites attracting escalating attention, which often signals that marks are remaining visible long enough to suggest re-tagging is tolerated.

A cleaning log that records only "cleaned" without noting whether ghost marks remained is a common gap in estate records. Ghost imaging on porous surfaces — stone, brick, render — acts as a visual marker that signals re-tagging is tolerated. Tracking completeness of removal is as important as tracking frequency.

### Causal Analysis: Access Points, Sightlines, and Adjacent Activity

Walk the site as an observer, not as a cleaning operative. Three factors are worth particular attention.

**Entry and exit routes.** Where does someone arrive from, and where do they go? If access requires passing through high-footfall areas, dwell time is constrained. If a quiet alley delivers someone to the blind face of a wall, that is a structural vulnerability that no cleaning programme addresses.

**Sightlines.** Can anyone approaching the surface be seen from occupied windows, passing pedestrian routes, or staffed locations? A wall with no overlooking windows and no facing pedestrian traffic has a structural vulnerability that prompt cleaning does not address.

**Adjacent activity patterns.** Changes in neighbouring land use alter the footfall that previously provided informal surveillance. A unit now vacant, a venue that has closed, a car park with altered hours — these shifts change conditions for a tagger without any change to the target surface. Teams that do not track adjacent changes tend to misread a rise in incidents as escalating behaviour rather than a deteriorating surveillance environment.

## Does the Removal Method Matter for Persistent Sites?

On a persistent hotspot, method selection is a strategic choice, not just an operational one.

Incomplete removal is itself a driver of re-tagging. On porous substrates — brick, sandstone, concrete render — chemical solvents and pressure washing frequently leave ghost marks or drive pigment into the pore structure rather than lifting it cleanly. The surface may look acceptable at close range but reads as marked from the street.

Laser cleaning works on a different principle. A pulsed laser ablates the contaminant layer through differential absorption: the paint or ink absorbs the energy and is removed; the substrate beneath absorbs far less and is left undisturbed. On porous stone, the contaminant is lifted from the surface rather than driven into it, returning the wall to a genuinely unmarked state rather than a cleaned-but-shadowed one.

That matters strategically because it removes one of the visual cues that encourages re-tagging. It does not substitute for addressing access and sightlines, but it eliminates a compounding factor that reactive methods tend to perpetuate.

Where a persistent site involves painted render, brick, or stonework with accumulated layers from multiple incidents, the case for reviewing removal method is strongest. Multiple rounds of chemical treatment can degrade surface finish; abrasive methods carry documented risks of substrate damage even at low pressures. Laser removal, operated at the correct fluence, avoids both concerns.

## How Do You Build a Business Case for Changing Approach?

A business case for changing removal method or investing in site-level intervention needs to demonstrate whole-life cost, not a day-rate comparison.

The elements to quantify are: cleaning frequency and cost per incident over the past twelve to twenty-four months; management time attributable to community or reputational impact; the cost of structural interventions under consideration; and projected cleaning frequency post-intervention if the causal analysis supports a reduction.

Most estates teams find that a hotspot cleaned six to eight times in a year has already cost more than a single specialist intervention that removes the marks completely. Reactive cleaning costs are distributed across a maintenance budget and never appear as a single line, whereas a specialist intervention is a named expenditure. Making the cumulative comparison explicit — supported by log data — converts a persistent nuisance into a defensible business case.

Presenting this to a budget holder requires clarity about what a change in approach does and does not claim to achieve. A method change reduces one driver of recurrence; physical and environmental measures address others. The strongest cases propose a combination.

## When Should You Commission a Specialist Assessment?

If a site has been cleaned more than four times in twelve months without reduction in recurrence, a specialist review is worth commissioning. That review should cover causal analysis and surface condition alongside method recommendation — not simply a quote to clean the current marks.
