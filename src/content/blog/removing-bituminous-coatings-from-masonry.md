## Why Are Bituminous Coatings So Difficult to Remove?

Bituminous products have been applied to masonry for well over a century. Coal tar pitch, bitumen emulsions, and bitumen-modified damp-proof compounds were the default waterproofing solution for basements, retaining walls, bridge abutments, tunnels, and the lower courses of external walls on Victorian and Edwardian terraces. Applied correctly, they resist moisture penetration and bond tenaciously to brick, sandstone, and concrete alike.

That tenacity is precisely why removal is difficult. Bituminous coatings are hydrocarbon-based polymer systems — dense, chemically inert, and engineered to repel water. The same properties that made them effective waterproofers also make them resistant to most standard removal methods. Age compounds the problem: coal tar products cure and harden over decades, becoming increasingly brittle in cold conditions and increasingly adhesive at ambient temperatures.

## Is Chemical Stripping Effective on Bitumen?

Solvent-based strippers can soften bitumen, but the products capable of doing so are typically aggressive — methylene chloride-based formulations or high-concentration alkaline gels. On porous substrates such as brick and limestone, the softened bitumen migrates into the stone matrix rather than lifting cleanly from the surface. The result is contamination embedded in the substrate, which is a more intractable problem than the original surface coating.

COSHH obligations also complicate large-scale solvent application. The fume stream, waste disposal requirements, and risk assessment for high-VOC strippers on an open site carry significant administrative and cost implications. Any responsible method statement must address these.

Alkaline (caustic) strippers are sometimes proposed as an alternative. These are less effective against bitumen than against oil-based paints, and there is a well-documented risk of driving soluble salts into the masonry — causing efflorescence or sub-surface crystallisation damage, particularly in historic brick and limestone. On any building with heritage significance, caustic application demands careful consideration and may not be appropriate at all.

## What About Abrasive Blasting?

Abrasive blasting is frequently proposed for bituminous coatings because the hardness contrast between the coating and the masonry appears to favour the technique. In practice, results are inconsistent.

Bitumen does not fracture cleanly under abrasive impact at ambient temperatures. It tends to smear rather than lift, redistributing across the surface rather than releasing from it. Chilling the substrate with dry ice can harden the coating enough for it to fracture — cold-temperature blasting is a legitimate specialist technique — but it adds equipment complexity, site safety requirements, and cost.

More significantly, even low-pressure abrasive blasting causes measurable surface erosion of historic masonry. Research published by the building conservation sector confirms that compressed-air abrasion removes material from brick and stone even when the operator is targeting only the surface film. On listed or otherwise significant fabric, that material loss is permanent. Water jetting at pressures sufficient to be effective on bitumen carries similar risks and can drive contaminated runoff into open mortar joints.

## Why Is Laser Removal Well-Suited to Bituminous Coatings?

The photothermal mechanism of laser cleaning is well-matched to the specific challenge that bituminous coatings present. A Q-switched Nd:YAG laser operating at 1,064 nm delivers pulsed energy in nanosecond bursts. Bituminous materials absorb strongly at this wavelength. The masonry substrate beneath — brick, limestone, sandstone — absorbs far less energy. This differential absorption is the basis for selective removal.

When each laser pulse strikes the bituminous surface, the coating absorbs the energy rapidly and ablates: it vaporises or fractures and lifts from the surface. The substrate receives only a fraction of the incident energy, which means the operator is not mechanically or chemically attacking the stone in order to remove what sits on top of it.

Fluence calibration is critical and must be carried out by a competent operator. The safe working threshold is typically around 1.5 J/cm²; above this level, plasma formation can occur and substrate heating increases. Any experienced operator will carry out test patches on an inconspicuous area and adjust pulse energy and repetition rate to match the coating thickness and substrate type before treating any visible elevation. Laser cleaning of bituminous coatings is not a set-and-forget process — it requires continuous operator judgement.

## What Does the Substrate Look Like After Removal?

Expectations should be managed carefully before work commences, because the masonry beneath a long-standing bituminous coating is unlikely to be pristine.

Bituminous waterproofing was applied to masonry that was already affected — by rising damp, ground water, or condensation — before the coating went on. After removal, the substrate will typically reveal one or more of the following conditions:

- **Soluble salt deposits** previously sealed beneath the coating, which will bloom as the masonry is exposed and begins to dry out.
- **Surface friability or erosion** where moisture has been trapped between the coating and the masonry over many years, or where water ingress preceded the original application.
- **Mortar joint degradation**, particularly where coating failure allowed water migration that softened lime mortars over time.
- **Residual tonal staining** from organic compounds in coal tar products absorbed into the outer skin of porous stone or brick.

Some degree of permanent colour change — a slight darkening or yellowish cast — is common in porous limestone and sandstone after decades of bitumen contact. Complete restoration of original tone is not always achievable, and clients should be advised of this before any removal programme begins.

## When Is Laser the Right Method?

Laser removal is particularly well-suited to bituminous coatings where:

- The substrate is historic or architecturally significant and cannot sustain any mechanical surface loss.
- The coating covers carved or moulded masonry detail that abrasive methods would obscure or damage.
- The project requires a dry process with no chemical waste stream or contaminated water runoff.
- The scope allows for the measured pace that laser cleaning requires — it is not a rapid bulk-removal technique.

For large-scale industrial structures — retaining walls, bridge soffits, culverts — where the masonry has no heritage sensitivity and the area is substantial, cold-temperature abrasive methods with appropriate substrate monitoring may remain more practical. A professional assessment of the full scope, substrate condition, and any heritage or consent constraints should be carried out before any method is committed to.

## Planning Consent and COSHH Obligations

For listed buildings or structures in conservation areas, removal of surface coatings — regardless of method — may require prior consent from the local planning authority. For scheduled monuments, Historic England's consent is required. This applies even where the coating is a later, non-original addition to the building. Establishing consent requirements before procurement is essential.

COSHH obligations apply to the fume stream generated during laser ablation of bituminous coatings. Adequate extraction or ventilation must be in place, and operators should provide a COSHH assessment as part of their written method statement.
