When a fire damages a masonry structure, the primary concerns are thermal shock, surface contamination, and potential loss of mortar strength. Timber behaves fundamentally differently. It burns, chars, and absorbs water from firefighting efforts — often in that order, within hours.

The key distinction is that timber is a combustible, anisotropic material. Char formation is not purely destructive: a thick layer of char can insulate the residual timber core and slow further degradation. Whether that residual core remains structurally viable is the central question of any assessment. The answer determines whether a beam, joist, or post is retained, repaired, or replaced — and it shapes every cost line in the insurance schedule.

## What Does the Assessment Sequence Look Like?

The sequence matters. Arriving at a remediation contractor before the structural engineer has signed off is a common mistake that leads to abortive work, scope disputes, and occasionally to retained elements that should have been removed.

### Structural engineer sign-off

No remediation work — including surface cleaning — should commence on fire-damaged timber until a structural engineer has inspected the elements in question. The engineer will assess residual cross-section, load path, and connection integrity. In many cases this requires probing, drilling, or trial sections to determine how far degradation has penetrated beyond the visible char layer.

For listed structures, or those within conservation areas, the structural engineer's report will inform the Schedule of Condition submitted to the local planning authority or Historic England. Remediation scope cannot be responsibly drawn up without this document.

### Char depth assessment

Char depth is the key variable for retained timber. The charring rate for softwood runs at approximately 0.65 mm per minute of fire exposure under standard conditions; hardwoods char somewhat more slowly. These are averages: density, moisture content at the time of the fire, and airflow all affect actual char depth on any given element.

Physical probing — using a sharp tool at regular intervals along the element — is usually more reliable than visual inspection alone. Laser profilometry or core sampling may be specified on higher-value or heritage elements where invasive probing would cause additional damage.

The char layer itself is friable and has near-zero structural contribution. Once the engineer has measured it, it becomes a contaminant to be removed before any assessment of residual timber can be completed, or before any protective or decorative finish is applied.

### Moisture content testing

Firefighting water introduces significant moisture into timber. Elevated moisture content creates two problems: it promotes mould and rot in the short term, and it causes dimensional change — shrinkage and checking — as the timber dries over the following weeks and months.

Moisture readings should be taken at multiple depths using a resistance-type moisture meter with insulated probes. The target range for stabilisation before remediation work proceeds is typically 14–19% for most building timbers, but this should be confirmed against the specification from the structural engineer or conservation architect.

Do not attempt any surface treatment or coating until moisture is within acceptable limits. Sealing damp timber is a persistent error that accelerates rot behind the treated surface.

### Embedded fixings and connectors

Metal fixings — bolts, joist hangers, coach screws, ring connectors — behave very differently from timber in fire. Steel loses a significant proportion of its yield strength above 300°C, and many fixings will have reached temperatures well above this threshold in a structural fire. Timber around fixings may show less visible char because metal conducts heat away from the surface, but the fixing itself may be degraded.

Each embedded fixing should be assessed individually, or replaced as a category, depending on the engineer's specification and the scope of the insurance claim. This is particularly important in truss structures, where connector plates may have lost integrity even where the surrounding timber appears largely sound.

## What Can Laser Cleaning Do for Fire-Damaged Timber?

Laser cleaning has a specific and useful role in post-fire timber remediation: removing the friable char layer and soot deposits from retained structural and decorative timber without the mechanical stress associated with wire brushing, abrasive blasting, or aggressive chemical stripping.

A pulsed Nd:YAG laser ablates char and soot through differential absorption. The carbonised surface layer absorbs the laser energy far more strongly than the underlying timber. At correctly calibrated fluence levels, this allows controlled removal of contamination without cutting into the residual wood fibre beneath.

This is particularly valuable for decorative timber — panelling, carved joinery, exposed structural beams in historic buildings — where the underlying substrate carries significant heritage value and mechanical abrasion would cause irreversible damage. Laser cleaning in these contexts can reveal the condition of the timber beneath the char layer and support a more accurate assessment of what can reasonably be retained.

Before-and-after photography, combined with operator calibration records, provides an auditable documentary record of condition both before and after surface remediation. This is useful to the loss adjuster's file and protects all parties in any subsequent dispute about residual condition.

## What Laser Cleaning Cannot Do for Timber

Laser cleaning is not a structural intervention. It does not restore mechanical strength, close checks or cracks, treat active rot, or address moisture-related degradation. It removes surface contamination; it does not rehabilitate the element.

It is also not a substitute for the assessment sequence described above. Cleaning before structural sign-off removes evidence and may alter the appearance of elements in ways that complicate the engineer's inspection. The correct position for laser cleaning in the programme is after structural assessment and after moisture stabilisation — but before any consolidation, repair, or coating of the timber surface.

Operator calibration also matters. The fluence threshold at which a laser begins to ablate sound wood fibre rather than char is narrower than it is on stone or masonry. An inexperienced operator, or one using settings calibrated for a different substrate, can cause surface damage that would not occur with correctly set parameters.

## How Does This Feed Into the Insurance Schedule?

A well-structured insurance claim for fire-damaged timber separates costs into distinct categories: structural assessment, temporary propping and weatherproofing, controlled drying and monitoring, element removal and disposal, surface remediation, and structural repair or replacement. Each category requires its own specification and, typically, a separate line in the schedule of works.

Loss adjusters reviewing timber fire claims should look for evidence that the assessment sequence was followed: a structural engineer's report before any remediation, moisture monitoring records, documented char depth measurements, and a clear rationale for each element retained versus replaced. Claims that run directly from fire brigade release to remediation contractor — without documented structural assessment — are harder to defend in scope disputes and more likely to generate supplementary claims later.

Where laser cleaning is included in the scope, the method statement should specify equipment type, fluence settings, operator qualifications, and the COSHH controls in place for the fume stream produced during ablation. This documentation supports both the initial claim valuation and any future audit of the remediation standard.
