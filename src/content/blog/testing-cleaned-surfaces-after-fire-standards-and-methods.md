After a fire, the pressure to get a building back into use is real. Contractors clean visible soot and smoke deposits, take photographs, and declare the site ready. But visual assessment alone does not meet the standard of care required by insurers, conservation bodies, or health and safety legislation. Demonstrating that a surface is clean requires testing — and the type of test chosen must match the standard being signed off against.

## What does "clean" actually mean after a fire?

The question sounds simple. In practice, it splits into several distinct concerns:

**Chemical residue.** Soot and smoke deposits contain polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), carbon particles, and acidic compounds that continue to corrode and degrade materials after the fire is out.

**Biological load.** Where fire suppression water or elevated humidity is involved, microbial growth can establish quickly on smoke-affected organic substrates.

**Surface pH.** Incomplete combustion leaves acidic residues. On stone, brick, and metals, elevated acidity accelerates substrate degradation over time.

**Embedded contamination.** On porous substrates — natural stone, brick, concrete — soot can be driven beneath the surface. A clean-looking face does not mean a clean substrate.

No single test covers all four concerns. A sign-off protocol for an insurer typically requires a combination of methods agreed in advance.

## Which standards govern post-fire cleaning verification?

### IICRC S500 and S520

The IICRC (Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification) S500 Standard for Professional Water Damage Restoration and S520 Standard for Professional Mold Remediation are widely referenced in UK insurance work, even though they originate from the United States. S500 provides the framework for documenting pre- and post-cleaning conditions; S520 covers biological contamination verification. Neither is a UK statutory requirement, but insurers and loss adjusters commonly cite them in scope-of-works requirements. Meeting these standards provides a defensible audit trail.

### BS EN standards for soot and smoke removal

There is no single overarching British Standard for soot removal verification from all substrates. The relevant European harmonised standards vary by material — BS EN guidance for masonry and stone conservation is referenced for heritage buildings, and BRE guidance covers post-fire structural assessment more broadly. In practice, contractors and loss adjusters often agree a specification in advance based on the substrate type and the intended future use of the building.

For Listed structures or buildings in Conservation Areas, Historic England's guidance on fire damage should be consulted, and the cleaning specification may need to be agreed with the Local Planning Authority before works begin.

### Health and safety obligations

Soot and smoke residue constitute a hazardous substance under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH). Contractors are required to assess worker exposure during cleaning, and post-cleaning verification may be required to confirm that residual contamination levels are safe for reoccupation. This obligation sits alongside — and is separate from — the insurer's sign-off requirement.

## What test methods are available?

### ATP bioluminescence testing

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) bioluminescence testing detects biological residue — organic material left on a surface after cleaning. A swab is taken from a measured area, inserted into a luminometer, and a reading in relative light units (RLUs) is returned within seconds. The method is well-established in food hygiene and healthcare, and has been adopted in post-fire cleaning verification where biological contamination is a concern following suppression water damage.

ATP testing is rapid and straightforward to deploy on site. Its limitation is that it measures organic load rather than chemical contamination specifically. A low ATP reading does not confirm the absence of PAHs or acidic residue.

### Chemical wipe testing

Wipe samples from cleaned surfaces can be submitted to an accredited laboratory for analysis of specific compounds — PAHs, heavy metals, or other fire-specific contaminants, depending on the origin and nature of the fire. The wipe test area, sampling method, and the laboratory reference limits used as pass/fail thresholds should all be agreed with the insurer or loss adjuster before works commence. Results typically take days rather than hours, which needs to be factored into the project programme.

### pH testing

A pH indicator strip or electronic pH meter applied to a wetted surface gives an immediate reading of surface acidity. Post-fire soot leaves acidic residues; a properly cleaned surface should return to the native pH of the substrate — typically 7–9 for concrete or stone. pH testing is inexpensive and rapid, making it a practical first-pass check, but it does not indicate biological load or the presence of specific chemical contaminants.

### Ultraviolet inspection

Under UV light, some organic residues fluoresce and remain visible after apparent visual cleaning. UV inspection is used as a supplementary check — particularly on irregular or textured surfaces where consistent wipe sampling is difficult. It does not replace quantitative testing but helps direct sampling effort to higher-risk areas.

## How are test results used to sign off a scope of works?

A defensible sign-off protocol follows a clear sequence.

Pre-works baseline samples establish the extent and nature of contamination before cleaning begins. This step protects the contractor by documenting what they inherited from the site condition.

Interim testing during the works allows the specification to be adjusted if contamination is found to run deeper than initial assessment indicated. This is particularly relevant for porous masonry substrates.

Post-works samples, taken from the same locations as pre-works samples, demonstrate the outcome. Insurers and loss adjusters typically require results from accredited laboratories rather than field readings alone.

The loss adjuster then issues a clearance certificate once results meet agreed thresholds. This is the document that formally permits reoccupation or reinstatement works to proceed.

The specification for each stage — which tests, which locations, which thresholds — should be agreed in writing before works begin. Contractors who wait until after cleaning to discuss test requirements expose themselves to disputes about whether agreed standards have been met.

## How does the cleaning method affect the reliability of test results?

The method used to clean fire-affected surfaces has a direct bearing on what post-cleaning test results actually demonstrate.

Wet methods — pressure washing or chemical application — can redistribute contamination rather than remove it, driving soot into porous substrates. The surface may test clean while contamination remains at depth. Dry methods avoid this problem. Laser cleaning ablates the contaminated layer without introducing water or solvents. On porous stone or brick, post-cleaning samples therefore reflect the condition of the substrate more accurately, rather than a redistributed surface deposit.

This distinction is material when results are used for insurer sign-off. A clean wipe test following wet cleaning can mask embedded contamination. A clean wipe test following dry cleaning provides stronger evidence that the substrate itself has been treated.
