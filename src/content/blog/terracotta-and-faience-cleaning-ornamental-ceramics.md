## What are terracotta and faience?

Terracotta and faience are architectural ceramics that became defining features of Victorian and Edwardian commercial buildings across the United Kingdom. Both are fired clay products, but they differ in one important respect: terracotta is unglazed or only lightly so, while faience carries a distinct vitreous glaze — typically white, cream, or a deep colour — fired onto the surface at high temperature.

During the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, terracotta and faience cladding was used extensively on banks, department stores, pubs, civic buildings, and municipal infrastructure. The material was prized for its durability compared with carved stone, its ability to be cast in complex moulded forms, and its resistance to the sulphurous pollution then blackening urban Portland stone. Manufacturers including Doulton, Burmantofts, and J. C. Edwards supplied tiles, panels, and decorative friezes that remain visible across British town centres today.

Many of these buildings fall within conservation areas or hold Listed Building status, placing legal constraints on how they are maintained and cleaned.

## Why does the glaze layer matter?

The glaze on faience is not merely decorative. It functions as a sealed, largely impermeable surface that protects the porous ceramic body beneath from moisture ingress, freeze-thaw cycles, and airborne soiling. A sound glaze in good condition is the building's first line of defence.

When that glaze is compromised — through previous incompetent cleaning, physical impact, or long-term weathering — moisture penetrates the ceramic body. In cold weather, freeze-thaw action expands water within the pores, causing spalling and crazing. Once established, this cycle is self-reinforcing and expensive to arrest.

Cleaning decisions on faience are therefore not only about aesthetics. A method that damages the glaze, even subtly, can initiate a deterioration process that far outweighs the original soiling problem. Conservation officers and estates managers should treat the glaze as the primary asset to protect, not an obstacle to overcome.

## What makes these materials difficult to clean?

Terracotta and faience present a particular challenge because they are simultaneously robust and fragile. The fired ceramic body is hard, but the glaze surface reacts very differently from the masonry substrates that cleaning operatives are more accustomed to treating.

Several approaches used routinely on stone or brick are unsuitable here.

**High-pressure water jetting** can force water behind crazed or delaminating glaze, accelerating the freeze-thaw damage described above. It can also drive soluble salts deeper into the ceramic body, contributing to efflorescence and sub-surface crystallisation on subsequent cycles.

**Abrasive grit or wet blasting** is particularly destructive on glazed surfaces. Even at low pressures, abrasive media will scratch and eventually erode the glaze, permanently dulling a surface that cannot be re-polished. Buildingconservation.com confirms that abrasive blasting damages masonry substrates even under controlled conditions; on faience, the consequences are more severe and irreversible.

**Chemical cleaning** carries its own risks. Products designed for stone may react unpredictably with specific glaze formulations, cause staining, or leave residues that are difficult to neutralise. The water used in rinsing compounds the moisture-related risks identified above.

Poulticing is employed in some conservation contexts to draw out embedded soiling, but it is slow, requires specialist expertise to formulate correctly, and is not appropriate for all contaminant types or contaminant depths.

## How does laser cleaning approach faience differently?

Laser cleaning works through a photothermal and photomechanical process. Pulsed laser energy is absorbed by the contaminant layer and converts rapidly to heat or mechanical shock, causing it to ablate from the surface. The governing physical principle is differential absorption: the contaminant absorbs the laser energy far more readily than the substrate beneath.

On glazed ceramic surfaces, this differential is especially useful. The glaze is typically light in colour and relatively reflective at the wavelengths used by standard Q-switched Nd:YAG equipment (1.064 µm). A dark contaminant — graffiti paint, atmospheric carbon, biological growth, surface crusting — absorbs the energy and is removed; the glaze beneath, receiving comparatively little energy, remains intact.

The process is dry. There is no water, no abrasive medium, and no chemical applied to the surface. This eliminates the moisture-ingress risk that makes conventional methods problematic on faience with crazed or partially delaminated glaze.

Fluence calibration — the energy density delivered per pulse, measured in joules per square centimetre — is critical. Standard working thresholds sit around 1.5 J/cm²; above this, plasma formation can cause surface damage regardless of substrate type. Experienced operators work at the lower end of effective fluence, making gradual passes and monitoring the surface response rather than running at maximum power to increase throughput.

## What should specifiers check before commissioning work?

Before cleaning any Listed Building or structure within a conservation area, written consent may be required from the relevant local planning authority. Historic England's guidance on cleaning historic buildings should be consulted early in the planning process, and the conservation officer for the area should be informed of the proposed method. Do not assume that laser cleaning is automatically exempt from consent requirements simply because it is a low-intervention technique.

Operators using Class 4 laser devices must comply with BS EN 60825-1, the laser safety standard, and must have a trained Laser Safety Officer overseeing operations. Verify this before instructing any contractor.

The ablation process generates fine particulate fume from the contaminant material. On painted surfaces, this fume stream may contain lead compounds or other hazardous substances. Operators are required to carry out a COSHH assessment for each site and to have appropriate capture or filtration controls in place. Confirm this documentation exists before work commences.

For faience with existing glaze damage — crazing, delamination, spalling — a condition survey should precede any cleaning. Cleaning will not reverse existing deterioration, and it may be necessary to stabilise vulnerable areas before any cleaning activity begins.

## Is laser cleaning always the right choice for ceramics?

Not always. On lightly soiled terracotta with no existing surface damage and a straightforward contaminant, a carefully formulated chemical poultice or controlled low-pressure wash may achieve adequate results at lower cost. Laser cleaning is most clearly indicated where:

- The glaze is intact but the contaminant is strongly adhered (graffiti paint, bitumen, heavy atmospheric crusting)
- Previous attempts with other methods have been unsuccessful or have caused visible surface damage
- The surface is too fragile or too finely moulded for any abrasive approach
- The building holds Listed status and the consent authority requires a demonstrably low-intervention methodology

Whatever method is proposed, the decision should be informed by a sample trial on an inconspicuous area. Reputable operators will carry this out before committing to a full programme, and the results should be reviewed by a suitably qualified conservation professional before work proceeds at scale.
