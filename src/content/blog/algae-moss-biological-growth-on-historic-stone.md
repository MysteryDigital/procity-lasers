## The Problem Is Not Just Cosmetic

Green or black staining on historic masonry is easy to dismiss as a maintenance inconvenience. The reality is more serious. Biological colonisation follows a succession sequence — from simple algae to complex lichens — and each stage leaves a different kind of damage behind. For buildings under heritage protection, understanding this progression is essential before deciding on any remediation strategy.

## What Is Biological Succession on Stone?

Stone surfaces are colonised in a broadly predictable sequence, driven by moisture, aspect, and atmospheric conditions.

**Algae** are typically the first colonisers. Single-celled or filamentous, they need only moisture, light, and dissolved minerals from the stone surface. Algal films stain stone green, blue-green, or orange-brown depending on species. Direct physical damage at this stage is limited, but the film retains moisture and changes the microclimate at the surface — conditioning the stone for what follows.

**Mosses** follow where algae have prepared the ground. Mosses anchor via root-like structures called rhizoids that penetrate into the stone. These rhizoids exert mechanical pressure as the plant grows, and the contract-and-expand cycles of wetting and drying begin to open fissures in softer limestones and sandstones. Mosses also trap windborne debris and organic matter, building a thin soil layer that supports more complex plants over time.

**Lichens** represent the most complex — and potentially most damaging — stage of succession. A lichen is a symbiotic organism comprising a fungus and a photosynthetic partner, either algae or cyanobacteria. The fungal component produces organic acids, including oxalic acid, that chemically etch the stone. Some species also embed hyphae — microscopic thread-like filaments — directly into the substrate. When a lichen thallus is removed from the surface, it can pull stone grains with it.

## What Damage Does Each Stage Cause?

Algal films cause negligible mechanical damage but accelerate moisture retention. Persistent dampness leads to freeze-thaw cycles that spall the surface of porous stone, and to soluble salt mobilisation that causes sub-surface crystallisation damage (cryptoflorescence).

Moss rhizoid action is particularly destructive on soft oolitic limestone and sandstone. Conservation research has documented significant grain loss beneath established moss colonies. The damage is not always visible from the surface — it is often revealed only after the moss is removed, exposing a friable, weakened zone beneath.

Lichen damage is most significant in acid-producing species. Calcium oxalate crusts form at the stone-lichen interface, and sub-surface hyphae penetrate grain boundaries. On fine-grained marble or polished stone, lichens can permanently alter surface texture. On granite, the process is slower but cumulative over decades.

The idea that lichen acts as a protective layer — trapping the original stone surface beneath it — has some currency among conservators, but this view is increasingly qualified. The evidence for chemical and mechanical harm is robust enough that removal is generally warranted on structurally significant fabric.

## Why Not Just Apply a Biocide?

Biocide application is a common first response. It is low cost to deploy and can treat large areas quickly. The limitations are significant, however.

Biocides kill the biological growth but do not remove it. Dead organic matter continues to retain moisture and can be harder to remove cleanly than living growth. Some biocide formulations — particularly quaternary ammonium compounds — raise questions about run-off and impact on surrounding vegetation. On a churchyard or heritage site with ecological sensitivities, this is a genuine constraint rather than a secondary consideration.

There is also a timing problem. Many biocide treatments require repeated applications over months before the dead growth lifts naturally from the surface. For projects with a defined completion date or where visual outcomes are needed before a deadline, this timetable is often impractical.

Biocide remains appropriate as part of a maintenance regime on large areas of less sensitive fabric — boundary walls, flagstones — where speed and cost are the primary considerations. The question is whether it is sufficient on its own for heritage masonry where the substrate itself is at risk.

## How Does Physical Removal Compare?

Physical methods range from hand-picking and brushing through to pressurised washing and abrasive blasting. Historic England guidance and buildingconservation.com both note that abrasive blasting damages masonry even at low pressure, removing the surface skin and opening the substrate to faster re-colonisation. High-pressure washing can drive moisture and biocide residues deeper into porous stone.

Hand-removal is gentle but slow. It works well for thick surface moss on accessible areas but cannot address the sub-surface rhizoid zone or lichen hyphae without mechanical excavation of the stone itself.

## What Role Does Laser Cleaning Play?

Laser cleaning operates on a photothermal and photomechanical basis. Pulsed energy — typically from a Q-switched Nd:YAG at 1,064 nm — is absorbed selectively by the biological layer or its associated contamination. Because the contaminant absorbs significantly more energy than the stone substrate beneath, the removal threshold can be calibrated to lift the biological material without transferring damaging energy to the stone.

Against algal films, laser delivers consistent results across complex stone profiles — decorative carving, ashlar joints, finely tooled surfaces — where biocides or brushing require extended contact time or cannot reach uniformly.

Against moss, laser addresses both the surface growth and the shallow rhizoid zone. On harder substrates such as granite or hard sandstone, this works well. On very soft stone, operator calibration is critical: fluence must be kept below the threshold at which plasma formation risks surface damage. There is no substitute for an experienced operator assessing the substrate before treatment begins.

Against lichen, results depend on species and substrate. Thin crustose lichen on granite responds well. On limestone or fine marble, the calcium oxalate crust can complicate treatment because it may share similar absorption characteristics to the substrate. In these situations, laser is most effective as part of a combined approach — biocide pre-treatment to reduce biological viability, followed by laser removal of residual crust — rather than as a standalone intervention.

Because laser is a dry process with no consumables, it introduces no solvents or liquid into porous stone. On substrates where driving contamination deeper is a known risk with biocide drench or pressure washing, this is a meaningful advantage.

### Fume and Waste Management

Ablation of biological material produces a fume stream. Under COSHH obligations, operators must assess the hazard and provide appropriate extraction or respiratory protection. This obligation applies equally to biocide handling and to any mechanical disruption of biological matter. A thorough method statement should address it explicitly for each stage of the work.

## Choosing a Strategy That Fits the Substrate

No single method suits every situation. The right approach depends on the stone type, the succession stage, the heritage designation of the structure, and any ecological constraints on the site. A conservation professional should assess the fabric before a specification is written, and any intervention on a Listed Building requires agreement with the relevant planning authority.
