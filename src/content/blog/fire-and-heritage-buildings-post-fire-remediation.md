## Why the Cleaning Phase Is as Consequential as the Fire Itself

When a listed building suffers fire damage, the immediate instinct is understandable: get on site, clear the debris, and start making it safe. But for heritage structures, the period between the fire being extinguished and the first active cleaning intervention is one of the highest-risk windows in the entire remediation timeline. Decisions made in that window, often under time pressure and with incomplete information, can permanently compromise the fabric of a building that has survived for centuries.

The damage profile of a serious structural fire is rarely confined to charring. Superheated gases migrate deep into porous masonry. Combustion residues, soot, and the complex chemical compounds produced by burning modern fittings, insulation, and synthetic furnishings coat historic surfaces. Firefighting water introduces moisture into already-stressed substrates. Thermal cycling causes micro-cracking in stone and mortar that may not be visible to the naked eye. Each of these requires a different treatment approach, and treating them as a single undifferentiated cleaning problem is the most common technical error seen in post-fire remediation work.

## The Regulatory and Consent Framework

Heritage buildings in England and Wales fall under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990. That framework does not pause because a building has sustained fire damage. Any works that affect the character of a listed building, including cleaning and stabilisation treatments, require listed building consent unless they can be demonstrated to fall within the scope of emergency works or urgent repairs as defined under the Act.

In practice, this means engaging with the local planning authority and Historic England (or Cadw in Wales, Historic Environment Scotland north of the border) before undertaking anything beyond genuinely urgent stabilisation. Emergency scaffolding, propping, and protective covering are typically acceptable without prior consent. Applying chemical cleaning agents to smoke-stained stonework is not.

Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 also come into play, particularly where contractors are handling fire residues that may include asbestos-containing materials or heavy metal deposits from burning older paints and coatings. A COSHH assessment is required before any work disturbs those residues, regardless of the heritage status of the structure.

## Substrate Assessment Before Any Intervention

### Understanding What the Fire Has Done to the Stone

No two post-fire scenarios present identically. The substrate condition depends on the stone or brick type, the intensity and duration of the fire, the presence or absence of protective coatings, and the extent of water ingress from firefighting. An assessment phase is not optional: it is the only responsible starting point.

Thermally damaged limestone and sandstone frequently undergo mineralogical change at temperatures above 300 degrees Celsius. Calcium carbonate converts to calcium oxide, a process that is irreversible and that dramatically weakens the stone. Rehydration of calcium oxide during firefighting produces calcium hydroxide, which is mildly caustic and highly soluble. Cleaning with water-based systems before this process has stabilised can accelerate surface loss and introduce new soluble salt deposits into the masonry body.

A conservation specialist or materials scientist should assess the substrate condition and define moisture levels before any cleaning methodology is selected. This is not bureaucratic caution: it is the step that prevents the cleaning contract from becoming the cause of the structural survey report that follows.

### Documentation as a Baseline

Comprehensive photographic and written documentation before cleaning begins serves two purposes. It establishes the precise extent of fire damage for insurance purposes and provides the baseline against which the success of cleaning treatments can be measured. Attempting to reconstruct the pre-cleaning condition from memory or incomplete records is a persistent source of dispute between contractors, heritage bodies, and insurers.

## Common Mistakes in Post-Fire Cleaning

### High-Pressure Water Washing

Pressure washing is frequently proposed in the early stages of a post-fire remediation because it is fast, cheap, and visually effective in removing loose soot. On heritage masonry it is almost always contraindicated. High-pressure water drives soluble fire residues deeper into porous stone rather than removing them. It also introduces significant quantities of water into a structure that is already moisture-compromised, displaces fragile residual pointing and consolidants, and can remove weathered surface detail that has taken decades or centuries to develop.

### Aggressive Chemical Treatments

Proprietary cleaning products formulated for fire damage on modern building materials are not validated for historic substrates. Many contain surfactants, bleaching agents, or solvents that react unpredictably with the mineral composition of older stone or with pre-existing consolidation treatments. Without prior testing on a concealed area, applying them to a listed building is speculative at best.

### Proceeding Without a Method Statement

A written method statement is not a formality. It forces the cleaning contractor to define, in advance, the specific treatments proposed, the sequence of application, the dwell times, the water volumes, and the contingency approach if the primary method proves unsuitable on contact. For heritage work post-fire, Historic England and most local authority conservation officers will expect to see and approve a method statement before cleaning begins. Producing it retrospectively is not an option.

## Choosing the Appropriate Cleaning Method

The appropriate cleaning method is determined by the substrate assessment, not by the contractor's available equipment. On heavily sooted limestone or brick, where thermal damage has been limited and the substrate is sufficiently stable, controlled laser ablation has established a strong track record. Laser energy is selectively absorbed by the dark soot and combustion residue layer, which vaporises cleanly, without the mechanical impact and moisture loading that make other methods so problematic on fragile heritage surfaces.

Laser cleaning does not suit every post-fire scenario. Where substrates are thermally weakened or structurally compromised, or where contamination has penetrated beyond the surface layer, it forms one element of a staged remediation programme rather than the complete solution. Any responsible assessment will identify which zones benefit from which approach, and will specify them accordingly.

Consolidation, mortar repointing, and surface protection treatments follow cleaning rather than preceding it. Introducing hydrophobic coatings or consolidants to surfaces that have not been adequately cleaned traps residues and salts against the substrate, which leads to accelerated decay.

## Working With the Heritage Body

Historic England's published guidance on fire-damaged historic buildings is the practical reference point for specification writers and conservation architects working on these projects. The guidance emphasises the importance of retaining authentic fabric wherever possible, even where that fabric is fire-damaged, and of resisting the pressure to replace damaged material that could be conserved.

Loss adjusters and insurers engaged on heritage losses should anticipate that the scope and timeline for post-fire remediation will be longer and more complex than for a comparable non-listed property. The additional consent requirements, assessment phases, and specialist treatment methodologies are not discretionary costs: they are the legally and technically correct approach, and underwriting a rapid solution that bypasses them creates liability exposure.
