Lead has been used on British roofs for well over a thousand years. It is found on cathedrals, country houses, civic buildings, and Georgian terraces, and its longevity is partly a function of its chemistry: left alone, lead forms its own protective surface layer that limits further corrosion. That makes it one of the more forgiving heritage materials — but that same chemistry means incorrect cleaning can do more damage than the soiling it was meant to remove.

## What Is Lead Patina and Why Does It Matter?

Fresh lead is bright silver-grey and highly reactive. Within hours of exposure to air it begins to oxidise, forming a thin layer of lead oxide. Over months and years, in the presence of moisture and atmospheric carbon dioxide, this converts further into lead carbonate (cerussite) and lead sulphate — the stable, mid-grey patina characteristic of aged leadwork.

This patina is not cosmetic. It is chemically stable and tightly bonded to the surface, and it significantly reduces the rate at which the underlying lead corrodes. In conservation terms, it is part of the material's integrity. Removing it — even unintentionally — restarts the corrosion clock.

Problematic soiling is a different matter. Lead roofs accumulate:

- **Biological growth**: algae, lichen, and moss, particularly on north-facing slopes with limited sun exposure.
- **Pollution crusts**: sulphate and carbon deposits in urban and industrial environments.
- **Surface staining**: run-off staining from adjacent materials and localised organic matter.
- **Organic debris**: leaf litter and retained moss can hold moisture against the surface, accelerating corrosion at joints and drips.

The cleaning challenge is to remove that soiling without disturbing the underlying patina.

## Why Does Abrasive Cleaning Damage Lead?

Lead is a soft metal, roughly 1.5 on the Mohs scale — softer than a fingernail. Any abrasive process, whether grit blasting, pressure washing, or mechanical scrubbing, risks abrading the surface and stripping the patina along with the soiling.

Once the patina is removed, fresh lead is exposed. It re-oxidises quickly, but not uniformly. On aged leadwork — particularly on sheet lead that has work-hardened and fatigued over decades — uneven re-oxidation can lead to differential corrosion. In practice, abrasive cleaning, even when carried out with care, can leave lead in a worse condition than before treatment.

Grit blasting is particularly problematic. At pressures sufficient to remove compacted biological growth or pollution crust, blasting will also deform the lead surface and can physically compress or fracture the substrate at vulnerable points — rolled joints, drips, and welted seams. High-pressure water jetting carries similar risks, and can force water into lap joints that are already under stress.

Chemical strippers present a different set of risks. Acid-based products attack lead carbonate directly, dissolving the patina. Alkaline strippers can loosen surface contaminants but are difficult to neutralise fully and may leave residues that continue to react with the substrate. Both require careful containment of run-off, which is logistically complex on pitched roofs and creates additional COSHH obligations around lead-contaminated waste water.

## What Does Conservation Guidance Say?

Historic England's conservation principles advocate minimal intervention as a baseline. Applied to lead cleaning, this means: establish that soiling is causing measurable harm before treating it, and use the least invasive effective method when treatment is necessary.

For leadwork on a listed building, any significant intervention — including cleaning — is likely to require Listed Building Consent. Conservation officers will expect a method statement demonstrating that the proposed approach will not damage the historic fabric. A pre-treatment condition survey is standard practice. This should identify the type and extent of soiling, any existing defects such as fatigue cracking or failed joints, and the condition of the patina. Soiling that is cosmetically unattractive but not causing structural or chemical harm may not warrant intervention at all.

Getting this assessment right before specifying a cleaning method avoids unnecessary work and protects the building owner from liability if the treatment damages the material.

## How Does Laser Cleaning Work on Lead Roofing?

Laser cleaning uses short pulses of light to remove surface contaminants through a photothermal and photomechanical process. The critical property is differential absorption: the contaminant layer absorbs far more laser energy than the substrate beneath it. The contaminant is ablated — vaporised or mechanically detached — while the underlying material receives minimal energy.

On lead, this differential is useful. Pollution crusts, biological matter, and organic soiling absorb laser energy readily at the wavelengths used by Q-switched Nd:YAG equipment (1,064 nm). The lead substrate and its carbonate patina are comparatively reflective at that wavelength. With correct fluence calibration, it is possible to remove surface soiling without disturbing the patina layer beneath.

There is no mechanical contact, no abrasive media, and no water or chemical introduced to the substrate. For isolated staining, biological growth on complex lead details, or soiling around decorative cast leadwork, this selectivity is difficult to match with other methods.

### Operator Calibration and COSHH Obligations

Laser cleaning is not a low-skill process. Fluence must be calibrated for each substrate and soiling type. The threshold above which plasma formation and surface alteration become a risk is well-established — approximately 1.5 J/cm² — but operating safely below that threshold requires accurate equipment setup and an operator who understands how different substrates and contamination types respond to the beam.

Lead-bearing ablation produces a fume stream. Lead particles and vapour present a health hazard, and COSHH obligations apply to operators working on or near lead-bearing substrates. Adequate local exhaust ventilation, appropriate respiratory protection, and site controls for containing and disposing of ablation waste are mandatory. Any building owner or estates manager commissioning this work should expect a contractor to provide documented procedures for lead fume management as part of their method statement.

## When Is Laser Cleaning Appropriate for Lead?

Laser cleaning is well-suited to:

- Localised pollution staining or crust removal on lead roofing and flashings
- Biological growth — algae, lichen, and moss — on flat or low-pitch lead surfaces
- Carbon and sulphate deposits in sheltered or poorly ventilated bays
- Cleaning around decorative lead details and cast ornament where mechanical access is restricted

It is not a remedy for structural defects. Fatigue cracking, open joints, active leaks, and delamination are repair issues requiring lead working skills, not cleaning. A thorough condition survey should precede any cleaning programme to confirm the roof is in a suitable state to receive treatment and that the scope of work has been correctly defined.

## What Are the Whole-Life Cost Implications?

Abrasive and chemical methods that strip the patina require the surface to re-stabilise before the lead's natural corrosion resistance is restored. On heritage leadwork, the existing patina may represent decades of stable formation. Removing it is not a neutral act: there is a period of elevated vulnerability while the new patina develops, and there is no guarantee the re-formed patina will be as uniform or as well-bonded as the original.

A cleaning approach that targets soiling while preserving the underlying patina avoids that period of vulnerability. When evaluating quotes for lead cleaning on a heritage building, ask contractors how they propose to protect the patina, and what evidence they can provide that their method has been used successfully on comparable substrates. Before-and-after documentation from previous projects is the most useful indicator of competence and care.
