## Why Does Graffiti Attract More Graffiti?

The connection between existing graffiti and new tagging is well-established in criminological research. The broken windows theory, developed by Wilson and Kelling in 1982, holds that visible signs of disorder — broken windows, litter, and graffiti — signal that a space is not maintained or monitored. That signal lowers the perceived risk of further offending.

More recent field research has gone further. A 2008 study by Kees Keizer and colleagues, published in *Science*, tested this hypothesis in real-world conditions. When researchers placed a wall covered in graffiti next to a bicycle rack, the rate at which passersby engaged in other low-level rule-breaking nearly doubled compared to a clean wall. The effect was not marginal.

For councils and property managers, the implication is straightforward: each piece of graffiti that remains on a surface is actively inviting the next one.

## What Does the Research Say About Response Time?

Academic and operational evidence consistently points in one direction: faster removal produces lower recurrence rates.

Research conducted across a number of US cities in the 1990s found that surfaces cleaned within 24 hours of tagging were re-tagged at significantly lower rates than those left for several days. UK experience has found similar patterns. Rapid-response graffiti programmes that operate under strict 24-hour removal mandates have consistently reported reductions in incident volumes at attended sites, compared with programmes that allow longer dwell times.

The mechanism appears to be partly psychological and partly reputational within tagging subcultures. A tag that disappears overnight is not visible to other taggers or to the tagger's own social circle. It does not function as a territorial marker, it does not build reputation, and it does not invite escalation to more elaborate pieces. The incentive to return — or for others to add to an established spot — is substantially reduced.

This creates a clear obligation for any reactive graffiti contract: response time is not just an operational metric. It is the primary determinant of whether the programme actually works.

## Why First-Time Clean Matters as Much as Speed

Speed alone is not sufficient. A rapid response that leaves a ghost image, staining, or visible residue on the substrate can still signal neglect. Taggers, and the broader public, can see that an attempt was made and failed. In some cases, a partial removal makes the surface a more prominent target rather than less, because it draws attention to the original location.

This is where the cleaning method directly affects the deterrence outcome. Chemical and abrasive approaches can struggle to achieve complete removal from certain substrates — particularly porous masonry, engineering brick, and painted concrete — without multiple treatments. Each treatment cycle adds time, cost, and further substrate wear. If a crew attends, treats, and leaves a partial result, the surface requires a return visit. The 24-hour window for maximum deterrence may already have closed by the time the second visit occurs.

Laser cleaning operates on a fundamentally different basis. The pulsed laser ablates the contaminant layer through selective absorption: paint or ink absorbs the laser energy far more readily than the substrate beneath, allowing the contaminant to be removed without aggressive mechanical action. On porous stone and brick — substrates that are particularly vulnerable to abrasive methods — laser cleaning removes material from the surface without driving it deeper into the pores.

The practical result is that a well-calibrated laser treatment is considerably more likely to achieve a clean result on the first visit. That matters in a rapid-response context because it closes the deterrence loop: the surface is restored to its original condition promptly, and no residual evidence of the tag remains.

## What Are the Economics of a Rapid-Response Programme?

Graffiti removal contracts are often costed on a day rate or cost-per-incident basis. That framing can be misleading, because it ignores the cost of repeat incidents on the same surface and the overhead of multiple treatment visits.

A reactive contract relying on chemical or abrasive methods may require two or three visits to achieve a clean result on difficult substrates. Each visit carries mobilisation costs, labour costs, and — for abrasive methods — consumable costs. Across a portfolio of sites, the cumulative cost of partial treatments can be substantial. Delayed closure also extends the window during which the tag is visible and actively generating further incidents.

Laser equipment carries no consumable costs during operation. Once on site, the treatment is completed in a single visit for substrates and contaminant types within the equipment's capability. For a council running hundreds of reactive call-outs per year, the reduction in repeat visits has a direct and measurable effect on programme cost.

There is a further consideration for protected or heritage assets. Abrasive blasting, even at low pressure, causes measurable damage to historic masonry — this is documented by building conservation bodies and has led many heritage organisations to move away from abrasive methods entirely. Where a graffiti incident occurs on a listed structure or scheduled monument, the method used for removal may itself require prior approval from the relevant authority. Laser cleaning's substantially reduced substrate impact makes it a more defensible choice in those contexts and is increasingly specified by conservation officers for that reason.

## How Should Reactive Contracts Be Structured?

If response time is the primary deterrence lever, contracts should be structured to reward speed and to measure outcomes beyond the initial call-out.

A 24-hour response target is the benchmark most supported by available evidence. Anything beyond 48 hours begins to compromise the deterrence effect, particularly on high-footfall surfaces where a large audience sees the tag within the first day. Key questions for procurement officers reviewing reactive graffiti contracts include the following.

**Response time commitment.** Is there a contractual service level agreement, and is it monitored with audit data? An SLA without a reporting mechanism is difficult to enforce.

**First-time clean rate.** What proportion of incidents are resolved without a return visit? A contractor achieving full removal on 90 per cent of first visits is more valuable to a deterrence programme than one achieving lower cost per visit but requiring frequent returns.

**Substrate compatibility.** Can the cleaning method achieve a satisfactory result on the specific substrates in your estate — porous sandstone, engineering brick, painted render, glazed ceramic, glass? Not all methods perform consistently across substrate types, and specifying a method without confirming capability can result in avoidable damage.

**Audit trail.** Are before-and-after photographs provided for every attended incident as standard? A photographic record demonstrates contract performance, supports enforcement action against repeat offenders, and provides evidence for any insurance or compensation claim.

## Thinking About Programme Design

The deterrence case for rapid graffiti removal is not theoretical. It is supported by field evidence, and it has practical implications for how reactive programmes are designed and what cleaning methods are specified. A method that achieves a clean result on the first visit is not merely operationally preferable — it is a functional part of the deterrence strategy. Speed and quality are not in tension; for the programme to work, both are required.
