The budget line most graffiti contracts are assessed on is cost per call-out. It is a legible, auditable number. It also tells you almost nothing about what graffiti management is actually costing your organisation over time.

When the true cost is disaggregated properly, including repeat treatments, substrate repair, procurement overhead, and internal management time, the reactive call-out model frequently costs significantly more than the headline figures suggest. For asset-intensive organisations such as local authorities and housing associations, this gap between perceived and actual expenditure is worth examining carefully before the next contract renewal.

## What "Reactive" Actually Means in Practice

A reactive model, as most graffiti contracts are structured, means that removal is triggered by a report. Vandalism is identified, logged, a call-out is raised, and a crew is dispatched. The work is assessed on arrival and treatment is applied.

The model has clear advantages: you pay only when an incident occurs, and mobilisation can be fast where a framework contract is already in place. The disadvantages are less immediately visible. They accumulate quietly across a financial year and rarely appear as a single line in a budget review.

## The Cost Components Most Reactive Budgets Fail to Capture

### Call-out and mobilisation fees

Each reactive call-out carries a minimum mobilisation charge, regardless of the scale or complexity of the work. For scattered tagging across a housing estate, several small call-outs within a single month may each attract the same minimum fee. Framework agreements reduce unit costs but do not eliminate the mobilisation component. Where a call-out falls outside contracted hours, or at a location at the edge of a contractor's operating area, additional rates typically apply.

The cumulative effect of numerous small call-outs across a year is easy to underestimate, particularly where incidents are logged across multiple cost centres or reported through different housing or estates officers.

### Labour and repeat visits

First-time removal rates vary considerably depending on the substrate, the paint or ink type, and how long the graffiti has been in situ. Vandalism that has cured and penetrated porous masonry is substantially harder to remove in a single visit than fresh tagging on a smooth painted surface. Chemical-based methods, which remain the most common approach in reactive contracts, frequently require multiple treatment cycles with dwell periods between applications.

Each return visit is billed as a further call-out. In reactive contracts without a guaranteed fixed-price outcome, the second and third visits may carry their own mobilisation fees. What began as a single call-out cost can double or triple before the work is complete.

### Substrate repair

This is the cost that most reactive contracts do not price into the initial quotation, because substrate damage is not always apparent until treatment is underway or complete.

Pressure washing at excessive concentration, or with inappropriate nozzle distances, can erode soft stone, open masonry joints, or saturate render. Chemical solvents suitable for smooth painted surfaces can bleach natural stone, stain limestone, or interact unpredictably with existing coatings or consolidants. When this occurs, the remediation cost (re-pointing, repainting, or specialist conservation repair) falls outside the graffiti removal contract and is typically absorbed into a separate maintenance budget. The causal connection to the original call-out becomes invisible to anyone reviewing expenditure.

## Why Repeat Treatments Are More Common Than They Appear

The assumption embedded in most reactive budgets is that each call-out resolves the problem. In practice, hotspot locations are re-tagged repeatedly. A wall cleaned in January may require several further visits before the year ends, each treated administratively as a fresh incident rather than as part of a persistent pattern.

For organisations managing large housing estates or extensive public realm, the absence of trend data across call-outs means the same locations are budgeted for on a one-off basis, repeatedly. Without aggregated reporting by location, the true annual cost of managing a persistent hotspot is rarely visible to anyone reviewing overall spend. The total cost remains distributed across individual purchase orders rather than appearing as what it actually is: a concentrated expenditure problem at a small number of sites.

A managed programme treats persistent hotspots differently. Higher-frequency planned visits, consideration of anti-graffiti coating application to reduce paint adhesion and accelerate future removal, and in some cases a review of site conditions that may be contributing to persistent targeting. The cost per incident falls; the management overhead falls with it.

## The Procurement Overhead No One Accounts For

Each unplanned call-out is a procurement event. Even where a framework contract is in place, someone within the organisation must log the incident, raise the instruction, track completion, and verify the invoice. For estates and housing officers managing graffiti alongside a range of other responsibilities, this overhead is absorbed invisibly into staff time.

The cumulative administrative burden of managing a high-volume reactive programme across multiple sites is a real cost. It does not appear in the cost-per-call-out figure, and it is not captured in most graffiti removal budgets. It is worth estimating and including when comparing reactive and managed programme models.

## What Whole-Life Costing Looks Like in Practice

A whole-life cost model for graffiti management incorporates the following elements, considered across a multi-year horizon:

- Direct removal costs per call-out, including mobilisation and any repeat visits
- Substrate repair costs attributable to removal activity
- Anti-graffiti coating application and reapplication cycles
- Contract management and internal staff time overhead
- Compliance considerations under relevant anti-social behaviour and housing standards obligations

When these components are brought together and compared against a managed programme model, the differential is often material. The managed programme carries a higher planned expenditure in year one, but a lower total cost of ownership over a three to five year horizon. The planned element is also substantially easier to budget accurately, which has its own value for organisations working to medium-term financial plans.

## Moving from Reactive to Managed Programmes

The shift from reactive to managed graffiti removal is not primarily a technical decision. It is a procurement and contract design question. It requires commissioners to specify and cost outcomes rather than individual call-outs, to accept a higher fixed commitment in exchange for greater certainty, and to build performance metrics that capture removal quality alongside speed and response time.

Laser-based removal is particularly well suited to managed programme models. The technique removes contaminants from the substrate surface without the chemical or mechanical abrasion that drives substrate damage costs, is effective across a wide range of substrates including listed and heritage structures, and produces a consistent, verifiable outcome. Because it does not rely on solvents, it avoids the disposal requirements that apply to chemically contaminated waste under the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002, reducing contractor risk and simplifying compliance documentation.
