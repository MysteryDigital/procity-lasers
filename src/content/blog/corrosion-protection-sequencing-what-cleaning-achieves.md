Surface preparation is often treated as the primary deliverable in steel maintenance contracts. The cleaning contractor achieves a specified grade; the work is signed off. But a clean surface is not a protected surface. Achieving a recognised cleanliness grade is a precondition for everything that follows — it creates the conditions in which a coating system can perform, but it does not itself provide corrosion protection.

Understanding this distinction matters practically. Procurement officers, estates managers, and site managers who oversee industrial steel maintenance programmes carry responsibility across the full sequence: cleaning, priming, intermediate coats, and topcoat. Treating cleaning as the end point rather than the starting point is a common cause of early coating failure.

## Is a Cleanliness Grade Enough to Prevent Corrosion?

No. A steel surface cleaned to a recognised grade but left unprimed will begin to flash-rust within hours in humid conditions. The cleanliness grade does not delay corrosion onset; it creates the opportunity for a coating system to do so.

The relevant standards for surface preparation are set out in ISO 8501-1, which defines preparation grades for blast-cleaned steel. The most commonly specified grade for protective coating applications is Sa 2½ — very thorough blast-cleaning to the point where only faint staining remains. Sa 3 represents blast-cleaning to visually clean steel with no visible residue. For non-blast methods, equivalent St grades apply.

What these grades measure is the visual absence of existing contamination — rust, mill scale, existing coatings, and foreign matter. They do not measure what happens next. The surface preparation is the foundation; it cannot stand in for the structure built on top of it.

## What Does Coating Adhesion Actually Depend On?

Two factors govern whether a coating system bonds adequately and performs over its intended service life: surface chemistry and surface profile. Both are affected by decisions made during and after cleaning.

### Surface Chemistry and the Overcoating Window

Blast-cleaning exposes freshly activated metal. That freshly activated surface is reactive, which is the property that allows primers to bond effectively. It is also vulnerable: re-contamination after cleaning — by oil, dust, chlorides, or condensation — will compromise adhesion even if the visual cleanliness grade is technically still met.

The time between cleaning and priming is therefore a controlled variable. Most coating system specifications define a maximum overcoating interval: the period within which primer must be applied before the surface condition deteriorates below the required standard. Programme slippage, adverse weather, or simple oversight that pushes application beyond this interval can undermine the entire surface preparation effort.

Chloride contamination warrants particular attention on structures in coastal locations or environments where de-icing salts are used. Chlorides are hygroscopic — they attract and retain moisture beneath coatings, initiating under-film corrosion that is not visible until the coating system has already failed. ISO 8502 provides test methods for assessing soluble salt contamination levels. Where levels exceed the threshold specified for the coating system, further treatment is required regardless of visual cleanliness grade achieved.

### Surface Profile and Mechanical Interlocking

Surface profile — sometimes called anchor profile or surface roughness — refers to the microscopic peaks and valleys created by abrasive blast-cleaning. Profile depth is measured in micrometres and assessed using comparators or profilometers in accordance with ISO 8503.

Profile matters because it increases the true contact area between steel and primer and provides mechanical interlocking points for the coating. A smooth steel surface, even if chemically clean, offers less adhesion area than a correctly profiled one. Most high-performance coating systems specify both a minimum and a maximum profile depth: too shallow and adhesion is inadequate; too deep and the peaks can project through a thin primer coat, creating discrete corrosion initiation points across the surface.

Grit or shot blast processes generate profile as a direct consequence of the cleaning action. Laser cleaning, by contrast, removes contamination through selective thermal ablation without mechanically abrading the substrate. Laser-cleaned surfaces will not carry blast-induced profile. Whether this is a limitation depends entirely on the downstream specification: for re-coating applications that require a defined profile range, additional surface preparation may be required after laser cleaning, or the coating specification may need to be assessed against the actual substrate profile. This question should be resolved at the specification stage, not discovered at the inspection stage.

## Why Does the Sequence from Primer to Topcoat Matter?

A corrosion protection system is a layered assembly. Each component has a defined function, and each depends on the integrity of the one beneath it.

**Surface preparation** creates the physical and chemical conditions for adhesion. It removes what would prevent bonding and, in blast-cleaned applications, generates the profile that enables mechanical interlocking.

**Primer** provides the primary bond between the steel surface and the coating system as a whole. Primers for corrosion protection typically contain active inhibitors — zinc phosphate or zinc-rich formulations — that provide electrochemical protection to the underlying metal. The primer is not a topcoat. It is not formulated to resist UV exposure, abrasion, or chemical attack; it is formulated to adhere to the prepared substrate and to anchor what follows.

**Intermediate coats**, where specified, build film thickness between primer and topcoat. They contribute barrier resistance without carrying the adhesion function of the primer or the external exposure resistance of the topcoat.

**Topcoat** provides resistance to whatever the structure encounters in service — weathering, UV degradation, industrial chemicals, or mechanical damage. It is the most visible layer, but the least load-bearing in terms of corrosion protection. A topcoat applied over a failed or poorly adhering primer will fail itself.

This is why quality oversight should apply at each hold point in the sequence — after surface preparation, before and after primer application, and before overcoating — rather than being concentrated at the cleaning stage alone. Inspection of the completed topcoat cannot compensate for undetected failures in the layers beneath it.

## Where Does Laser Cleaning Sit in This Sequence?

Laser cleaning removes contamination through selective thermal ablation. The laser pulse is absorbed preferentially by the contaminant layer — rust, paint, scale, or deposits — which is ejected as vapour or particulate while the steel substrate is largely undisturbed. The process can achieve cleanliness grades equivalent to Sa 2½ or better on appropriate substrates, without the abrasive impact on the surrounding material associated with grit blasting.

This selectivity suits applications where abrasive blasting is impractical — intricate steelwork, confined access, or structures where grit would damage adjacent material. The ablation product stream constitutes a fume hazard and is subject to COSHH obligations; adequate extraction and operator controls are required.

For re-coating applications following laser cleaning, the surface profile question must be addressed explicitly with the coating manufacturer before specifying the primer system. This is not a barrier to laser cleaning as a preparation method, but it requires that the downstream specification is developed with awareness of the surface condition being presented.
