## What Makes a Scheduled Monument Different from a Listed Building

Scheduling and listing are distinct designations with separate legislative bases and entirely separate consenting regimes. Listed buildings are controlled under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990, with consent processed through the local planning authority. Scheduled monuments are protected under the Ancient Monuments and Archaeological Areas Act 1979, and consent for any works rests with the Secretary of State, exercised in practice through Historic England in England, Cadw in Wales, and Historic Environment Scotland in Scotland.

The practical complication is that a structure can carry both designations simultaneously. A medieval gatehouse, an industrial engine house, or a post-medieval bridge may be both a listed building and a scheduled monument. In such cases the consenting requirements are cumulative: an application that satisfies the local planning authority does not discharge the obligation to obtain Scheduled Monument Consent (SMC). Assuming that listed building consent is sufficient is a common error, and it is one that carries criminal liability.

## What Works Require Scheduled Monument Consent

The Ancient Monuments and Archaeological Areas Act 1979 prohibits any works that would have the effect of demolishing, destroying, damaging, removing, repairing, altering, or adding to a scheduled monument without prior written consent. The breadth of this is deliberate. Cleaning, surface treatment, consolidation, and biological growth control all fall within scope if they physically affect the fabric of the monument.

Interventions that are frequently treated as low-risk maintenance but that typically require SMC include:

- Chemical biocide application for lichen or algae control
- High-pressure water or steam cleaning
- Laser cleaning of stone, brick, or metal surfaces
- Repointing or mortar consolidation
- Installation of monitoring or protective equipment

The threshold is set low by design. If there is any question about whether a proposed activity requires consent, the consenting body should be contacted before the work is specified, not after.

### What Does Not Require Consent

Some routine activities fall outside the consent regime. Vegetation clearance that does not disturb ground surfaces is generally considered permitted, as is like-for-like maintenance within tightly defined categories. The consenting bodies publish current guidance on permitted activities, but these categories are narrow. Do not assume that a type of work is permitted without checking with the relevant statutory body.

## The Scheduled Monument Consent Application Process

Applications for SMC in England are submitted to Historic England. There is no application fee. The applicant must be, or must act with the authority of, the owner of the monument. In practice, an appointed conservation architect, structural engineer, or specialist contractor may prepare the submission, provided the owner's consent is documented.

The application requires a description of the monument and its scheduling, a full description of the proposed works, a method statement, supporting photographs, drawings, and a site plan. Processing times vary with the complexity and sensitivity of the site, but applicants should allow a minimum of eight weeks and should not build a programme around a shorter lead time. Works undertaken without consent are a criminal offence under the 1979 Act regardless of whether damage results.

### Pre-Application Dialogue

Historic England regional teams will discuss proposed works before a formal application is submitted. This pre-application stage is not a formality: it is the most reliable way to identify concerns early and to shape a method statement around what the consenting body is likely to accept. Inspectors who have been consulted informally are better placed to process the application efficiently. Those who encounter unfamiliar methods or materials for the first time through a formal submission will require more time.

Contact the relevant regional office at the earliest opportunity. In Wales, approach Cadw; in Scotland, Historic Environment Scotland. All three publish regional contact details and pre-application guidance on their websites.

## What a Method Statement Must Cover

The method statement is the central technical document in any SMC application for cleaning or surface treatment. A submission that is too vague to allow the consenting body to assess risk is the single most common cause of delay or refusal. The following areas should be addressed clearly and specifically.

**Condition survey.** A written and photographic record of the monument's current condition, including existing damage, areas of active biological growth, and any zones of particular fragility. This establishes the baseline against which the outcome of works will be assessed and forms part of the archive record.

**Proposed method and parameters.** The specific process to be used, described in enough detail for a specialist to assess its risks. For laser cleaning, this means wavelength, pulse duration, repetition rate, energy density, and spot size. For chemical treatments, it means the product name, concentration, method of application, dwell time, and neutralisation procedure. Generic descriptions are insufficient.

**Conservation principles.** The method statement should demonstrate that the proposed approach is consistent with the principle of minimum intervention: achieving the required outcome without removing or disturbing more than is strictly necessary. Reversibility is a secondary but relevant consideration, and its applicability should be addressed honestly rather than asserted without qualification.

**Risk assessment and control measures.** A structured assessment of risks to the monument's fabric, any associated archaeology, and the surrounding setting, together with the specific measures that will be taken to manage each risk.

**Operatives and supervision.** The qualifications and documented experience of those carrying out the works, and the supervision arrangements in place. Works to scheduled monuments should not be carried out by operatives without relevant demonstrable competence.

### Why the Choice of Cleaning Method Is Examined Closely

Cleaning is not inherently among the most invasive interventions on a scheduled monument, but method selection carries long-term consequences that conservation bodies will examine. Abrasive approaches erode surface detail and can destroy archaeological evidence held within surface deposits. Certain chemical treatments penetrate porous masonry and trigger salt crystallisation damage over subsequent seasons. These are documented risks, and inspectors are familiar with them.

Laser cleaning, when correctly specified and applied by trained operatives, provides a degree of control that other methods cannot offer. Parameters can be adjusted for substrate type and surface condition during the works; the process introduces no liquids that could carry contaminants into masonry; and the operator receives direct visual feedback on the result in real time. These are characteristics relevant to the consenting body's risk assessment and should be addressed explicitly in the method statement.

## Co-ordinating Consents and the Programme

Where a monument carries both listed building and scheduled monument status, both consent streams must be identified and pursued in parallel from the outset. Conservation area notification requirements may add a further layer. A specification that identifies all applicable consents at the brief stage avoids the programme disruption that results from discovering a missing consent after procurement.

SMC timescales do not compress for convenience. Integrating the consenting process into the project programme from the initial stages, rather than treating it as a late-stage administrative step, is the practical discipline that separates projects that proceed smoothly from those that do not.
