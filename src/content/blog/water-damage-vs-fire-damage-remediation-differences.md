Fire damage and water damage often arrive together. Sprinkler systems activate; fire brigade hoses do their work. By the time a loss adjuster walks a site, both types of damage are present and the temptation is to treat the remediation as a single interchangeable task.

It is not. The two damage types require different interventions, in a specific sequence, and conflating them leads to rework, hidden defects, and inflated final reinstatement costs.

## What Does Fire Damage Actually Leave Behind?

Fire leaves an active contamination problem. The primary residues are:

**Soot and char** — carbonaceous particles that bond to masonry, timber, steel, and finishes. Soot is acidic and hygroscopic: it absorbs moisture and continues to degrade surfaces if left in place. On porous substrates such as brick or stone, soot can migrate inward within hours of the fire being extinguished.

**Smoke deposits and tars** — heavier hydrocarbons that condense on cooler surfaces, often at a distance from the fire seat. These compounds are harder to shift than loose soot and cause persistent staining if not addressed promptly.

**Acid gases** — combustion of plastics, treated timbers, and furnishings releases hydrogen chloride, sulphur dioxide, and similar gases. These dissolve into surface moisture and attack metals and masonry. Structural steel that appears intact can develop corrosion over months if the acidic residue is not removed and neutralised.

**Odour compounds** — volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that penetrate porous substrates and continue off-gassing long after visible contamination is removed.

The common thread: fire damage must be actively removed. Drying, ventilating, or painting over it does not resolve the contamination. It conceals it.

## What Does Water Damage Actually Leave Behind?

Water damage is fundamentally a moisture management problem. The primary concerns are:

**Elevated moisture content** in masonry, timber, and insulation. Timber above approximately 20% moisture content by weight is at risk of fungal decay; masonry with sustained saturation develops spalling and salt crystallisation damage over time.

**Salt efflorescence** — soluble salts carried to the surface as water evaporates. In heritage masonry this can cause surface delamination and may obscure the true extent of damage beneath.

**Mould risk** — in enclosed spaces where relative humidity remains above roughly 70% for more than 48 hours, mould colonisation becomes a realistic outcome. This has implications for both structural materials and indoor air quality.

**Secondary damage from drying** — rapid forced drying of masonry can cause thermal shock cracking. Timber that dries too quickly will split and warp. The drying process must be managed, not simply initiated and left.

The common thread: water damage must be stabilised and dried before any surface treatment is applied. Applying coatings, consolidants, or surface cleaning methods to a substrate that is still saturated produces poor outcomes — adhesion fails, moisture is trapped, and the defect is deferred rather than resolved.

## Why Does the Sequence of Work Matter?

On a fire-damaged site that has also suffered water ingress, the sequencing question is genuine: which do you address first?

Structural drying should begin immediately — water damage creates an active biological risk within 48 hours and cannot wait. Surface remediation, cleaning soot and smoke deposits from substrates, should wait until moisture content is below the threshold appropriate for the cleaning method being used.

The problem arises when programme pressure causes contractors to begin surface remediation before drying is complete, or causes loss adjusters to sign off on scope that combines the two phases without adequate stage controls.

### What Goes Wrong When the Order Is Reversed?

Several failure modes are well established:

**Coating over soot before removal** — if a primer or stabilising coat is applied over soot deposits to accelerate the programme, the acidic compounds continue attacking the substrate beneath. The coating will eventually fail and the underlying damage will be worse than it would have been had the soot been properly removed first. This is a common reinstatement defect that only becomes apparent months or years later, often after practical completion.

**Redecorating before structural drying completes** — coatings applied to wet masonry trap residual moisture. This causes blistering, delamination, and in the worst cases accelerates the salt crystallisation that structural drying was intended to prevent. In heritage buildings, trapped moisture can cause irreversible surface loss on lime renders and decorative stonework.

## How Does Laser Cleaning Fit Into Fire Damage Remediation?

Laser cleaning is a surface contamination removal method. In fire damage remediation, it removes soot, char, tar deposits, and smoke staining from building surfaces. The approach has several practical advantages over abrasive and chemical alternatives in this setting:

**No additional moisture** — the process is dry. Abrasive wet blasting or chemical washing introduces further water to a substrate that has already been saturated. Laser ablation does not, which simplifies moisture management during the remediation programme.

**Selective removal** — the laser removes carbonaceous contamination without eroding the substrate beneath. On carved stonework, decorative plasterwork, heritage brickwork, or ornamental metalwork, this precision matters. Abrasive methods typically cannot discriminate between the deposit and the underlying material at the level of detail that heritage and insurance specifications require.

**Suitable for porous substrates** — on brick and stone, laser ablation removes soot from the surface layer without driving it further into the pore structure. Some pressure-based methods carry this risk on open-grained materials.

**Documented output** — the before/after record from laser cleaning is clear, because the method removes contamination layer by layer in a controlled pass sequence. This supports scope closure and claim substantiation.

Laser cleaning does not neutralise acid gas attack, treat mould, dry saturated substrates, or address structural damage. It is one element within a fire damage remediation sequence. Its position in that sequence — after structural stabilisation and drying, before redecoration — should be explicitly defined in the specification.

## What Should a Specification Include?

A well-structured fire damage specification distinguishes between the moisture stabilisation phase and the contamination removal phase. It should define:

- Moisture content targets to be achieved before surface treatment begins, with named measurement method and frequency
- The cleaning method selected for each surface type, with a brief rationale for why that method is appropriate
- How acid gas attack on ferrous and non-ferrous metals will be assessed and treated
- Whether odour treatment or specialist air purification is required, and at which stage
- Sign-off criteria for each phase before the next begins

Where laser cleaning forms part of the scope, the specification should define the substrate types, the extent of contamination to be removed, and the intended fluence range. Class 4 laser equipment carries operator safety obligations under BS EN 60825-1; these should be addressed in method statements, not left to on-site discretion.
