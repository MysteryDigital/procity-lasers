Graffiti on a war memorial creates a specific kind of pressure that other removal jobs do not. The community wants it gone immediately. Local councillors are fielding calls. News cameras may be on site by the following morning. That urgency is understandable, but it creates conditions in which contractors and commissioning bodies can make decisions that cause lasting damage to protected structures — sometimes greater than the graffiti itself.

This article covers the heritage constraints that apply to war memorials, the technical considerations that govern removal, and why a documented method statement is not bureaucratic overhead but a genuine protection for every party involved.

## Why are war memorials different from other graffiti jobs?

Most war memorials are listed structures, and a significant number are Grade II* or Grade I. Even memorials that sit below the listed building threshold are frequently in conservation areas or form part of a scheduled monument. That legal status changes what you are permitted to do, and to what standard of care you are obliged to work.

The fabric of the memorial itself presents additional complications. Portland stone, limestone, and granite — the most common materials — have different surface characteristics and porosities. Many memorials have stood for over a hundred years and have accumulated biological growth, sulphation crusts, or previous cleaning residues that alter how the surface responds to treatment. An assessment adequate for a brick wall is not adequate here.

Location compounds everything. War memorials are overwhelmingly in exposed public settings — town squares, churchyards, roadsides — so wind, moisture, and public access during works are live variables that need managing.

## What consent is required before work begins?

If the memorial is a listed building, works that affect its character — including cleaning — may require Listed Building Consent from the local planning authority. Historic England guidance makes clear that even apparently benign interventions can be caught by this requirement. In Wales, the equivalent process runs through Cadw.

Commissioning bodies should not assume that urgency places the work outside the consent regime. Contact the local conservation officer before any treatment begins. Where consent is needed, the application will typically require a method statement — another reason to have one prepared before mobilising.

Scheduled monument consent may also apply. The penalties for proceeding without the correct permissions are serious and can fall on the commissioning body as well as the contractor.

## Why does urgency work against you technically?

The pressure to act within hours of discovering graffiti is real and politically understandable. But most removal errors are made when that pressure overrides the assessment process.

Spray paint on porous stone does not behave like spray paint on a painted wall. The longer it remains, the deeper carrier solvents may migrate into the stone matrix — but the treatment chosen to address it can cause equal or greater damage if it is wrong for the substrate. Applying a chemical solvent without first testing on an inconspicuous area risks bleaching or staining the stone permanently. High-volume pressure washing removes surface detail that has taken a century to acquire.

A rapid visual assessment combined with photographic documentation of existing condition takes a modest amount of time and provides a baseline against which the outcome can be measured. That record also supports due diligence if the treatment is later questioned.

## Why are abrasive methods unsuitable for memorial stonework?

Grit blasting and wet abrasive blasting remain widely used on industrial and commercial substrates. On memorial stonework they are generally inappropriate.

Evidence from building conservation practice confirms that even low-pressure abrasive blasting damages masonry surfaces, rounding carved lettering, opening pore structure, and accelerating future soiling. On a listed structure, that surface loss is not an acceptable trade-off for speed. Historic England and most conservation officers will not approve abrasive treatments on listed memorials.

Chemical methods carry their own risks on porous stone. If residues are not fully neutralised after application, they remain in the stone and continue to cause damage long after the visible graffiti has gone.

## How does laser cleaning perform on memorial stonework?

Laser cleaning uses pulsed laser energy to ablate the contaminant from the substrate surface. The key principle is differential absorption: spray paint absorbs the laser energy far more strongly than the stone beneath it. When the pulse is calibrated correctly, the paint layer reaches the ablation threshold before the substrate does, so the contaminant is removed without significant energy transfer to the stone.

The process uses a Q-switched Nd:YAG laser at 1.064 µm wavelength with pulse durations in the nanosecond range. There are no abrasive media, no solvents, and no water pressure involved. The operator calibrates fluence — energy delivered per unit area — against the substrate, beginning with a test patch to establish appropriate settings before working across the affected area.

Operator skill and the test patch protocol are not optional steps. Wrong settings can still cause substrate damage, which is why the selection of an experienced operator matters as much as the selection of the method.

## What should a method statement include?

A method statement serves two purposes: it demonstrates that the contractor has assessed the risks and selected an appropriate approach, and it gives the commissioning body a document that establishes due diligence if the work is later questioned.

A thorough method statement should cover: the listed or scheduled status of the structure; a substrate assessment including material type, condition, and any previous treatments; photographic documentation of the pre-treatment condition; the proposed removal method with equipment specifications and fluence parameters; a test patch protocol with defined pass/fail criteria; a COSHH assessment; laser safety compliance details; post-treatment inspection criteria; and a procedure for reporting incidental damage discovered during work.

Where Listed Building Consent is required, the conservation officer may want to review the method statement before work commences. Having it prepared in advance prevents the consent process from becoming a bottleneck at an already pressured moment.

## Does COSHH apply to laser cleaning?

Yes. Laser ablation of spray paint generates a fume stream containing combustion products from the paint binder and pigments. The composition of that fume depends on the paint formulation, which is rarely known in advance on a vandalism job. A COSHH assessment is required, and control measures — typically local exhaust ventilation or respiratory protection appropriate to the identified hazards — must be in place before work begins.

Laser operators also work with Class 4 devices, which require compliance with BS EN 60825-1. That means defined exclusion zones and laser-rated eye protection matched to the specific wavelength in use. Any contractor working on a memorial in a public setting needs a documented plan for managing public exclusion from the hazard area during operations. Contractors who present both a COSHH assessment and a laser safety plan as part of their method statement are demonstrating competence, not adding unnecessary process.
