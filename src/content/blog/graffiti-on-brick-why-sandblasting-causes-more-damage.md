## Why Contractors Still Reach for Abrasive Methods

Sandblasting and its variants, wet abrasive blasting and dry grit blasting, have been used for surface cleaning since the late nineteenth century. They are familiar, widely available, and the equipment is not expensive to hire. For some substrates, particularly robust engineering metals or dense concrete, they remain appropriate. The problem is that brick and natural stone are not those substrates, and graffiti removal work on masonry still sees abrasive methods specified or substituted in more often than the condition of the finished surface warrants.

Part of the reason is visibility. Graffiti on a brick wall presents as a surface problem, so the instinct is to apply a surface solution at force. Sandblasting looks decisive: the paint comes off quickly, the job appears done, and the wall dries to an apparently clean face. The damage it causes, however, does not announce itself immediately. It accumulates, becomes apparent weeks or months later, and by then the contractor responsible is usually long gone.

This article is written for specifiers, procurement officers, and site managers who want to understand what actually happens to masonry under abrasive pressure, and why the method statement for graffiti on brick needs to be more carefully considered than a like-for-like substitution of cleaning methods.

## What Abrasive Pressure Actually Does to Brick and Mortar

### The Mechanics of Surface Erosion

Brick is a fired-clay material, and its outer surface is not uniform throughout its depth. The fired surface skin, sometimes called the fireskin or fire skin, is the hardest and most weather-resistant layer of the brick. It forms during kiln firing and provides the primary barrier against moisture ingress, frost damage, and atmospheric pollution. Once that layer is abraded away, the more porous, softer material beneath is permanently exposed.

Sandblasting removes the fireskin. Even at pressures adjusted downward to limit visible damage, abrasive media is indiscriminate: it acts on the paint or coating it is directed at, and on the material underneath it, simultaneously. The grit cannot distinguish between the graffiti and the substrate. At the velocities required to mobilise and project the media, some surface erosion of the underlying brick is effectively unavoidable.

The result, once weathering cycles accumulate, is accelerated deterioration: moisture penetrates further, freeze-thaw cycles act on a greater volume of water held within the brick, and spalling begins. A wall that looked clean after sandblasting may begin to show face erosion and fretting within a few years. Repair at that point involves selective brick replacement and repointing, work that is substantially more expensive than the original cleaning contract.

### Mortar Joints: The More Immediate Problem

If the brick face is the longer-term casualty, the mortar joint is the immediate one. Mortar, particularly traditional lime mortar found in pre-1920 buildings, is softer than brick by design. That differential hardness is intentional: lime mortar is sacrificial, allowing the masonry to accommodate thermal movement and preventing stress from concentrating in the bricks themselves. When it needs replacing, it can be raked out and repointed without damaging the surrounding masonry.

Abrasive blasting inverts this logic. The grit erodes the mortar joint faster and more severely than the brick face, undercutting the joint edges, widening the profile, and reducing the joint depth. What had been a tight, weather-resistant joint becomes a shallow, irregular channel. Water tracks along it. If the original mortar was hydraulic lime, it is often irreplaceable with a matching mix without specialist material sourcing and skilled application. Repointing with an incompatible modern Portland cement mortar, which is harder and less permeable than the surrounding brick, creates a new set of problems: differential movement concentrates stress in the brick rather than the joint, and the harder mortar face sheds water onto the brick rather than allowing it to evaporate.

## Compliance Considerations for Listed and Conservation Area Buildings

For assets within the planning system, the method used to clean graffiti is not purely an operational decision. The Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990 imposes duties of care on those managing listed structures, and any treatment that causes material change to the fabric of a listed building can constitute unauthorised works. Local planning authorities and Historic England both take a position on abrasive cleaning of historic masonry: the general guidance strongly discourages it for precisely the reasons set out above.

Conservation area designation provides a lower level of statutory protection than listing, but permitted development rights are more restricted and local policies often require that cleaning methods preserve the character and fabric of the area. A method statement that proposes sandblasting on a Victorian brick terrace within a conservation area should expect scrutiny from the local authority conservation officer.

Beyond planning, the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 are relevant to any abrasive cleaning operation involving silica-based media. Crystalline silica dust generated during blasting is a known respiratory hazard, and contractors using silica sand as blasting media are subject to specific exposure controls. Switching to alternative abrasives reduces but does not eliminate the dust exposure risk, and operatives and bystanders require appropriate controls regardless.

## Why Selective Removal Methods Perform Better on Masonry

The defining characteristic of graffiti on brick is that two materials with very different physical properties are sitting in close contact: the paint or ink applied by the vandal, and the ceramic or lime substrate beneath it. An effective removal method needs to act on one without damaging the other. Abrasive blasting cannot achieve this because its mode of action is purely mechanical and does not distinguish between materials.

Laser cleaning operates differently. A pulsed laser beam delivers energy at a wavelength and pulse duration selected for the target material. When directed at a painted surface on brick, the energy is absorbed by the paint layer, which ablates (converts rapidly from solid to vapour or particulate) without the underlying brick reaching temperatures that would cause thermal stress. The laser can be tuned to the specific absorption characteristics of the coating type and adjusted in real time as the operator works. The substrate is left intact.

This selectivity is why laser cleaning has become the preferred specification for graffiti removal on listed buildings, conservation area masonry, and heritage stonework, and why it is increasingly specified on standard commercial buildings where owners want the masonry preserved in serviceable condition for future maintenance cycles rather than eroded prematurely.

The method is not universally applicable. Very thick or heavily layered coatings, metallic spray paints with unusual optical properties, and substrates with existing damage or instability may require a combined or preparatory approach. A site assessment establishes what is present before any specification is written.

## Asking the Right Questions Before Work Begins

For those procuring graffiti removal rather than carrying it out, the practical questions to raise with any contractor are as follows. What media or method will be used, and why is it appropriate for this substrate? Has the brick type and mortar mix been assessed? Is a test panel proposed before full treatment? If the building is listed or within a conservation area, has the method been checked against local authority guidance?

A contractor unable to answer these questions confidently, or who proposes sandblasting on brick without caveat, should be pressed for more detail before the contract is awarded. The cost of remedying abrasion damage to historic or traditional masonry routinely exceeds the cost of the original cleaning work.
