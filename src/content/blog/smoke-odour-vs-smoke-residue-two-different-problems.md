After a fire, the instinct is to focus on what you can see: blackened walls, soot-stained ceilings, charred material that needs stripping out. But remediation teams and loss adjusters regularly encounter a problem that survives a thorough visual clean — the smell comes back.

That is not a failure of cleaning effort. It is a failure to understand that soot on surfaces and smoke odour in a building are two fundamentally different problems, with different causes, different treatment methods, and different indicators of success. Treating them as a single scope risks missing the second problem entirely.

## What Is Smoke Residue?

Smoke residue is physical matter deposited on surfaces during combustion. It includes soot (fine carbon particles), ash, partially combusted organic material, and chemical by-products that vary depending on what burned. On a wall or ceiling it appears as a dark, oily film. On porous substrates — brickwork, timber, plaster — it penetrates the surface to some depth.

Residue is a contamination problem. It can be seen, measured, and physically removed. The challenge is substrate sensitivity: the cleaning method that works on glazed tile will damage historic stonework or leave solvent residue on timber. Choosing the wrong approach may strip the surface while leaving contamination behind, or damage the substrate in the process.

Laser cleaning is well suited to smoke residue on masonry and stone. The pulsed laser preferentially ablates the carbon-rich soot layer, which absorbs significantly more energy than the underlying substrate. On porous surfaces, this avoids driving contamination deeper — a known hazard with high-pressure washing. The process produces no abrasive media and no liquid waste stream, which simplifies site management and protects adjacent materials.

## What Is Smoke Odour?

Smoke odour is a chemistry problem, not a surface problem. During combustion, volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are produced and released into the air. Some settle on surfaces as part of the residue layer. Many more penetrate deeply into porous materials — timber, plaster, soft furnishings, cavity walls — where they continue to off-gas long after the visible fire damage has been addressed.

The compounds responsible include aldehydes, phenols, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), among other organic molecules. Their volatility varies: some dissipate relatively quickly in ventilated conditions; others remain adsorbed to materials for months or years.

This is why a building can look visually clean and still smell of smoke. The surfaces have been treated, but the odour source is not at the surface — it is within the material matrix or in spaces that cleaning did not reach.

## Why Does Odour Persist After Surfaces Look Clean?

Several mechanisms explain persistent odour following a visual clean.

**Surface residue was removed but VOCs within the substrate were not.** Wiping a wall removes the soot film but does not extract the VOCs that have migrated into plaster or blockwork.

**HVAC and cavity spaces were not treated.** Smoke travels through ductwork, ceiling voids, and wall cavities. Without treatment of these spaces, they act as ongoing odour reservoirs.

**Secondary off-gassing from building materials.** Some materials — particularly MDF, composite timber products, and synthetic floor coverings — can release combustion by-products when heated or disturbed. These may not be obvious during the initial inspection.

**Humidity changes.** VOCs adsorbed to porous materials can be released when humidity rises, which is why a building that seemed acceptable when dry may smell strongly after rain or once occupation begins.

## What Does Residue Remediation Require?

The scope for surface residue remediation should address:

- Identification of substrate types and their sensitivity to cleaning methods
- Selection of the appropriate technique per substrate (laser, chemical, dry-ice, mechanical, or a combination)
- Removal of irreparably contaminated material such as charred timber or saturated plaster
- Air quality monitoring during cleaning to manage fume and particulate exposure — COSHH obligations apply to the fume stream generated by any ablation process
- Validation through visual inspection and surface swabbing before sign-off

On heritage buildings or structures with mixed substrate types, this becomes a specialism in its own right. A conservation officer or structural surveyor should be involved in defining acceptable standards before work commences, particularly where listed building consent conditions apply.

## What Does Odour Remediation Require?

Odour remediation is a separate discipline. Typical approaches include:

**Thermal fogging** — applying a deodorising agent as a fine mist that follows the same pathways smoke took, reaching cavities and porous surfaces that a surface clean cannot address.

**Ozone treatment** — ozone generators oxidise VOC molecules, breaking down the chemical compounds responsible for odour. This requires the building to be vacated and sealed during treatment, and residual ozone must be fully cleared before re-occupation.

**Hydroxyl generation** — an alternative to ozone that can operate in occupied or partially occupied buildings, though treatment times are typically longer.

**Encapsulation** — sealing porous surfaces with a specialist primer that locks in residual VOCs and prevents further off-gassing, used where replacement of affected material is not practical or economic.

No single method works universally. The appropriate approach depends on the materials present, the severity of contamination, the timeline for re-occupation, and budget. A well-drafted scope of works will specify both the method and the test protocol for sign-off — typically an odour panel assessment or air quality sampling for specific VOC species.

## How Do the Two Interact in a Scope of Works?

Residue and odour remediation must be sequenced, not just listed. Cleaning the surfaces first is correct — it removes the primary source of surface-layer VOC off-gassing and allows an accurate assessment of what odour remains beneath. But the surface clean should not be treated as the final step in an odour strategy.

Loss adjusters and contractors who scope the two together — or assume that a visual clean resolves both — frequently encounter callbacks when the smell returns after handover. By that point, the building may have been redecorated, making further investigation and treatment significantly more disruptive and expensive.

The better approach is to specify each problem with its own success criterion from the outset. For surface residue: what does clean look like, and who validates it? For odour: what is the acceptable VOC concentration or odour threshold at sign-off, and how is it measured?

Getting both answers into the scope of works before any cleaning begins avoids the dispute about responsibility when one party has cleaned everything visible and the other can still smell smoke.
