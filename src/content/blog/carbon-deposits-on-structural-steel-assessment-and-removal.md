## Why Full Carbon Removal Cannot Be Deferred

When a fire affects a steel-framed structure, the most visible consequence is not always the most urgent. Carbon deposits and soot accumulation on structural steelwork may appear to be a cosmetic issue, but they represent a substantive obstacle to the assessments that must precede any reinstatement decision.

Structural engineers and loss adjusters assessing post-fire steel cannot give reliable opinions on heat-affected zones, surface integrity, or the condition of protective coatings while those surfaces remain contaminated. Carbon residues mask the base metal, obscure discolouration that indicates temperature exceedance, and trap moisture that accelerates corrosion. An assessment conducted through a layer of soot is, at best, qualified; at worst, it defers the discovery of material damage to a later stage in the reinstatement programme, with corresponding cost and programme implications.

Removal is a prerequisite, not a finishing step.

## What Fire Deposits on Structural Steel

### Carbon, Soot, and Combustion Residues

Post-fire contamination on steelwork is rarely simple carbon. Depending on the materials involved in the fire, surface deposits can include combusted polymers, accelerant residues, partially oxidised organic compounds, and airborne particulates that have settled and bonded under heat. Soot is heterogeneous in character: fine carbon particles suspended in a matrix of tars and acidic compounds that, once cooled, adhere tenaciously to steel surfaces.

The acidity of soot deposits is relevant beyond surface chemistry. In the presence of moisture, acidic residues initiate corrosion processes on bare or damaged steel surfaces quickly. Where protective coatings have been compromised by heat, the underlying metal is immediately vulnerable. The longer contamination remains in place, the greater the risk of active corrosion developing before assessment can occur.

### Heat-Affected Zones and the Assessment Problem

Heat-affected zones on steel are identified partly by surface discolouration: colour changes in the metal or in residual paint indicate temperature ranges experienced during the fire. The presence of carbon deposits obscures this evidence. Structural engineers typically require clean metal surfaces to assess discolouration reliably, particularly when deciding whether steel members have exceeded the temperature thresholds relevant to their load-bearing capacity.

This is the core reason why carbon removal is a prerequisite for structural inspection rather than a post-assessment task.

## Assessing the Extent of Carbon Contamination

Before any removal method is specified, the extent and character of the contamination must be understood. Key variables include:

- The geometry and accessibility of the steel sections affected
- Whether the contamination is surface soot only, or includes bonded carbon with tar or polymer residues
- The condition of the existing protective coating or intumescent paint beneath the deposits
- The access constraints of the structure (height, confined spaces, proximity to fire-damaged materials still in place)

Where intumescent coatings are present, the assessment must determine whether those coatings remain serviceable, damaged, or have been consumed. This affects both the removal specification and the reinstatement scope, as intumescent coatings are typically a controlled application requiring manufacturer approval and certification.

A method statement addressing removal should be agreed with the structural engineer and the appointed loss adjuster before work commences. Under the Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015, the principal contractor carries responsibility for ensuring that the method of work is appropriate to the hazards present, including those arising from post-fire contamination.

## Removal Options

### Abrasive Blasting

Abrasive blasting, including dry grit blasting and wet slurry methods, is the conventional approach for large-scale industrial carbon removal. It achieves effective cleaning on accessible steelwork and can be specified to various surface preparation standards (Sa grades under BS EN ISO 8501-1).

The primary limitation in fire-damaged structures is control. Abrasive blasting removes carbon and any residual coating beneath it without discrimination. Where the objective is to assess whether an intumescent coating survives and can be retained, blasting removes the information before it can be evaluated. In confined or complex geometries, such as between flange faces, at bolted connections, or in shallow void spaces, blast access is restricted and cleaning may be incomplete. Spent abrasive and removed material must be managed under Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH), as post-fire soot can contain hazardous combustion products.

Abrasive blasting also generates significant dust and debris, which may not be acceptable in partially occupied structures or where the fire has affected areas adjacent to salvageable finishes or sensitive materials.

### Chemical Stripping

Chemical cleaning agents can dissolve carbon and soot residues on steel surfaces without abrasion. They are useful where surface preservation is a priority and where abrasive access is restricted. Application is typically by dwell and agitation, requiring scaffolded or mechanised access for overhead and vertical surfaces.

The practical limitation is that chemical methods are slower than abrasive or laser approaches, require containment for run-off and residues, and their effectiveness varies with the character of the contamination. Bonded carbon-tar deposits are more resistant than loose soot. COSHH assessments are required for both the cleaning agents themselves and for the contaminated waste they generate.

### Laser Cleaning

Laser ablation removes surface contamination by directing a concentrated beam of pulsed laser energy at the substrate. The contaminant absorbs the energy and is vaporised or ejected from the surface; the underlying material, with a different absorption coefficient, is unaffected. The process is dry, non-contact, and generates no secondary waste stream beyond airborne particulates extracted at source.

The characteristics that make laser cleaning relevant to post-fire steel are control and selectivity. Beam parameters can be adjusted to remove carbon deposits without disturbing surviving coatings beneath them, allowing the engineer to assess coating condition after cleaning rather than having the decision made by the removal method itself. The process is also appropriate for complex geometries, including connection details, stiffener plates, and constrained sections where abrasive access is impractical.

### Access Considerations Across Methods

All methods require a considered access strategy. Under the Work at Height Regulations 2005, any method involving elevated working must be subject to a hierarchy-of-control assessment, preferring working from the ground or existing structure where practicable.

Laser equipment is transportable and can be deployed from scissor lifts, mobile elevated platforms, or temporary scaffolding. Because the process is dry and generates no secondary debris, it simplifies protection of adjacent areas and reduces the containment burden compared to blasting. For structures where fire damage is localised and the remainder of the building is partially accessible, this containment advantage can be significant in practice.

## Implications for Specification and Programme

The choice of removal method should be made in consultation with the structural engineer and, where coatings are involved, with the coating manufacturer. The selected method will affect what can be assessed post-cleaning, what reinstatement specification is possible, and the programme sequence.

Where intumescent coating retention is being considered, the removal specification must explicitly protect surviving coating zones. Where full strip-back is required regardless, the method choice is driven by access, containment, and programme constraints.

In either case, a method statement should be agreed and approved before mobilisation. This document forms part of the project record and supports any subsequent insurance claim or reinstatement certification.
